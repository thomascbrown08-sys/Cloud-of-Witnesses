Season IV · Study 28 · India, 20th century

The Strange Man from India

Sadhu Sundar Singh and the Gospel Taking Indigenous Root

Sundar SinghIndiaIndigenous Christianity1889–c.1929 AD
Opening

The Boy Who Burned a Bible — and Then Met Its Author

Punjab, 1904. Sundar Singh was fifteen years old, the son of a Sikh landowner, deeply religious, deeply hostile to Christianity. His mother — who had taught him the Hindu scriptures and the Sikh sacred texts from childhood — had recently died, and in his grief he had turned that hostility into action: he bought a New Testament, tore it apart page by page, and burned it in front of his family. He was proud of himself. Three days later, still unable to find peace, he decided to throw himself in front of a train.

He woke before dawn and prayed — demanding, he later said, that God appear to him before morning or he would end his life. He expected nothing. Then the room filled with light. A face. Not the faces of Rama or Krishna or the Sikh Gurus he had expected to see. He heard the question in Hindustani, the language his heart thought in: "Why do you persecute me? How long will you do this? I died for you; I have given my life for you." He recognized the face from the picture he had seen in a Christian school. It was Jesus.

He told his family at breakfast. They gave him poison, but he did not die. His father disinherited him and expelled him from the village. He arrived at a Sikh temple, was refused entry, and slept that night under a tree, feverish from the poison, oddly at peace. He had found what the Sikh scriptures called the shanti — the peace — that he had spent his whole short life searching for. He spent the remaining twenty-five years of his life in the saffron robe of a sadhu, a wandering holy man, carrying the Christian gospel across India, Tibet, Ceylon, and eventually to Europe and North America — not as a Western missionary, but as an Indian, in Indian forms, addressing Indian questions.

The Doctrine

The Gospel and Culture — When the Seed Grows in Its Own Soil

Sundar Singh's significance for the history of mission lies not just in what he did but in what he demonstrated: that the Christian gospel could take root in Indian soil, in Indian forms, without being imported as a Western cultural package. He was not a convert to Western Christianity. He was an Indian Christian — a figure recognizable to his countrymen as standing in the tradition of the wandering sadhu, the holy man who possesses nothing and goes wherever the Spirit leads — who had encountered the living Christ and could not stop talking about him.

He refused to wear Western dress. He declined ordination by Western mission societies on the grounds that the sadhu's role was a more natural form for an Indian Christian witness than the institutional clergy model. He expressed his theology in categories drawn from Indian philosophy and mysticism — not to compromise the gospel, but to make it receivable in forms that Indian minds could engage without first having to learn European theological vocabulary. When he described his experience of Christ, he used the language of the Upanishads alongside the language of Paul. When he spoke of prayer, he drew on the contemplative traditions of his own culture alongside the Psalms.

This approach — sometimes called "inculturation" or "contextual theology" — remains one of the most important and most contested questions in global mission. The question is not whether the gospel is culturally neutral (it is not — it comes in specific words in specific languages) but whether the cultural forms of its original messengers are part of the gospel or merely its packaging. Sundar Singh answered with his life: the packaging can be Indian. The content — Christ crucified and risen — cannot be negotiated.

The Figure

Sadhu Sundar Singh (1889–c. 1929 AD)

Sundar Singh's biography has a strange and unresolved ending: he disappeared in 1929 while attempting to cross into Tibet on one of his regular visits there. He was forty years old. His body was never found. He had always said he expected to die in Tibet — a land that had, on multiple visits, treated him with extraordinary hostility, including imprisonment in a pit full of corpses from which he reported a miraculous escape. Whether the final disappearance was death from exposure, arrest and execution, or something else, no one has ever determined with certainty.

Before his disappearance he had become one of the most famous Christians in the world — a figure both admired and contested, whose theology some found too mystical and whose methods some found too unorthodox. He was not an institution-builder. He left behind no denomination, no seminary, no systematic theology. What he left behind were thousands of people across three continents who had heard, from the lips of an Indian holy man in saffron robes, the story of the Christ who had appeared to him in the light before dawn and asked why he was persecuting him.

Christ in Focus

Sundar Singh's encounter with Christ was, structurally, a Pauline conversion: the hostile persecutor overwhelmed by a vision of the risen Lord, asked "why do you persecute me?" (Acts 9:4 — the same words), transformed from enemy to witness overnight. It is a reminder that Christ's appearances are not confined to the first century, that he continues to make himself known in ways that transcend institutional channels, and that no one is beyond the reach of the one who appeared to the persecutor Saul on the Damascus road.

Sundar Singh had a phrase he returned to repeatedly in his writing and speaking: "I am not a Christian because Christianity is the religion of the West, or because it is the most powerful religion. I am a Christian because Christ gave me the peace and joy which I could not find elsewhere." This is the testimony that no cultural argument can dislodge: not "I have accepted a Western religion" but "I have met a person, and that person is alive, and nothing has been the same since." The peace — the shanti — he had searched for in the Sikh scriptures, he found not in a system but in a face.

Then & Now

Indian Christianity — Complex and Growing

The church in India today is the product of many streams: the ancient Thomas Christians of Kerala (who trace their origin to the Apostle Thomas in the 1st century), the Catholic missions of the Jesuit era, the Protestant missions of the 18th and 19th centuries, and the indigenous movements like the one Sundar Singh represented. India's Christian population is estimated at around 28–30 million — a minority in a nation of 1.4 billion, but a vibrant and growing one, facing significant pressure from Hindu nationalist movements that treat Christianity as a foreign import.

The question Sundar Singh's life posed — whether Christianity can be genuinely Indian rather than a Western cultural import — is still alive and still contested. The emergence of indigenous Indian theologians, Indian Christian art, Indian Christian music, and Indian forms of worship suggests that the answer is yes: the gospel is proving itself capable of deep inculturation in the oldest and most complex civilization on earth. Sundar Singh would not be surprised. He had always known that the peace he had found in Jesus was not a Western peace but a universal one — available to every heart that had grown tired of searching and was willing to ask.

A Quote to Carry
"I am not a Christian because Christianity is the religion of the West. I am a Christian because Christ gave me the peace and joy which I could not find anywhere else."
— Sadhu Sundar Singh
Reflect

Sundar Singh found in Christ what he had been searching for in the sacred texts of his own tradition. Is there someone in your life whose spiritual searching in other places might be, without their knowing it, a search for the same Christ?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for appearing to the boy who burned your book — for the light before dawn, for the face he recognized, for the question in his own language that dissolved his hostility and gave him peace. Thank you that you are not a Western God or an Eastern God but the God of every heart that has ever been restless for the real thing. Give us Sundar Singh's ability to speak of you in the language the people around us actually think in — not our preferred theological vocabulary, but the words that reach the place where the searching is actually happening. Amen.
