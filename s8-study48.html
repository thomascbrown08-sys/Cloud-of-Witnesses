Season VIII · Study 48 · Romania & the World, 20th century

The Voice in the Prison

Richard Wurmbrand — Fourteen Years in Communist Prisons, and the Voice That Would Not Stop

WurmbrandVoice of the MartyrsPersecution1909–2001 AD
Opening

The Day He Stood Up When Everyone Else Sat Down

Bucharest, Romania, February 1948. The new Communist government had called a congress of all religious denominations, and one by one the delegates — priests, pastors, bishops — had risen to pledge their loyalty to the new order, to praise the Communist Party, to thank the state for the freedoms it so generously provided to religious practice. The hall was full. The cameras were present. The pressure was overwhelming. And one by one the representatives of the Romanian church stood up and performed the required ritual of submission.

Richard Wurmbrand was sitting in the hall. His wife Sabina, beside him, said quietly: "They are spitting in the face of Christ. Will you not stand up and wash his face?" Wurmbrand stood up and walked to the microphone. He told the congress that a Christian's duty was to glorify God and Christ alone — not the Communist Party. He sat down. He had signed his arrest warrant in front of the cameras. He knew it. He did it anyway.

He was arrested in 1948 and held in solitary confinement for three years in a cell twelve feet underground, with no light, no human contact, and no sound except the shuffling of guards and the distant cries of other prisoners being tortured. The Communists used drugs, sleep deprivation, sensory deprivation, and systematic psychological torture designed not merely to break him but to destroy his sense of God's presence entirely. They came close. Wurmbrand later wrote that there were periods in the darkness when he was not sure whether God existed. He held on by repeating Scripture and hymns and by composing sermons — hundreds of them — in his head, for the congregation he could not see and might never see again. When he was eventually released and later left Romania, he had retained those sermons. He preached many of them.

The Doctrine

Suffering and the Presence of God

Richard Wurmbrand's theology was not systematic. He was a preacher and a pastor, not an academic theologian, and his writing has the quality of someone speaking from inside an experience rather than analyzing it from the outside. But the theological claim embedded in his story is one of the most important in Christian thought: the presence of God is not dependent on the presence of favorable circumstances.

The Psalms know this. Psalm 22 — "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — is the cry of a man who has not felt God's presence for a very long time, who names the absence with absolute honesty, and who ends the psalm in praise. The lament is not a failure of faith. It is faith in extremis — the kind that does not pretend, that names what is actually happening, and that continues to address itself to God even when God seems silent. Wurmbrand, composing sermons in a lightless cell, was living Psalm 22.

The specific torture the Communists used — designed to produce the feeling of divine abandonment — was theologically sophisticated in a perverse way. They understood that if they could make a believer feel that God had withdrawn, the faith might collapse. What they did not understand was that the presence of God in the Christian tradition is not a feeling. It is a fact — grounded not in emotional experience but in the objective promise: "I will never leave you nor forsake you" (Hebrews 13:5). Wurmbrand held on to the promise when the feeling was gone, which is the truest form of faith the New Testament describes. "We walk by faith, not by sight" (2 Corinthians 5:7).

The Figure

Richard Wurmbrand (1909–2001 AD)

Wurmbrand was born to a Jewish family in Bucharest and converted to Christianity as a young man, largely through the witness of a peasant carpenter named Christain Wolfkes who had prayed for years that God would send him a Jew to convert so that he could repay the debt the church owed to the Jewish people for the gospel. Wurmbrand and his wife Sabina (who survived her own imprisonment in a labor camp) became Lutheran pastors and built an underground network of Christian fellowship and evangelism that reached both Romanians and Soviet occupation soldiers during and after World War II.

He was imprisoned twice by the Communists — the second time for ten years — and was finally ransomed to the West in 1965 for $10,000, paid by Norwegian mission organizations. In 1967 he founded Voice of the Martyrs (originally the Jesus to the Communist World organization), which continues today as one of the primary global organizations supporting persecuted Christians. He testified before the United States Senate in 1966, removing his shirt to show the eighteen deep scars from his torture — a moment that changed American policy awareness about religious persecution behind the Iron Curtain.

His book Tortured for Christ (1967) has sold millions of copies and been translated into dozens of languages. It remains one of the most direct first-person accounts of Christian suffering under Communism ever written — raw, specific, unsparing about both the suffering and the grace that accompanied it.

Christ in Focus

On the cross, Jesus cried out: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — the opening of Psalm 22, which Wurmbrand repeated in his cell. The eternal Son of God, in his human nature, experienced the full weight of divine abandonment: not as a feeling to be managed but as a reality to be endured, as the substitute for all those whose sin had genuinely separated them from God. The desolation Wurmbrand experienced in his underground cell was a shadow of the desolation the Son experienced at Golgotha. And the promise that kept Wurmbrand — "I will never leave you nor forsake you" — is grounded in that cry, because the one who cried it did so as our substitute, so that it would never be finally true of us.

Wurmbrand wrote that he learned to love his torturers — not by suppressing his anger or pretending the wrong was not happening, but by fixing his attention on the Christ who prayed from the cross, "Father, forgive them, for they know not what they do." He did not arrive at this easily or quickly. He arrived at it through years of darkness, through the kind of sustained suffering that burns away every comfortable theology and leaves only what is actually real. What remained real, when everything else was stripped away, was Christ — not as a doctrine but as a presence. The presence the torturers had tried to remove turned out to be the one thing their instruments could not touch.

Then & Now

The Suffering Church — Still Present, Still Witnessing

The Cold War is over. The Iron Curtain fell in 1989–1991. But the persecuted church that Wurmbrand helped the West see has not disappeared — it has relocated. North Korea is currently the most dangerous country on earth for Christians, with an estimated 50,000–70,000 Christians in concentration camps for the crime of faith. China's house church movement operates under increasing government surveillance and restriction. Christians in Nigeria, Sudan, Ethiopia, Egypt, Pakistan, India, and across the Middle East face violence, legal discrimination, and social exclusion at levels ranging from severe to lethal.

Voice of the Martyrs, which Wurmbrand founded, continues to document these cases and support persecuted believers with practical and spiritual resources. The organization is one of the most rigorous trackers of the global persecution data, and its resources are among the most useful for Christians in comfortable societies who want to understand what their family members are enduring elsewhere.

Wurmbrand's challenge to the Western church has not dated: "Dare to stand up when everyone else is sitting down." The pressures are different in form; the structural demand is the same. Every generation of the comfortable church is offered a version of the congress in Bucharest — an invitation to stand up or sit down, to wash the face of Christ or let it be spat on. The cost varies. The choice does not.

A Quote to Carry
"Ultimately the only weapon that overcomes hate is love. The Christian who does not succeed in converting his enemy to Christ has nonetheless won a spiritual battle if he loves him."
— Richard Wurmbrand, Tortured for Christ, 1967
Reflect

Wurmbrand held on to God's promises when the feeling of God's presence was completely gone. Is your faith grounded in the objective promises of God, or primarily in the feeling of his presence? What would remain if the feeling were taken away?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for Richard Wurmbrand — for the man who stood up when everyone else sat down, who held on through three years of darkness and solitary confinement by repeating your promises when he could not feel your presence. Thank you that your promise — "I will never leave you nor forsake you" — is not a feeling but a fact. Strengthen our brothers and sisters who are in their own cells right now, in North Korea, in China, in Nigeria, in Pakistan and Sudan and Egypt. Let them know you are there. Let your promises hold them when nothing else does. And give us here, in our comfort, their courage to say your name when it costs something. Amen.
