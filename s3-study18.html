The City on a Lake

John Calvin and the Vision of a Community Shaped by Grace

CalvinGenevaInstitutes1509–1564 AD
Opening

The Man Who Never Meant to Stay

Geneva, 1536. John Calvin was twenty-six years old, a French lawyer turned theologian who had just published the first edition of his Institutes of the Christian Religion — a systematic account of Reformed theology that he had written, astonishingly, at the age of twenty-five. He was passing through Geneva on his way to Strasbourg, where he intended to spend his life as a scholar, writing and studying in quiet. He had not planned to stop. He stopped only because the roads through the Alps were blocked by war.

In Geneva, he was recognized by Guillaume Farel — a fiery Swiss reformer who had been trying, with limited success, to reform the city's church. Farel confronted Calvin and demanded that he stay and help. Calvin refused. Farel, who had a gift for the dramatic, told Calvin that if he put his own comfort above the need of Geneva, God would curse his studies. Calvin stayed. He later wrote that it felt less like a free choice and more like God grabbing him by the collar.

Geneva became the laboratory for a vision that no one had attempted at this scale before: a city — not just a church, but an entire city — organized around the principles of the Reformation gospel. It attracted Protestant refugees from across Europe. It produced the Geneva Bible (1560), which the Pilgrim Fathers carried to America. It trained pastors who were sent across France, the Dutch Republic, Scotland, and the English-speaking world. John Knox, who spent time in Geneva, called it "the most perfect school of Christ that ever was in the earth since the days of the Apostles." Others found it more than a little severe. Both reactions were accurate.

The Doctrine

What Calvin Actually Taught — and What He Is Usually Blamed For

Calvin is most often associated, in popular imagination, with a single doctrine: predestination. The association is not entirely wrong — Calvin wrote about divine election at length and with great seriousness. But it gets the emphasis exactly backwards. Calvin scholars consistently note that the organizing center of his theology is not predestination but union with Christ: the conviction that the believer is, by faith, so joined to Jesus that everything that is true of Christ becomes true of the believer — his righteousness, his Spirit, his resurrection life, his sonship. Election, for Calvin, is not a cold philosophical doctrine about God sorting humanity into arbitrary categories. It is the assurance that the love of God toward his people precedes and does not depend on anything in them — that their standing before God is as secure as God himself is constant.

The Institutes — which grew through four editions from a short catechism in 1536 to a massive systematic theology by 1559 — addresses the whole range of Christian doctrine with a logical rigor and pastoral warmth that surprised many who expected a cold system. Calvin was, at heart, a preacher: he preached through entire books of the Bible, verse by verse, morning after morning in Geneva, and his written theology was always oriented toward the life of ordinary believers and congregations rather than purely academic questions.

His vision of the church included a genuine accountability structure — elders who could exercise discipline, deacons who cared for the poor, pastors who preached and taught — because he believed that the gospel produces community, and that community needs shape and structure to remain healthy. This included aspects that we rightly find troubling today: the execution of Michael Servetus for heresy in 1553 was something Calvin supported and that belongs honestly in any account of his legacy. He was a man of his time in ways that were genuinely wrong, even as he was ahead of his time in ways that were genuinely prophetic.

The Figure

John Calvin (1509–1564 AD)

Calvin was privately modest to the point of self-effacement — he asked to be buried in an unmarked grave, and the request was honored — but publicly formidable in a way that left almost no one neutral. He was thin, chronically ill for most of his adult life, relentlessly productive, and emotionally reticent in his published writing in ways that have sometimes made him seem cold. His letters, however, reveal a man capable of deep friendship, genuine pastoral tenderness, and a grief at the fractures within the Reformation movement that he never stopped trying to heal.

His influence on the shape of Western civilization is difficult to overstate. Reformed theology — flowing through the Dutch Republic, Scotland, the Huguenots, the English Puritans, and the New England settlers — shaped the constitutional structures, educational systems, and economic practices of the modern West in ways that political and social historians have traced carefully. The idea that rulers are accountable to a higher law, that church and state are distinct though related institutions, that literacy matters because ordinary people need to read Scripture — these ideas, worked out in Geneva, became the seedbed of much that we take for granted.

Christ in Focus

The heart of Calvin's theology can be stated in a single question he returned to constantly: what does it mean to be "in Christ"? Paul uses that phrase or its equivalent over a hundred times in his letters. Calvin took it with full seriousness: the believer is not merely forgiven by Christ from a distance, not merely helped by Christ as an external aid. The believer is united to Christ — joined to him so intimately that his righteousness is the believer's righteousness, his life is their life, his Spirit indwells them, his future is their future.

This union is the source of both justification (the verdict of righteousness pronounced over the believer on the basis of Christ's righteousness) and sanctification (the ongoing transformation of the believer's character by the Spirit of Christ who lives within them). The two are always distinct — justification is a legal declaration, not a process; sanctification is a process, not a one-time verdict — but they are never separated, because they both flow from the same source: the union with Christ that is the gift of saving faith.

Calvin's famous phrase is that "nearly the whole of sacred doctrine consists in" this — the knowledge of God and ourselves, and how we come to God through Christ. The God we come to is not a God encountered first as a threat and approached only after sufficient performance. He is the God who, in Calvin's words, "condescends to us" — who reaches down into our condition in Christ to bring us up into his.

Then & Now

Geneva's Children — and Geneva's Lessons

The Reformed tradition that flows from Calvin — Presbyterian, Dutch Reformed, French Reformed, Continental Reformed, and much of the English Puritan tradition — is one of the most theologically rich streams in the history of Christianity. Its insistence on the sovereignty of God, the authority of Scripture, the structure of the covenant, and the vocation of all believers (not just clergy) to work in the world as an act of worship has shaped cultures, laws, universities, and economies across four centuries and multiple continents.

It has also, at its worst, produced a kind of severity — a tendency to make the Christian life feel like a perpetual audit rather than a life of grateful freedom. Calvin himself, in his best moments, was at pains to prevent this: he insisted that the believer's relationship to God was that of a child to a father, not a subject to a magistrate, and that Christian obedience flows from gratitude rather than fear. Whether his followers have consistently followed him on this point is a question worth asking honestly.

The enduring gift of Geneva is the vision of a community — not just individuals — shaped by the gospel. Not a theocracy, which was Geneva's error. But a community in which Scripture is taken seriously, the poor are cared for, leaders are accountable, and ordinary believers understand that their work in the world is itself a form of worship. That vision is still worth pursuing, and still being pursued, in congregations on every continent.

A Quote to Carry
"We are not our own; therefore, let us not seek what is expedient for us according to the flesh. We are God's; therefore, let us live and die to him."
— John Calvin, Institutes of the Christian Religion, Book III
Reflect

Calvin insisted that union with Christ is the source of both our forgiveness and our transformation — the two always together, never separated. How does that framing differ from thinking of justification and sanctification as two separate tasks?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for the vision Calvin saw: that your people are not merely forgiven individuals but a community remade by your Spirit, called to live together in the light of what Christ has done. Forgive us when we reduce the gospel to a private transaction and forget that it creates a people. Give us something of Calvin's seriousness — without his severity. Give us the joy of those who know they are yours: not our own, not adrift, but held by the One who reached down to claim us. Amen.
