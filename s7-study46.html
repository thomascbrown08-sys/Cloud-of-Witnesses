The Wound That Won't Heal

The Church and Slavery — Sin, Silence, and the Work of Slow Repair

SlaveryDark ChapterRaceRepair
Opening

The Chapter That Is Not Over

We have named the Crusades, the Inquisition, and the prosperity gospel as dark chapters. Each has a definable historical location — the medieval period, the 16th-century Spanish church, the 20th-century American revival scene. This chapter is different. The church's entanglement with slavery, and its aftermath in racial hierarchy, is not safely historical. It produced consequences that are still structurally present in the American church, still felt in communities whose grandparents were enslaved, and still generating controversy about what acknowledgment and repair look like.

We approach this chapter with the same principle that has governed all of them: a family that cannot name its worst chapters cannot be trusted with its best ones. But here the naming requires more precision than the others, because the failure was not uniform. The church's record on slavery is not a simple story of complicity. It includes Wilberforce and the abolitionists, the black church that kept faith alive through generations of oppression, the Quakers who opposed slavery when almost no one else did, and the missionaries who died fighting it. It also includes the systematic theological justification of slavery by major American denominations, the silence of others, the use of Scripture to argue that the enslavement of Africans was divinely sanctioned, and a racial hierarchy that survived emancipation and shaped the American church — and American society — for generations after.

Both things are true. The honest account names both.

What Happened

The Church and Slavery: Three Failures and One Heroic Thread

Failure One: Theological Justification. The American church — particularly in the antebellum South — did not merely tolerate slavery. Large portions of it developed an elaborate theological defense of it. The "curse of Ham" (Genesis 9:20–27), in which Noah curses Canaan, was systematically misread as a divine sanction for the enslavement of Africans — a reading that required ignoring the text's actual content, the identity of who was cursed, and the obvious question of what Noah's drunken curse has to do with the Trans-Atlantic slave trade. Passages commanding slaves to obey their masters (Ephesians 6:5–8, Colossians 3:22) were applied to the chattel slavery of Africans as if they were straightforward endorsements — ignoring the entirely different institution to which they referred in the first-century Roman world and the whole of the New Testament's logic about human dignity and freedom in Christ.

The Southern Baptist Convention — the largest Protestant denomination in America — was founded in 1845 specifically over the question of whether slaveholders could serve as missionaries. Its founding was a defense of slavery. It issued a formal apology for this in 1995. That 150-year gap is part of the record.

Failure Two: Silence and Accommodation. Many churches and church leaders who did not actively defend slavery chose silence — a silence that, in the context of the most morally urgent question of their era, functioned as complicity. The vast majority of American denominations that survived the Civil War did so by avoiding the question, which required treating the enslavement of human beings as a matter on which Christians could simply disagree. That accommodation did not end with emancipation. Racial segregation in American churches — the "most segregated hour in America" description of Sunday morning worship is attributed to Martin Luther King Jr. — persisted through the 20th century and persists in substantial form today.

Failure Three: Post-Emancipation Racial Hierarchy. The end of slavery did not produce racial equality in the American church. Jim Crow laws were enforced in a society whose dominant moral culture was Christian. Lynchings — over 4,000 documented between 1877 and 1950 — often took place in communities with active churches, sometimes with the tacit or explicit approval of church members, occasionally advertised as public events. The theology that had defended slavery was adapted rather than abandoned: if Black people could no longer be enslaved, they could be legally subordinated, economically excluded, and socially controlled. The church that had the theological resources to challenge this — the imago dei, the universal family of God, the baptismal declaration that "there is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free" — mostly chose not to deploy them.

The Heroic Thread. But the record is not unrelieved. The black church — the churches that emerged from and sustained enslaved and later segregated African Americans — represents one of the most extraordinary chapters in the history of Christianity. It kept faith alive through conditions designed to destroy it. It developed a theology of liberation, lament, and hope rooted in Exodus and the Psalms and the prophets that is, in many respects, more biblical than the comfortable Christianity surrounding it. It produced Sojourner Truth and Frederick Douglass and Harriet Tubman and eventually Martin Luther King Jr. It preserved the gospel when the dominant institutions were using the gospel to oppress.

The Quakers were the first organized Christian group to formally oppose slavery — issuing antislavery statements as early as 1688 in Germantown, Pennsylvania, and pressing the question within their community for generations. The abolitionist movement that eventually succeeded was substantially driven by evangelical Christians — Wilberforce in Britain, the Tappan brothers and Charles Finney in America — who grounded their opposition in the same theological convictions the proslavery theologians had the resources to draw on but did not. The failure was not of the theology. It was of the will to apply it.

Voices From the Wound

Those Who Named It and Those Who Lived It

Frederick Douglass (1818–1895), the formerly enslaved abolitionist, made the most searing indictment of the American church's complicity in his 1845 Narrative and throughout his public career. He distinguished carefully between "the slaveholding religion of this land" and "the Christianity of Christ," arguing that the former was not a corruption of the latter but its contradiction — and that the clearest evidence of this was what he had seen in the behavior of the most "religious" slaveholders, who were also among the most cruel. His critique was theological as well as political: he knew the Bible well enough to know that what was being done to him in the name of the Bible was a perversion of it.

Harriet Tubman (c. 1822–1913), who made thirteen missions into slave territory and liberated approximately seventy people via the Underground Railroad, was explicitly motivated by a faith she described in terms of direct divine guidance. "I never ran my train off the track," she reportedly said, "and I never lost a passenger." She prayed through danger and acted on what she believed God told her to do. She is one of the most practically courageous figures in American history, and her faith was not incidental to her courage — it was the engine of it.

The Confessing Church parallel. Bonhoeffer's Barmen Declaration (Season V, Study 32) stated: "We reject the false doctrine, as though the State, over and above its special commission, should and could become the single and totalitarian order of human life." The same principle applied to the American church and slavery: when the state ordered that human beings could be property, the church's only legitimate response was refusal. The churches that found biblical arguments to accommodate that order were doing what the German Christians did for Nazism — accommodating to the political arrangement in ways that compromised the gospel's non-negotiable claims about human dignity. The parallel is not a rhetorical flourish. It is structural.

Christ in Focus

Galatians 3:28: "There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is no male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus." Paul is writing about the social implications of baptismal union with Christ. He is saying that the categories that structure the social hierarchy of the first-century world — ethnicity, legal status, gender — do not define the community of Christ. The Christian community is the one place in the ancient world where those categories are explicitly overridden by a deeper identity: children of God, heirs of the promise, one in Christ.

This text was available to every church that justified slavery. Every pastor who argued from Scripture that Africans were divinely appointed for bondage read past Galatians 3:28, or explained it away, or treated it as referring only to spiritual realities rather than the social community of the church. The failure was not a lack of resources. It was a failure of nerve and will in the face of a social arrangement from which those doing the explaining materially benefited.

Philemon — the shortest of Paul's letters — is the New Testament's most direct engagement with slavery in a specific case. Paul writes to Philemon about his enslaved worker Onesimus, who has become a Christian and whom Paul is returning. He writes: "I am appealing to you for my child Onesimus... No longer as a slave, but better than a slave, as a dear brother. He is very dear to me but even dearer to you, both as a fellow man and as a brother in the Lord." He declines to give a direct order but writes: "Confident of your obedience, I write to you, knowing that you will do even more than I ask." The clear implication is manumission. The brother in Christ cannot remain the property of the brother in Christ. Paul knew it. Philemon knew it. Every American slaveholder who read Philemon knew it and chose otherwise.

Then & Now

The Work of Slow Repair

What does the gospel ask of the church in the aftermath of this history? The question is genuinely difficult and genuinely contested, and this study does not claim to resolve it. But several things can be said with confidence.

Acknowledgment is not optional. A church that has not named what happened — specifically, in its own tradition and denominational history — has not yet begun the work. Generalized regret about "historical injustice" is not the same as acknowledging that a specific institution was founded to defend slavery, or that a specific congregation enforced segregation, or that a specific theological tradition produced arguments used to justify oppression. Specific sins require specific naming.

Acknowledgment is not sufficient. Apology without changed practice is what the prophets called offering sacrifice without justice — the form of religious compliance without its substance. The structural dimensions of the church's racial history — patterns of wealth, opportunity, access, and power that accumulated over generations — do not dissolve with a statement of regret. What repair looks like, practically, in specific communities is a question that requires local knowledge, genuine relationship, and the long patience of communities willing to do hard work across the divides those patterns produced.

The black church has something to teach the white church. A community that developed its theology under conditions of oppression, that read the Bible as a word of liberation when the dominant interpretation was using it to justify bondage, that kept singing when there was nothing visible to sing about — that community has a wisdom about God, suffering, hope, and the long arc of history that the comfortable church needs. The church's racial reconciliation is not primarily about the comfortable church extending generosity to the disadvantaged church. It is about the whole family recovering something that the disruption of slavery severed — and discovering that what was lost on one side is being kept on the other.

A Quote to Carry
"There is neither slave nor free, for you are all one in Christ Jesus."
— Galatians 3:28 — the text every proslavery theologian read past, and the text the black church never stopped preaching
Reflect

The failure of the American church on slavery was not a failure of theological resources — the resources were available. It was a failure of will in the face of social and economic benefit. Where in your own community is there a gap between the theological resources available and the willingness to apply them to a costly situation?

Prayer

Lord, we grieve what was done in your name to people you made in your image, people for whom your Son died, people whom Paul declared your children and heirs. We grieve the theological arguments constructed to justify their bondage. We grieve the silence of those who knew better. We grieve the distance between Galatians 3:28 and the Sunday morning worship of a racially segregated church. Forgive us. And give us the courage that acknowledgment requires, the patience that repair demands, and the humility to receive from our brothers and sisters what the disruption of our history kept us from learning. Bring your family together — slowly, honestly, at whatever cost — so that the watching world might see what you can make of the wreckage we have made. Amen.
