Season VIII · Study 52 · Here. Now. You.

Your Place in the Cloud

The Church as God's Ongoing Project — and You Are Next

The ReaderYour StoryVocationThe Cloud of Witnesses
Opening

This Study Is Different

Every study in this curriculum has looked at someone else — a figure from history, a movement in the past, a moment in the long story of the church. This study looks at you.

Not because you are the most important person in the story. You are not. Athanasius went into exile five times for the truth about the eternal Son. Polycarp stood before the arena and said "he has done me no wrong." Tyndale was strangled and burned so that a plowboy could read the New Testament in English. Lottie Moon starved to death giving her food to Chinese children. Wurmbrand kept his faith alive in a lightless cell twelve feet underground. You have not done any of those things. Very likely, you have not been asked to.

But you are here. You are reading this. You have walked — in fifty-one studies, across two thousand years, through eight seasons — through the cloud of witnesses that Hebrews 12:1 describes. And Hebrews 12:1 does not end with the cloud. It continues: "Therefore, since we are surrounded by such a great cloud of witnesses, let us throw off everything that hinders and the sin that so easily entangles, and let us run with perseverance the race marked out for us, fixing our eyes on Jesus, the pioneer and perfecter of faith."

The cloud is not a gallery to admire from a distance. It is a crowd of witnesses watching a race. And the race is the one you are running right now, in your actual life, in your actual circumstances, with the actual faith — large or small, settled or shaky — that you brought to this page.

What You Have Received

The Inheritance

You are an heir of an estate you did not build. Consider what you have received from the people in this curriculum alone:

From Athanasius, you received a settled Christology — the confidence that the one you pray to is genuinely God, not God-adjacent, and that his death accomplished what only God can accomplish. From Polycarp, you received the testimony that eighty-six years of knowing Jesus produces a person who can face the arena without flinching. From Ignatius, you received the church — the gathered community around the risen Lord, which you did not invent and cannot un-need.

From Jerome and Tyndale, you received a Bible in your own language — words that cost two of them their lives, which you can read for free on a device in your pocket. From Augustine, you received the honest account of a restless heart that found its rest at last, and the theological framework that made the Reformation possible. From Luther and Calvin, you received the recovery of the gospel of grace — the announcement that you are justified not by your performance but by Christ's, received through faith, given as a gift, secured by the character of God himself.

From Patrick, you received the model of mission as return — going back to the difficult people, the hard places, the ones you have every human reason to avoid. From Lottie Moon and Hudson Taylor, you received the witness that identification — genuine presence, genuine love, genuine vulnerability — is how the gospel travels. From Wilberforce and King, you received the imago dei argument applied to the most uncomfortable places — the insistence that what you believe about human dignity must shape what you do about human suffering.

From Bonhoeffer, you received the distinction between cheap grace and costly grace — and the testimony of a man who paid the full price of the distinction he made. From Amy Carmichael, you received the witness of fifty-five years of quiet, cumulative, unglamorous faithfulness that kept a door open for children no one else was watching. From Wurmbrand, you received the testimony that God's promises hold when the feeling of his presence is entirely gone, and that the cross can be carried into the darkest room a government can construct.

From the underground church, you received the demonstration that what the church essentially is — two or three gathered in his name, holding Scripture and prayer and each other — cannot be confiscated, destroyed, or expelled. From C.S. Lewis, you received the example of a mind that followed the argument honestly into a faith it had not wanted and found it more true than anything else. From the whole global church, you received the vision of Revelation 7 — the multitude from every tribe and tongue and people and nation, gathered around the Lamb, and still being assembled.

This is your inheritance. It was not earned. It was given — by grace, through the faithfulness of people who held on when it was hard and handed on what they had received.

Christ in Focus

Hebrews 12:2 gives the reason the race is possible: "fixing our eyes on Jesus, the pioneer and perfecter of faith." Not on Athanasius, though he was faithful. Not on Tyndale, though he was courageous. Not on any figure in this curriculum, however remarkable. On Jesus — the one all of them were looking at, the source and substance of everything they had and gave.

He is called the "pioneer" — the Greek word archēgos means the one who goes first, who blazes the trail, who leads the way into territory that has not yet been entered. He went first into death. He went first into resurrection. He is the one who has already run the race you are running, who has already crossed the finish line, who is therefore competent to lead you to it. He is also called the "perfecter" — the one who brings faith to its completion, who does not give up on the faith he has begun in you, who will not be finished until the race you are running is done.

"For the joy set before him he endured the cross, scorning its shame, and sat down at the right hand of the throne of God." The cross was not the end of his story. It was the instrument of the world's redemption and the door to the joy that followed. Your hard chapters — the losses, the failures, the seasons of darkness — are not the end of your story either. The pioneer has been there before you. The perfecter is bringing you through.

Your Place in the Story

What the Cloud Is Watching

Hebrews 12:1 says the cloud of witnesses surrounds us as we run. There is an old question in the theology of the intermediate state — what the dead in Christ are aware of, whether they can observe the church still on earth — that this image raises without fully answering. What the image clearly means is this: the same faith that carried Polycarp to the arena and Tyndale to the stake and Carmichael through fifty-five Tamil Nadu winters is the faith available to you, grounded in the same promises, drawing on the same Spirit, fixed on the same Lord.

You do not need to be Polycarp. You need to be faithful where you are. You do not need to go to China or India or Nigeria (though you might). You need to make disciples in the places you already inhabit, in the relationships you already have, with the gifts you have actually been given. The race marked out for you is yours — not Wilberforce's, not Lewis's, not Wurmbrand's. Yours. Specific. Personal. Marked out by the one who made you and knows exactly what you are capable of and what you will need.

The cloud is not watching to judge. It is watching as a crowd watches a runner they love — as witnesses, as those who have already run, as those who know what the finish line looks like and are cheering for you to reach it. They have thrown off what hindered them. They are urging you to throw off what hinders you. They have fixed their eyes on Jesus. They are telling you where to look.

A Quote to Carry — and to Live
"Therefore, since we are surrounded by such a great cloud of witnesses, let us throw off everything that hinders and the sin that so easily entangles. And let us run with perseverance the race marked out for us, fixing our eyes on Jesus, the pioneer and perfecter of faith."
— Hebrews 12:1–2
Reflect

Two questions — one about what you carry, one about where you look: What is the specific weight you need to throw off in order to run the race marked out for you? And what would it mean, practically and specifically, to fix your eyes on Jesus today — not on your performance, not on the approval of others, not on your circumstances, but on him?

Prayer

Lord Jesus, pioneer and perfecter — thank you for going first. Thank you for the cross that opened the way and the resurrection that proved the way leads somewhere worth going. Thank you for the cloud of witnesses: for every person in these fifty-two studies who held on when it was hard and handed on what they had received, so that it could eventually come to us.

We take our place in the race. We are not Athanasius or Polycarp or Tyndale or Carmichael. We are ourselves — with our specific weaknesses and our specific gifts and our specific calling and the specific people you have placed around us who need what only we can give them.

Help us throw off what hinders. Help us run with perseverance. Help us fix our eyes on you — not on our track record, not on the distance still remaining, not on the runners ahead of us or behind us, but on you: the one who ran this race before us and is running it with us now and will be waiting at the finish line.

The story is not over. We are in it. Let us run well. Amen.
