The Professor Who Came In from the Cold

C.S. Lewis — From Determined Atheist to the Most Widely Read Christian Apologist of the 20th Century

C.S. LewisApologeticsOxford1898–1963 AD
Opening

The Most Reluctant Convert in England

Oxford, 1929. Clive Staples Lewis was thirty years old, a Fellow of Magdalen College, and one of the most intellectually formidable atheists he knew. He had been building his case against theism for years — a case grounded not in the fashionable anti-religion of his era but in serious philosophy. He was not indifferent to the question. He had been, as a child, a believer; as a teenager, a committed atheist; and as a young scholar, someone who had thought harder about the existence of God than most people who believed in him.

The conversion came in stages, and Lewis described each one with the precision of a man who had been ambushed rather than persuaded. In 1929, riding on the top deck of a bus to Whipsnade Zoo, he described "the steady, unrelenting approach of Him whom I so earnestly desired not to meet." He gave in — grudgingly, reluctantly, with what he called the most dejected and reluctant convert in England, "kneeling down and admitting that God was God." But this was theism, not yet Christianity. The next step — from a vague theism to the specific, particular, scandalously concrete claims of the Incarnation — took two more years and was completed, in its decisive moment, during a late-night conversation with J.R.R. Tolkien and Hugo Dyson in 1931. They walked through the grounds of Magdalen in the early morning hours, talking about myth and fact and the Christian story, and Lewis went to bed a theist and woke up something more.

He described himself ever after as a "dinosaur" — an old Western Christian who believed the things that the Oxford dons of his generation had largely stopped believing, and who did not particularly care that they had stopped. He had come in from the cold not because it was fashionable but because he had followed the argument where it went, and it went to the empty tomb.

The Doctrine

Mere Christianity — and the Art of Apologetics

Lewis's contribution to Christian thought operates at two levels that are rarely found together. He was a rigorous logical thinker — his arguments have been subjected to decades of professional philosophical scrutiny and have held up better than most — and he was one of the finest prose stylists in the English language. The combination made him the most effective popular Christian apologist of the 20th century, and arguably of any century since the patristic era.

His apologetic method was shaped by his own experience as a convert. He had not believed; he knew from the inside what the objections felt like; and he refused to answer caricatures of those objections. His most enduring argument — the "Trilemma" or "Lord, Liar, or Lunatic" argument from Mere Christianity — is not, as often presented, merely a rhetorical device. It is a serious response to the most common evasion of the 20th century: that Jesus was a great moral teacher whose divinity was a later accretion. Lewis's argument: the moral teachings depend on the teacher's authority, and the teacher explicitly claimed divine authority. You cannot strip the divine claims and keep the moral teachings, because the moral teachings depend on the one making them — and a man who claimed to be God was either telling the truth, or lying, or insane. "He did not leave that option open to us."

His apologetics for the problem of evil — particularly in The Problem of Pain and later, more personally, in A Grief Observed — represent the most honest engagement in Christian literature with the question that most threatens faith. He did not minimize the difficulty. He did not offer easy consolations. He followed the question into the darkest places he could find and came out the other side with something harder and more durable than comfort.

Mere Christianity is worth naming specifically in its ecumenical ambition: Lewis deliberately avoided denominational controversy and attempted to articulate the core that all genuine Christians share — the "hall" from which the various "rooms" of denominational life lead off. This curriculum has tried to do something similar in Season VI. Lewis's framing of the enterprise remains the clearest available statement of why that kind of project matters.

The Figure

C.S. Lewis (1898–1963 AD)

Lewis was born in Belfast, Northern Ireland, to a solicitor father and a mother who died of cancer when Lewis was nine — a loss that shaped his understanding of grief and his eventual skepticism about a world governed by a good God. He served in the First World War, was wounded, returned to Oxford, and built one of the most distinguished academic careers of his generation as a scholar of medieval and Renaissance literature. His The Allegory of Love (1936) and English Literature in the Sixteenth Century remain standard scholarly works. He was, in other words, not a popular writer who dabbled in serious thought. He was a serious scholar who wrote for ordinary people as well.

His friendship with Tolkien shaped not only his conversion but his understanding of the relationship between imagination and truth — the insight, which both men shared, that myth is not the opposite of truth but one of its carriers, and that the Christian story is the myth that also happened. His Chronicles of Narnia are the most successful attempt in literary history to communicate the gospel through story to children; his Space Trilogy is a more demanding adult exploration of the same themes; his Till We Have Faces is by his own account his best book and the most theologically rich.

He died on November 22, 1963 — the same day as Aldous Huxley, the same day as the assassination of John F. Kennedy. His death was largely unnoticed in the news for this reason. He had stipulated that his funeral be simple. His grave in the churchyard at Holy Trinity, Headington, bears the words from King Lear: "Men must endure their going hence." His friends knew he would have smiled at the understatement.

Christ in Focus

Lewis's most famous statement about Christ remains among the clearest apologetic arguments in modern Christian literature: "A man who was merely a man and said the sort of things Jesus said would not be a great moral teacher. He would either be a lunatic — on a level with the man who says he is a poached egg — or else he would be the Devil of Hell. You must make your choice. Either this man was, and is, the Son of God: or else a madman or something worse. You can shut him up for a fool, you can spit at him and kill him as a demon; or you can fall at his feet and call him Lord and God. But let us not come with any patronizing nonsense about his being a great human teacher. He has not left that option open to us."

What makes this argument powerful is not its cleverness but its honesty. Lewis is refusing a comfortable evasion. He is insisting that the Jesus of the Gospels — who forgave sins (which only God can do, as the Pharisees correctly noted), who claimed to be "before Abraham," who said "I and the Father are one" — does not fit the category of "inspiring teacher." He is forcing the question that the Gospels themselves force: not "what did Jesus teach?" but "who is this man?" It is the question that drove the councils of Nicaea and Chalcedon. It is the question that drove Athanasius into five exiles. It is, Lewis understood, the only question that finally matters.

Then & Now

Still the Most-Read Christian Apologist

Lewis died in 1963. His books have never gone out of print. Mere Christianity, The Screwtape Letters, The Problem of Pain, Surprised by Joy, A Grief Observed, and the Narnia books are read more widely today than in his lifetime — by people across denominations, across cultures, and across the spectrum of faith and doubt. The evidence suggests that his particular combination of rigorous logic, genuine imaginative power, and autobiographical honesty about doubt and conversion continues to reach people that more institutional forms of Christianity do not.

His influence on the apologetic tradition that followed him — including Timothy Keller, Alister McGrath, and Peter Kreeft — is direct and acknowledged. His insistence that the intellectual case for Christianity must be made seriously, without condescension toward doubters and without capitulation to the assumptions of the secular academy, remains the model that the best Christian apologetics in every subsequent generation has tried to follow.

And his personal story — the atheist who followed the argument where it led, who was brought into the kingdom reluctantly and found it more than he had bargained for — is the most effective single testimony in modern Christian literature to the reality that the God of the gospel is not primarily found through emotional experience or cultural inheritance but through honest, relentless pursuit of what is actually true.

A Quote to Carry
"I believe in Christianity as I believe that the sun has risen: not only because I see it, but because by it I see everything else."
— C.S. Lewis, "Is Theology Poetry?" 1945
Reflect

Lewis described himself as the most reluctant convert in England — someone who had to be argued into faith against his will. How does his story speak to the relationship between intellectual honesty, doubt, and genuine faith?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for bringing in the reluctant ones — for the atheist on the bus to Whipsnade, for the professor who followed the argument into a faith he had not wanted and found it more true than anything else he knew. Thank you for the gift of a mind that thought hard about you and came back with wonder rather than dismissal. Give us that same honesty: to follow the questions where they actually lead, to refuse comfortable evasions, and to find, as Lewis found, that the sun we were trying to look at directly is the very light by which we see everything else. Amen.
