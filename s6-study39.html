Who's Minding the Flock?

Church Government — Three Structures, One Church

Church GovernmentEpiscopalPresbyterianCongregationalSecond-Order
Opening

Who Has Authority in the Church — and Where Does It Come From?

The question of how the church should be structured and governed may seem like an administrative detail — the kind of thing that matters to denominational bureaucrats but not to ordinary Christians trying to follow Jesus. It is not. The question of church governance is ultimately a question about authority: who has the right to teach, to exercise discipline, to ordain, to make binding decisions for the community of faith, and on what basis? Get that wrong in one direction and you get a church where unaccountable leaders can do almost anything unchecked. Get it wrong in the other direction and you get a church so fragmented that it cannot maintain any coherent teaching or discipline at all.

The three main Protestant approaches to church governance are episcopal, presbyterian, and congregational — named for the level at which authoritative leadership is located. Each traces its structure to Scripture, each produces distinctive strengths and weaknesses, and Christians who love each other across these lines have been arguing about them since the Reformation without producing consensus. What follows is the fairest account of each that space permits.

Three Structures, Stated Fairly

What Each Position Teaches — and What It Is Protecting

Episcopal Government locates authority in bishops (from the Greek episkopos, "overseer") who stand in a line of succession from the apostles. Anglican, Methodist, Catholic, and Orthodox churches practice episcopal government in various forms. Bishops ordain priests, confirm members, and provide oversight to multiple congregations within a region (a "diocese"). The argument: the New Testament describes a distinction between apostles (and their delegates, like Timothy and Titus) who exercised authority over multiple churches, and local elders who led individual congregations. The chain of apostolic authority must be maintained through visible succession, or the church loses its connection to the apostolic deposit.

What it protects: Historical continuity with the apostolic church. A structure for accountability that transcends the local congregation. The unity of the church across regions. A clear locus of authority for settling disputes.

Presbyterian Government locates authority in a plurality of elders (from the Greek presbuteros) at multiple levels: the local session (elders of a single congregation), the presbytery (elders from multiple congregations in a region), and the general assembly (representatives from across the denomination). The argument: the New Testament uniformly speaks of elders in the plural — local churches were governed by a plurality of elders, not a single bishop. The Jerusalem council of Acts 15 shows this multi-congregation, representative structure in the earliest church. Authority resides in collegially governed bodies at each level, each accountable to the next.

What it protects: Protection against the abuse of individual authority through shared governance. The interconnectedness of local churches — no congregation is an island. The accountability of each level to broader bodies. The Reformed conviction that all authority is derived and accountable, never absolute.

Congregational Government locates authority in the local congregation itself — the gathered assembly of regenerate believers, under Christ as its only head. Baptists, Congregationalists, and many independent evangelical churches practice some form of congregationalism. The argument: the New Testament addresses its letters, instructions, and discipline to entire congregations, not primarily to their leaders. Matthew 18:17 — "tell it to the church" — assumes the church as the final court of appeal. The church is the assembly of those who have personally committed to Christ; its authority comes from their collective discernment under the Spirit, not from succession or hierarchy.

What it protects: The priesthood of all believers in its most direct application — every member of the body has a voice. The local congregation as the primary locus of Christian community and accountability. Freedom from institutional structures that can become corrupt or captured. The direct responsibility of each believer to the Lord of the church.

What History Shows

Strengths and Weaknesses on Both Sides

Episcopal structures have produced the great liturgical traditions, sustained Christianity through the fall of Rome and the medieval centuries, and provided the institutional backbone for Christianity's spread across multiple civilizations. They have also produced some of the worst clerical abuse in history — the very concentration of authority that makes episcopacy effective also makes it prone to the corruption of unaccountable power. The sexual abuse crisis in Catholic and Anglican churches in recent decades is not unconnected to the structural dynamics of episcopal governance.

Presbyterian structures have produced some of the most theologically rigorous and intellectually serious Christian communities in history — the Scottish Kirk, the Dutch Reformed church, the American Presbyterian tradition. They have also produced some of the most suffocating ecclesiastical bureaucracies. A denomination's general assembly can become as remote from local pastoral reality as any bishop.

Congregational structures have produced genuine grassroots vitality, extraordinary flexibility, and communities of remarkable mutual accountability at the local level. They have also produced the most fragmented landscape in Protestant Christianity — thousands of independent congregations with no accountability to anything beyond themselves, vulnerable to the capture of a single charismatic and potentially dangerous leader. The history of cult movements in America has a disproportionately congregationalist structure.

The honest conclusion: every governance structure is capable of faithful expression and capable of failure. The structures do not guarantee the outcomes; the character of the leaders and the theological health of the community matter more than the polity. But the structures do shape the possibilities for good and ill, which is why the question is worth taking seriously.

Christ in Focus

"I will build my church," Jesus said, "and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it" (Matthew 16:18). He did not specify the polity. What he specified was the foundation — his own identity as the Christ, the Son of the living God — and the agent — himself. The church he is building is not the product of episcopal succession or presbyterian governance or congregational democracy. It is the product of his own action, through his Spirit, in communities gathered around his name.

The New Testament is less systematic about church structure than about the character required of those who lead, whatever the structure: above reproach, hospitable, able to teach, not quarrelsome, not a lover of money, managing their own household well (1 Timothy 3:1–7). The qualities are the same for every tradition. The structure differs; the requirement of godly character does not.

Paul's image of the church as a body — with many members, different functions, all necessary, all under the one Head who is Christ — offers the deepest logic of why governance matters: the body must be organized in a way that serves the Head's purposes, which means structures that build up rather than tear down, that protect the vulnerable rather than exploit them, that serve the community rather than the leaders' power. Every tradition has produced structures that did this well, and structures that failed catastrophically. The standard against which all of them are measured is the same.

What Holds the Family Together

All Under One Head

Episcopal, presbyterian, and congregational Christians share more than they disagree about. They share a Bible, a gospel, a Lord, a Spirit, a baptism, and a table. They share the conviction that the church exists to proclaim Christ, make disciples, and love its neighbors. They share the conviction that church leadership must be characterized by servant-heartedness, not self-promotion. And they share the experience, across all three structures, of seeing those convictions honored and violated in roughly equal measure across history.

The church's unity is not primarily organizational. It is given in Christ — "one body and one Spirit... one Lord, one faith, one baptism, one God and Father of all" (Ephesians 4:4–6). The organizational structures are attempts to express and protect that given unity in the messiness of human communities across time. All three traditions are trying to do the same thing. Their disagreements about how are real and worth engaging. They do not define the boundary of the family.

A Quote to Carry
"There is one body and one Spirit — just as you were called to the one hope that belongs to your call — one Lord, one faith, one baptism, one God and Father of all."
— Ephesians 4:4–6 (the unity beneath the structural disagreements)
Reflect

What structure does your own church use, and do you know why? What are the strengths of that structure — and what are its specific temptations toward failure? What would it take to guard against those temptations in your own community?

Prayer

Lord, you are building your church, and the gates of hell will not prevail against it. Thank you that its foundation is you — not any structure of governance, not any succession of leaders, not any organizational arrangement. Give every church, in every structure, leaders who serve rather than rule, communities that hold them accountable, and a humility that keeps the structure the servant of the mission rather than its master. And give us grace to recognize the church — your church — in traditions organized differently from our own. Amen.
