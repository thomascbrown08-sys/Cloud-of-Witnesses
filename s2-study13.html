Burned for a Plowboy

William Tyndale and the English New Testament That Would Not Die

TyndaleEnglish New TestamentMartyrdomc. 1494–1536 AD
Opening

The Argument That Sealed His Fate

Sometime around 1522, the young scholar William Tyndale was arguing with a clergyman — the kind of church official who believed that the Latin Bible in the hands of trained clergy was all the Scripture England needed. The argument turned heated. The clergyman reportedly said something to the effect that he would rather be without God's law than the pope's. Tyndale's reply became one of the most consequential sentences in the history of the English language:

"I defy the pope and all his laws; if God spare my life, ere many years I will cause a boy that driveth the plough shall know more of the scripture than thou dost."

He kept his word. Between 1525 and 1536, working in hiding in the Low Countries — because England was too dangerous — Tyndale translated the New Testament from the original Greek, and then the Pentateuch and several other Old Testament books from the Hebrew. He printed them. He smuggled them into England in bales of cloth and bolts of linen. The authorities burned every copy they could find. They also burned the people who possessed them. They arrested Tyndale himself in 1535 through betrayal by a man he had befriended. He was imprisoned for over a year, tried for heresy, strangled, and then burned at the stake in October 1536 near Brussels. His last words, according to a witness, were: "Lord, open the King of England's eyes."

Within four years of his death, the king commanded that an English Bible — based largely on Tyndale's translation — be placed in every church in England. His prayer was answered.

The Doctrine

The Priesthood of All Believers — and What It Actually Costs

Tyndale's project was not merely linguistic. It was theological. Behind the conviction that a plowboy deserved to read Scripture lay a doctrine that Martin Luther was articulating simultaneously in Germany: the priesthood of all believers. Every baptized Christian, Luther and Tyndale both insisted, stands before God without needing a human mediator. Every Christian has direct access to the word of God. Every Christian can read Scripture and, guided by the Holy Spirit, understand it and apply it to their life.

This does not mean every Christian is their own pope, immune from teaching and correction. Both Tyndale and Luther valued preaching, commentary, and the pastoral tradition of the church. But it does mean that the word of God is not the exclusive property of the ordained — and that the church's health depends on ordinary people being able to read and test what they are taught against what the text actually says. A church whose people cannot do that is vulnerable to exactly the kind of corruption Tyndale saw around him.

Tyndale also made choices in his translation that were themselves theological acts. He translated the Greek ecclesia as "congregation" rather than "church," presbuteros as "elder" rather than "priest," and metanoia as "repentance" rather than "do penance." Each change subtly challenged the institutional structures that rested on specific English word choices. The authorities understood this. They were not merely objecting to an unauthorized translation. They were objecting to the theology embedded in the words.

The Figure

William Tyndale (c. 1494–1536 AD)

Tyndale was, by every account, a linguistic genius. He mastered Greek, Hebrew, Latin, Italian, Spanish, French, German, and English — and his English was not merely accurate but beautiful. Scholars estimate that approximately 80% of the King James Bible, published in 1611, is Tyndale's phrasing. The KJV translators were not starting from scratch. They were, largely, editing Tyndale. When you read "In the beginning was the Word," "Blessed are the meek," "the spirit is willing but the flesh is weak," "let there be light," "my brother's keeper," "a land flowing with milk and honey," or "the signs of the times" — you are reading Tyndale. The English-speaking world has been reading his words as Scripture for five hundred years.

He spent his entire working life as a fugitive, moving between safe houses in Germany, the Low Countries, and wherever he could find a printer willing to risk the work. He was betrayed by a man he had befriended and housed. He was imprisoned for sixteen months in the castle of Vilvoorde. During his imprisonment, he wrote to the prison governor asking for warmer clothes against the cold, a Hebrew Bible, and a Hebrew grammar — so he could keep translating even in prison. He was never idle. He was never bitter, as far as the record shows. He was consumed by one thing: the plowboy who still hadn't been given the Scriptures in his own tongue.

Christ in Focus

Tyndale's translation of John 3:16 — the verse perhaps most associated with the gospel in summary — reads: "For God so loveth the world, that he hath given his only son, that none that believe in him, should perish: but should have everlasting life." He worked from the Greek. He chose loveth for agape, believe in him for pisteuon eis auton, perish for apoletai. The King James revisers changed almost nothing.

The people Tyndale died for — the plowboys, the weavers, the servants, the women who could not read Latin — were the people John 3:16 was written for. "The world" includes them. "None that believe in him" includes them. The gospel he translated is exactly the gospel that every generation of the powerful has been tempted to gatekeep: the announcement that access to God is through Christ alone, not through the clergy who control access to the texts that describe him.

Tyndale's final prayer — "Lord, open the King of England's eyes" — was not a prayer for revenge. It was a prayer for the thing he had given his life for: that the one who could authorize the English Bible would finally see what Tyndale had seen when he first read it in Greek. That it was true. That it was for everyone. That a plowboy's soul was worth the same as a bishop's to the God who made them both.

Then & Now

The Man Who Gave English Its Bible Voice

It is difficult to overstate Tyndale's contribution to the English language as well as to the English church. Scholars of linguistics note that he effectively invented the cadences of written English prose. The King James Bible — through Tyndale's phrasing — shaped the rhythms of English literature, the patterns of English speech, the metaphors that English speakers still reach for when they need to describe the deepest things. John Milton, William Shakespeare (who was six years old when Tyndale died), John Bunyan, and virtually every significant English writer of the next three centuries was formed by language Tyndale shaped.

None of that was what he was trying to do. He was trying to give a plowboy the gospel. He succeeded. And the gift he gave that plowboy turned out to be, incidentally, one of the greatest contributions to the English language in its history.

The broader lesson endures: when the word of God is released into ordinary hands, in ordinary language, it does things that cannot be predicted or controlled. That is not a reason for caution. That is the point.

A Quote to Carry
"I defy the pope and all his laws; if God spare my life, ere many years I will cause a boy that driveth the plough shall know more of the scripture than thou dost."
— William Tyndale, c. 1522 AD
Reflect

Tyndale's dying prayer was answered within four years. He never saw it. What does his story suggest about faithfulness when the fruit of your labor may not arrive in your lifetime?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for William Tyndale — for the stubbornness of his love, the genius of his mind, and the courage of his death. We read his words this very moment, whether we know it or not. Thank you that the plowboy did get the Scripture. Thank you that every English-speaking person who has ever opened a Bible has been reading, in large part, Tyndale's work. Make us worthy of what he gave. Make us as eager to read the text as he was to translate it. And raise up in our own generation people willing to pay a similar price so that others might hear. Amen.
