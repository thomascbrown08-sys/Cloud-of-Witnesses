The Scholar in the Cave

Jerome and the Translation That Shaped a Thousand Years

JeromeVulgateTranslationc. 347–420 AD
Opening

The Dream That Haunted Him, and the Work That Consumed Him

Sometime in the 370s AD, a brilliant and deeply ambitious young scholar named Jerome was traveling through the desert of Syria, fasting severely, reading Cicero with private delight. One night he had a dream so vivid he would describe it thirty years later as if it had happened the week before. In the dream he stood before the judgment seat of God and was asked who he was. "A Christian," he said. The voice replied: "You lie. You are a Ciceronian, not a Christian. Where your treasure is, there your heart is also."

He woke up weeping. He swore an oath on the spot that he would never again read secular literature. He more or less kept the oath for the rest of his long, contentious, enormously productive life — and redirected his considerable gifts for language to something that would shape the Western world for the next millennium.

Jerome was summoned to Rome in 382 AD by Pope Damasus I, who gave him a commission: produce a reliable Latin translation of the Scriptures. The existing Latin versions were a mess — dozens of them, copied by different hands, full of inconsistencies and errors accumulated over centuries of copying. Jerome went to Bethlehem, settled into a cave near the traditional site of Jesus's birth, assembled a team of Jewish scholars to help him work through the Hebrew, and spent the next twenty-three years producing what would eventually be called the Vulgate — from vulgata editio, the edition for the common people.

The Doctrine

Translation as Sacred Work

Jerome's decision to go back to the Hebrew and Greek originals — rather than simply revising the existing Latin versions — was controversial. He was accused of arrogance, of introducing novelties, of destabilizing what people already knew and trusted. Even Augustine, who was his contemporary and his uneasy intellectual companion, wrote letters urging caution. The people in the pews had memorized certain texts in certain words. Change the words and you disturb the peace.

Jerome's reply has the ring of a man who has sat with the original languages and cannot pretend he hasn't: if the text has been mistranslated, then what the people in the pews have memorized is not Scripture — it is the translator's error. Reverence for a corrupted text is not reverence for Scripture. It is reverence for the corruption. You cannot honor God's word by refusing to find out what it actually says.

This is the doctrine that underlies all faithful translation: Scripture has a fixed, authoritative original — and the goal of translation is not to produce something comfortable or familiar, but to produce something accurate. Jerome understood that this work demanded both technical mastery (Hebrew, Greek, Latin at the highest level) and genuine humility before the text. His letters are full of arguments about particular words, specific grammatical choices, the precise meaning of an idiom in biblical Hebrew. He brought the full force of a first-rate mind to the conviction that every word God breathed was worth getting right.

The Figure

Jerome (c. 347–420 AD)

Jerome was, by his own admission, a difficult person. He had a razor tongue and used it on almost everyone who disagreed with him; his letters contain passages of withering sarcasm directed at theological opponents, fellow scholars, and occasionally people who had done him minor social slights. He feuded publicly with Augustine — though both men managed enormous mutual respect underneath the friction. He made enemies with impressive efficiency.

He was also one of the most learned human beings of the ancient world: master of Latin, Greek, and Hebrew; prolific letter-writer; biblical commentator on a massive scale; a man who understood that the intellectual life in service of Scripture was itself a form of devotion. He translated not just the text but the living world of the text — the geography, the customs, the idioms of Hebrew thought that would otherwise be lost on a Latin reader.

The Vulgate Jerome produced remained the authoritative Bible of the Western church for roughly a thousand years. When Wycliffe translated the Bible into English in the 14th century, he worked from Jerome's Latin. When Tyndale went back to the Greek (Study 13), he was consciously doing what Jerome had done — bypassing accumulated tradition to reach the original source. Every translator of the English Bible since has been, in some sense, working in the tradition Jerome established: the conviction that ordinary people deserve access to Scripture in words they can actually understand.

Christ in Focus

The Incarnation is, among other things, an act of translation. The eternal Word — who existed before language, before creation, in the pure communication of the Trinity — became flesh: became speakable, hearable, touchable, expressible in a particular language at a particular time. God translated himself into human terms so that we could receive him.

Jerome understood this, and it shaped his approach to the text. He wrote: "Ignorance of Scripture is ignorance of Christ." He did not mean Scripture replaces Christ — he meant that Christ speaks through Scripture, and to be ignorant of what Scripture says is to be ignorant of what Christ has said. The act of translation — taking what God has spoken and rendering it accurately into a new language — is therefore an act of love for the people who will read it, making Christ accessible to them in words they can hear.

Every faithful Bible translator from Jerome to Tyndale to the translators of the modern English versions is doing exactly what the Incarnation modeled: closing the gap between what God has said and what human beings can receive, so that the Word who became flesh might dwell among yet another generation in yet another tongue.

Then & Now

The Most Translated Book in History — Still Being Translated

The Vulgate shaped Western civilization in ways that are almost impossible to overstate. The vocabulary of Latin theology — words that filtered into every European language as Christianity spread — came largely from Jerome's translation choices. The rhythms of the Psalms in Western poetry and music trace back to his Latin. The structure of the church year was organized around his text.

Today the Bible has been translated, in whole or in part, into over 3,500 languages. The work of Wycliffe Bible Translators and similar organizations continues the tradition Jerome started: the conviction that every language community deserves to hear the Scriptures in the tongue their mothers sang to them. As of 2024, roughly 1,800 language groups still lack a complete Bible translation — and organizations around the world are working, often at significant personal risk, to close that gap.

In some countries today, possessing a Bible in the local language is illegal. In North Korea, it carries the death penalty. The act of Bible translation that Jerome began in a cave in Bethlehem in the late 4th century is still, in some parts of the world, a mortal risk. What Jerome chose to spend his life on turns out to be exactly the right thing to spend a life on.

A Quote to Carry
"Ignorance of Scripture is ignorance of Christ."
— Jerome, Commentary on Isaiah, c. 408 AD
Reflect

Jerome gave twenty-three years of his life to making Scripture accessible to ordinary people in their own language. What does that cost suggest about what it means to treat the Bible as genuinely important?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for people like Jerome — difficult, brilliant, single-minded — who spent their lives making sure your word could be heard. Thank you that we can hold a Bible in our own language without having studied ancient languages or risked our lives. Forgive us for the casual indifference we so often bring to the texts that cost others everything to preserve and transmit. Give us a fresh hunger for Scripture — and the humility to sit under what it actually says, not what we wish it said. Amen.
