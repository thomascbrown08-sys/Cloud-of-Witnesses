The Word Made Flesh

John 1:1–14 and the Incarnation as the Lens Through Which All of History Makes Sense

IncarnationJohn's GospelThe LogosSeason Anchor
Opening

Before Time: The Most Audacious Opening in All of Literature

John begins differently from the other Gospels. Matthew begins with a genealogy — Abraham begot Isaac, Isaac begot Jacob — rooting Jesus in a family tree stretching back to God's first promises. Luke begins with historical specificity: "In the days of Herod, king of Judea..." Mark begins mid-stride, with John the Baptist already in the desert, already shouting.

John begins before time. In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.

It is one of the most extraordinary opening sentences in all of literature. John reaches back to the very first words of the Bible — "In the beginning" — and places in that beginning not chaos, not void, but a Word. A Logos. In the Greek philosophical world where John's Gospel circulated, the Logos was the rational principle underlying all reality — the coherence holding the cosmos together. John takes that concept and does something absolutely audacious with it: he gives it a history. He gives it an address. He says: the Word was not an abstraction. The Word became flesh and dwelt among us.

The Greek word for "dwelt" is eskēnōsen — literally, "pitched his tent." The eternal Word of God, through whom the universe was made, moved into the neighborhood. Temporary dwelling, vulnerable housing, the smell of woodsmoke and humanity. God under canvas.

The Doctrine

The Incarnation: Not One Doctrine Among Many

The Incarnation — God becoming human in Jesus Christ — is not one doctrine among many. It is the hinge on which everything turns. Remove it and every other doctrine collapses. Keep it and everything else begins to make sense.

This season has been an exploration of that truth. Athanasius defended the eternal divinity of the Son because without it, the Incarnation is not truly God entering human flesh. Cyril and Chalcedon defined the union of natures because a Jesus who is two loosely-joined persons cannot truly reconcile God and humanity in one person. Polycarp and Ignatius died and wrote what they did because they were convinced the Incarnation was a real event in real history involving a real person who had actually conquered death. Frumentius and Gregory converted Ethiopia and Armenia because the Incarnate Lord commanded his witnesses to go to the ends of the earth. Irenaeus defended the goodness of the material world against the Gnostics because God himself had entered it. The creed summarizes it all because it is, at its core, the story of the Word-made-flesh.

Dorothy Sayers once wrote that the Incarnation is "the only religious story that really dares to be specific." It does not say "God is love" and leave that floating in the air. It names a year. A place. A woman. A night. A star. A census. A manger. A face. Christianity rises or falls on the embarrassing particularity of these details — and on the claim that the particular person born in that particular place on that particular night is the eternal Son of God who made the stars that the magi followed.

Christ in Focus

John 1:14 says: "We have seen his glory." The Greek verb is etheasametha — a word for unhurried, deliberate, wonder-filled beholding. John is not using a word for catching a glimpse. He is using a word for the kind of looking that takes your breath away and changes everything about how you see. He was there. He saw the face. He watched the hands that had made the stars heal a blind man's eyes. He heard the voice that called the cosmos into being tell a storm to stop — and watched the storm obey.

And what did he see? "Glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth." Two qualities that the ancient world rarely put together, because grace and truth are in tension. Truth without grace is brutal. Grace without truth is sentimental. But in Jesus, both are fully present: complete truthfulness about the human condition and complete graciousness toward the human person, in one face, in one voice, in one life.

This is the Jesus all the figures in this season were pointing at — and the Jesus every season that follows will continue to show you from different angles. He is not a doctrine. He is a person. And the people who met him, or met the people who met him, found themselves permanently reorganized around the fact of his existence.

Then & Now

John's Prologue Is Still the Seed; We Are Living in the Tree

Every season that follows in this curriculum traces the downstream current of this one fact. The Reformation happens because the church lost sight of the grace freely given in the Incarnate Savior and needed to be recalled to it. The missions movement happens because the Incarnate Lord commanded his people to carry the news of what he had done and is doing into every corner of his world. The ethical revolutions — abolition, child protection, human rights, literacy campaigns — happen because this God-in-flesh invested human bodies with a dignity that no power structure can legitimately override.

The great heresies we have examined this season — Arianism, Docetism, Gnosticism — were all, in different ways, attempts to make the Incarnation safer and more philosophically manageable by removing the most inconvenient parts of it. Arius removed the full divinity. The Docetists removed the full humanity. The Gnostics removed the goodness of the material world. In each case, what was removed was exactly what made the whole thing work: the scandalous, irreducible, embarrassingly physical claim that the eternal God pitched his tent in human flesh, lived a human life, died a human death, and walked out of a human tomb three days later.

That is still the claim. It has not been made safer. It has not been made more manageable. And it is still changing everything wherever people encounter it and find, as the figures of this season found, that it is true.

A Quote to Carry
"And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth."
— John 1:14
Reflect

The Word "pitched his tent" among us — a temporary, vulnerable, intimate dwelling. What would it mean for you, in your actual life right now, to live as though God has genuinely moved into your neighborhood and is not watching from a distance?

Prayer

Lord Jesus — Word of God, Light of the world, Emmanuel — thank you for not staying where it was safe and glorious and uncomplicated. Thank you for the scandalous specificity of your arrival: a particular woman, a particular night, a particular body, a particular face. You did not save us from a distance. You moved in. You pitched your tent in the same neighborhood as our fear and our failure and our mortality.

As we begin this long walk through church history — through all the men and women who followed you, and argued about you, and died for you, and carried your name to the ends of the earth — keep us anchored to the person at the center of all of it. Not the doctrine of Christ. Not the tradition of Christ. Jesus himself: full of grace and truth, risen from the dead, and more real than anything else in the universe. Amen.
