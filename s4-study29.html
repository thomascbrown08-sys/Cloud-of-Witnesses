The Kingdom Has No Borders

The Global South and the Christianity That Belongs to Everyone

Global SouthWorld ChristianityMajority WorldOur Moment
Opening

The Map Has Changed

In 1900, roughly 80% of the world's Christians lived in Europe and North America. The mission movement was, in its own self-understanding, a transfer of the faith from the Christian West to the non-Christian rest. Missionaries from Britain, America, Germany, and France carried the gospel to Africa, Asia, and Latin America. The direction of traffic was clear. The centers of gravity were obvious.

By 2000, that picture had been almost completely inverted. The majority of the world's Christians lived in the Global South — sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America, and parts of Asia. The fastest-growing churches were in Nigeria, Brazil, South Korea, and China. The largest single congregation in the world was in Seoul. The most missionary-sending nations per capita included South Korea, Nigeria, and Brazil. In 2015, the Anglican Communion — the global family of churches descended from the Church of England — had more active members in Nigeria alone than in England, Canada, and the United States combined. The direction of traffic had not just slowed; it had reversed. African theologians were teaching in American seminaries. Brazilian and Korean missionaries were planting churches in Europe. The center of Christianity had moved south and east.

This is not an accident. It is not a demographic curiosity. It is the fulfillment of a promise that runs through every season of this curriculum: "I, when I am lifted up from the earth, will draw all people to myself" (John 12:32). All people. The map was always supposed to look like this.

The Doctrine

World Christianity — One Faith, Many Expressions

The shift in Christianity's center of gravity raises important theological questions about what it means for the faith to be genuinely universal. For most of Western Christian history, "orthodox Christianity" has been implicitly identified with European theological categories, European philosophical frameworks, and European aesthetic forms. The emergence of vibrant, theologically serious Christianity in cultures with entirely different philosophical and aesthetic traditions is forcing a healthy reckoning with that identification.

African theologians have brought to biblical interpretation a depth of engagement with community, ancestor, creation, and spiritual power that Western biblical scholarship had largely neglected. Latin American liberation theology — at its best, stripped of its Marxist distortions — has insisted on reading Scripture from the underside of history, from the perspective of the poor and marginalized who are at the center of the biblical narrative. Asian theologians have brought to biblical interpretation deep engagement with the philosophical and religious traditions of their cultures — meeting real questions with the gospel rather than requiring people to adopt Western categories before they could receive it. These are not departures from Christian orthodoxy. They are the necessary broadening of a tradition that had become too narrow.

The Nicene Creed we explored in Season I — "one holy catholic and apostolic church" — was always a statement about geographical and cultural breadth as much as theological unity. The church that gathered at Nicaea included bishops from Africa, Asia, and Europe. The "catholicity" of the church — its universality — has always meant that the whole faith belongs to every people, not that one people's expression of it is the standard all others must imitate.

Voices of the Global Church

A Few of the Many

Desmond Tutu (1931–2021), Anglican Archbishop of Cape Town and Nobel Peace Prize laureate, was one of the most significant Christian public voices of the 20th century — a man who grounded his opposition to apartheid in a theology of the imago dei so confident and so joyful that it disarmed even his critics. His phrase "we are all of us prisoners of hope" is one of the most compressed summaries of Christian political ethics in any language.

Oscar Romero (1917–1980), Archbishop of San Salvador, was assassinated while celebrating Mass after calling on government soldiers to stop killing peasants. He was canonized by Pope Francis in 2018. His story is inseparable from the question of what the gospel demands of the church in the face of state violence against the poor — a question the Latin American church has been wrestling with at mortal cost for generations.

The Yoido Full Gospel Church in Seoul, South Korea, founded by David Yonggi Cho in 1958, grew from five members in a tent to over 800,000 registered members — the largest single congregation in Christian history. South Korea, which had almost no Protestant Christians in 1900, now sends more missionaries per capita than almost any country in the world. The Korean church's commitment to prayer — marked by pre-dawn prayer meetings that have continued for decades — is one of the most striking features of 20th-century Christianity.

The African Independent Churches — a loose term for the thousands of Christian movements across sub-Saharan Africa that emerged outside Western missionary structures — represent one of the most significant and most theologically diverse phenomena in contemporary Christianity. At their best, they represent the gospel genuinely taking indigenous root. They deserve more attention from the Western church than they typically receive.

Christ in Focus

Revelation 7:9–10 describes the final gathering of the redeemed: "After this I looked, and behold, a great multitude that no one could number, from every nation, from all tribes and peoples and languages, standing before the throne and before the Lamb, clothed in white robes, with palm branches in their hands, and crying out with a loud voice, 'Salvation belongs to our God who sits on the throne, and to the Lamb!'"

This is not a vision of uniformity — it is explicitly a vision of diversity in unity, every human difference present and uneliminated, all of it gathered around the one throne. The diversity of the global church — its Nigerian exuberance, its Korean discipline, its Brazilian warmth, its Chinese perseverance, its Latin American liberation, its African communal theology, its Asian contemplative depth — is not a problem to be managed. It is the vision of Revelation 7 in process of being assembled, right now, in our generation.

The Christ who draws all people to himself draws them as themselves — not as cultural converts to someone else's expression of him. The worship that rises from Lagos and Seoul and São Paulo and Beijing is not a translation of Western Christianity into local dialects. It is the indigenous sound of hearts that have met the same risen Lord and cannot help but sing, each in the voice they were made with.

Then & Now

The Church Has Always Been Coming

Every study in this season has told a version of the same story: the gospel refuses to stay where it started. Patrick went back to Ireland. Cyril invented an alphabet. Carey sailed for India. Lottie Moon walked into Chinese villages. Taylor went inland. Livingstone went further. Sundar Singh put on saffron robes. And in every case, what they carried took root in ways they could not have predicted and grew in directions they could not have controlled. The seed of the word, scattered by human hands, grew by a power not its planters'.

The growth of the Global South church is the latest chapter of that story, and it is not finished. The "unreached people groups" — those communities with no indigenous church and no gospel witness — are still primarily in the 10/40 Window: the band of the globe between 10 and 40 degrees north latitude, stretching from West Africa across the Middle East and South Asia to East Asia. Most are Muslim, Hindu, or Buddhist. Most are in the least-reached parts of the world. The mission is not complete. But the church doing the mission is no longer primarily Western. It is Nigerian and Korean and Brazilian and Chinese — and it is more committed, more numerous, and more willing to pay the price than the Western church that originally sent missionaries to those places has often been willing to imagine.

The kingdom has no borders. It never did. This season has been the evidence. The next chapter is being written right now, in languages you may not speak, by people you will meet in Revelation 7 if not before.

Reflect

The center of Christianity has moved to Africa, Latin America, and Asia. How does that shift challenge or expand your understanding of what the church is and who it belongs to?

Prayer

Lord, thank you that your church is bigger than any of us can see from where we stand. Thank you for the worship rising in Lagos and Seoul and São Paulo and Beijing and ten thousand villages whose names we will never know. Thank you that the Revelation 7 multitude is being assembled right now, in our lifetime, from every tribe and tongue and people and nation. Give us eyes to see the church as you see it — vast and diverse and unstoppable — and hearts humble enough to receive what our brothers and sisters in the Global South have to teach us. The kingdom has no borders. Keep us from building them. Amen.
