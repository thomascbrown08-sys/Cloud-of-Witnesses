The Shoemaker Who Changed Everything

William Carey and the Birth of the Modern Missionary Movement

CareyIndiaModern Missions1761–1834 AD
Opening

A Cobbler with a Map and an Uncomfortable Question

Northampton, England, 1780s. William Carey was a self-educated shoemaker's apprentice who taught himself Greek, Latin, Hebrew, Italian, and Dutch while stitching leather. On the wall of his workshop he had hung a handmade map of the world, which he had pieced together from whatever scraps of geographical information he could gather, and annotated with population figures and notes on the religious state of various nations. Every day, stitching shoes, he looked at that map. Every day, the same thought returned: hundreds of millions of people had never heard the name of Jesus.

He began raising the question in the Baptist ministers' association he belonged to. The response, from an older minister named John Ryland Sr., became one of the most regrettable sentences in church history: "Young man, sit down. When God pleases to convert the heathen, he will do it without your help or mine." The prevailing view among many Calvinist Baptists of the era was that the Great Commission had been fulfilled by the apostles and that waiting on God's sovereign action precluded human missionary initiative. Carey refused to accept it. He wrote a pamphlet — An Enquiry into the Obligations of Christians to Use Means for the Conversion of the Heathens — which argued from Scripture and from history that the Great Commission remained in force and that the church was obligated to act on it.

In 1793, at the age of thirty-two, he sailed for India with his reluctant wife Dorothy and their children. He would not return to England for the remaining forty-one years of his life. He would bury a son, watch his wife descend into mental illness, struggle financially for years, and endure the kind of slow, grinding difficulty that never makes it into hagiography. He would also translate the entire Bible into Bengali, portions of it into thirty-five other languages and dialects, help abolish the practice of widow-burning in India, found a college, and spark a missionary movement that sent hundreds of workers across the globe before his death in 1834.

The Doctrine

"Expect Great Things from God; Attempt Great Things for God"

Carey's famous sermon text — Isaiah 54:2–3, "Enlarge the place of your tent... for you will spread out to the right and to the left" — produced the two phrases that became the motto of the modern missionary movement: Expect great things from God; attempt great things for God. They belong together. Neither half works without the other.

Expecting great things from God without attempting anything is quietism — the passive waiting that Ryland's rebuke represented, dressed up as faith but functioning as an excuse for inaction. Attempting great things without expecting them from God is activism — the missionary enterprise as humanitarian project, running on human energy and institutional momentum until it runs out. Carey's theology held the two in tension: because God is sovereign and has promised that his word will not return empty, his people can and must step into the world with that word, trusting him with the outcomes.

Carey also challenged a theological error that had kept many Calvinist churches from mission work: the belief that unconditional election renders evangelism unnecessary. His argument was elegant: the Great Commission is addressed to the disciples and to all who follow them. Election determines who will be saved; preaching is the means through which God gathers the elect. The sovereign God who chose the ends also chose the means — and the means is the foolishness of preaching (1 Corinthians 1:21). Refusing to preach on the grounds that God will save his own without our help is like refusing to plant seeds on the grounds that God will grow the harvest anyway.

The Figure

William Carey (1761–1834 AD)

Carey arrived in India in 1793 and spent his first seven years in the country learning Bengali, translating the New Testament, and watching his wife Dorothy deteriorate under the pressure of a life she had never wanted. Dorothy had refused to go initially and only came under pressure from her sister — a beginning that carries its own kind of grief. She suffered a breakdown that left her permanently changed. Carey's son Peter died of dysentery. The early years were, by any measure, a failure by the standards the mission supporters in England expected.

He persisted. In 1800, joined by Joshua Marshman and William Ward — the "Serampore Trio" — he established a mission station at Serampore near Calcutta. They ran a printing press, a school, a college. They translated. They preached. They organized. By the time Carey died in 1834, the Bible had been translated into or from Bengali, Sanskrit, Marathi, Hindi, and dozens of other languages and dialects, largely through his initiative. He had also worked, with considerable political skill, to persuade the British colonial government to ban sati — the practice of burning widows on their husbands' funeral pyres — which was formally abolished in 1829. He understood that the gospel that changed individual hearts also had implications for the social structures those hearts built.

Christ in Focus

Carey's missionary theology was rooted in a single conviction: Christ is Lord of all nations, not just of Christendom. The risen Jesus's claim in Matthew 28 — "all authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me" — is the foundation of the Great Commission that follows. The command goes to all nations because the authority extends over all nations. There is no territory, no culture, no people group where Christ is not already Lord by right, and therefore no place where his church lacks the authority to bring his name.

That conviction also shaped Carey's respect for the peoples he worked among. He learned not just Bengali but Sanskrit — the classical language of Hindu scholarship — because he wanted to engage Indian intellectual and religious tradition at its deepest level rather than dismissing it. He founded Serampore College partly to preserve and study Indian culture. He believed that the Christ who made all peoples had left traces of himself in the religious longing and moral instinct of every culture — and that the missionary's task was not to bring Christ into a culture where he was absent, but to make visible the Christ who was already Lord there, though unrecognized.

Then & Now

The Movement He Started — and Its Complicated Legacy

Carey's Enquiry and the Particular Baptist Missionary Society he helped found in 1792 are generally credited with launching the modern Protestant missionary movement. Within fifty years of his sailing for India, the London Missionary Society, the Church Missionary Society, the American Board of Commissioners for Foreign Missions, and dozens of other organizations had sent workers across Africa, Asia, and the Pacific. The 19th century became the great century of Protestant missions.

That legacy is genuinely mixed and deserves honest acknowledgment. The best of the missionary movement — translation work, schools, hospitals, opposition to slavery and widow-burning — represented Christ's compassion taking concrete form in communities that had never encountered it. The worst — cultural imperialism, collusion with colonial power, the destruction of indigenous languages and practices under the pretense of Christianization — represented a failure to follow Carey's own principle: expecting great things from God while attempting things from human cultural pride. These failures were real and their effects lingered. They complicate the story without canceling it.

Carey's dictum is still the right framework: expect great things, attempt great things. The errors came not from the dictum but from forgetting which half of it was supposed to lead.

A Quote to Carry
"Expect great things from God; attempt great things for God."
— William Carey, sermon at Nottingham, May 1792
Reflect

Carey's map of the world covered his workshop wall. What equivalent "map" — awareness of people who haven't heard, or haven't fully understood — might God be putting before you today?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for the shoemaker who looked at a map and couldn't unsee it. Thank you that the Great Commission is still in force — that every language, every people, every culture is a place where you are Lord by right and where your gospel is needed. Forgive us when we wait passively while telling ourselves it is faith. Give us Carey's combination: deep confidence in your sovereignty that frees us to act boldly, and deep humility about our own cultural assumptions that frees us to serve genuinely. Amen.
