Sprinkle or Plunge?

Baptism — What the Debate Is Actually About, and What Holds the Family Together

BaptismPaedobaptismCredobaptismSecond-Order
Opening

Why This Matters — and Why It Shouldn't Divide Us

Baptism is the entry rite of the Christian life. Every tradition that takes the New Testament seriously practices it in some form. And yet the question of how and when and to whom it should be administered has divided sincere, Scripture-reading Christians for five hundred years — producing separate denominations, generating heat that is sometimes in poor proportion to the light it sheds, and occasionally causing people who share a Savior to treat each other as though they share very little.

The division runs along two main lines. The first is about subjects: should baptism be administered only to those who have made a personal profession of faith (believer's baptism, or credobaptism — from the Latin credo, "I believe"), or also to the infant children of believing parents (infant baptism, or paedobaptism — from the Greek pais, "child")? The second is about mode: should baptism be administered by immersion, by pouring, or by sprinkling?

Both questions have serious biblical arguments on multiple sides. Both questions have been answered differently by Christians who loved each other and Scripture equally. This study presents those arguments as fairly as it can — not to pick a winner, but to help you understand what your brothers and sisters across the denominational line actually believe, why they believe it, and what it means that the New Testament appears to leave enough room for both convictions to coexist in the family of faith.

The Question, Fairly Stated

What Each Side Believes — and What It Is Protecting

Credobaptism — the Baptist and Free Church tradition — holds that baptism in the New Testament is uniformly presented as the public response of a person who has personally heard and believed the gospel. The pattern in Acts is consistent: hear, believe, be baptized. "Repent and be baptized, every one of you, in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins" (Acts 2:38). "Can anyone withhold water for baptizing these people, who have received the Holy Spirit just as we have?" (Acts 10:47). Baptism is the public confession of personal faith — which means it must follow faith, not precede it. Applying it to infants who cannot yet believe or confess detaches baptism from the very thing that gives it its meaning.

What credobaptism protects: The personal, conscious, volitional character of saving faith. The visible connection between the sign and the thing signified. The integrity of church membership as composed of those who have genuinely believed. The clarity of the gospel as something personally received, not externally conferred.

Paedobaptism — the Reformed, Lutheran, Anglican, and Catholic traditions — holds that baptism is the New Covenant counterpart to circumcision: the sign and seal of covenant membership, administered to believers and their households just as circumcision was administered to Abraham and his household. The household baptisms in Acts (Cornelius, Lydia, the Philippian jailor) likely included infants, since oikos (household) typically did. God's covenant promise has always extended to the children of the faithful: "I will be your God and the God of your descendants after you" (Genesis 17:7). Baptism marks the child as belonging to the covenant community — not as a guarantee of their personal salvation, but as a sign of the grace extended to them in the covenant, which they must personally embrace as they grow.

What paedobaptism protects: The continuity of God's covenant purposes across both Testaments. The corporate, family, and communal dimensions of salvation — the church as a covenant people, not merely an assembly of individual converts. The grace and initiative of God that precedes human response. The responsibility of believing parents and the covenant community for the formation of children in the faith.

On mode — immersion, pouring, or sprinkling — the arguments are exegetical and historical. The Greek word baptizō most naturally means "immerse" or "dip," and the imagery of Romans 6 (buried with Christ, raised with him) fits immersion powerfully. But the early church documents (the Didache, c. 100 AD) explicitly permit pouring when running water is unavailable, and the imagery of the Spirit being "poured out" on Pentecost connects pouring to the sacrament's meaning. The mode question is secondary to the subjects question, and most traditions acknowledge that all three modes have been practiced by Christians of genuine faith throughout history.

Voices Across the Tradition

How Serious Christians Have Answered Differently

John Calvin defended infant baptism as the most natural expression of covenant theology: God's promise to be the God of believers and their children is not cancelled in the New Covenant but fulfilled. He was exasperated by the Anabaptists who rejected it as a departure from Scripture, and his exasperation was matched by their certainty. Both men were reading the same Bible with equal seriousness.

John Bunyan — the author of Pilgrim's Progress, a Baptist — disagreed with his fellow Puritan John Owen (a paedobaptist) about baptism and maintained genuine brotherly fellowship with him anyway. Bunyan's practice of open communion — welcoming paedobaptists to the Lord's Table despite the baptism disagreement — was itself controversial among Baptists and reflects the instinct, not universally shared, that the baptism question is real but not communion-breaking.

Charles Spurgeon (Baptist) and George Müller (Plymouth Brethren, who baptized believers) represent the credobaptist tradition at its most earnest and most pastoral. Their combination of theological clarity about baptism and genuine charity toward paedobaptist brothers is a model for how to hold the conviction without the contempt.

Christ in Focus

Whatever position we hold on the mode and subjects of baptism, the meaning of baptism is agreed across every tradition: it is a sign of union with Christ in his death and resurrection (Romans 6:3–4), a mark of belonging to the covenant people of God, and a public declaration that the baptized person stands under the name of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit (Matthew 28:19). The water points to Christ — to his blood that cleanses, his Spirit that renews, his resurrection that guarantees the baptized person's own.

Both credobaptists and paedobaptists are trying to honor what baptism points to. The credobaptist wants to ensure the sign is not separated from the personal faith that receives what the sign signifies. The paedobaptist wants to ensure the sign is not separated from the covenant grace that precedes and enables that faith. Both instincts are theologically sound. The question is which application of them most faithfully follows the New Testament pattern — and that question, it turns out, has occupied careful exegetes for five hundred years without producing consensus.

What Holds the Family Together

The Water Points Beyond Itself

Every tradition that practices baptism agrees on what it points to: the cleansing of sin by Christ's blood, the new birth by the Holy Spirit, incorporation into the body of Christ, and the believer's public identification with the name of God. These are not small things. They are the substance of the gospel itself — and the disagreement is about the sign, not the substance.

The Council of Nicaea in 325 AD — which defined the very Christology that makes baptism meaningful — was attended by bishops who practiced infant baptism and bishops who did not, and the creed they produced says nothing that adjudicates the question. The Apostles' Creed says "I believe in... the holy catholic church, the communion of saints, the forgiveness of sins, the resurrection of the body, and the life everlasting." It does not specify the mode or subjects of baptism. The center has held through five hundred years of argument about the edges — which is itself testimony to how much the family shares beneath its disagreements.

A Quote to Carry
"We are buried therefore with him by baptism into death, in order that, just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, we too might walk in newness of life."
— Romans 6:4 (the meaning both sides agree on)
Reflect

Do you know what your own tradition teaches about baptism — and why? And do you know what the tradition across the aisle teaches, from its own best arguments rather than from caricature? Which of the two needs more attention in your case?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for the water that points to your Son — to his blood, his Spirit, his resurrection, his name. Thank you for the brothers and sisters who have read your word carefully and arrived at different conclusions about the sign, while agreeing entirely on what it signifies. Guard us from the pride that turns secondary disagreements into primary ones. Give us the grace to hold our convictions clearly and our fellow believers warmly — baptized into one body, whatever water applied us there. Amen.
