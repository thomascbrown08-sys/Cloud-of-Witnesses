Season I · Study 3 · Asia Minor, c. 155 AD

The Bishop Who Wouldn't Bow

Polycarp of Smyrna and the Price of Knowing Jesus

MartyrdomSmyrnaApostolic Chainc. 69–155 AD
Opening

Eighty-Six Years

It was a Saturday in February, around 155 AD, and the crowd at the arena in Smyrna — modern-day Izmir, Turkey — was in a mood for blood. They had a name: Polycarp. The old bishop. Arrest him. Someone had betrayed his hiding place, possibly under torture. When the soldiers arrived, Polycarp asked them to let him pray first. They agreed. He stood for two hours, praying aloud by name for every church he knew around the world. The soldiers who came to arrest him were embarrassed by the time he finished.

In the arena, the Roman proconsul tried to be reasonable. He offered the old man an easy out: "Swear by the fortune of Caesar. Say 'Away with the atheists.'" (Christians were called atheists because they refused to worship the Roman gods.) Polycarp looked out at the roaring crowd, raised his hand toward them, and said quietly — "Away with the atheists." He was gesturing at the crowd, not at Christians. The arena went silent for a moment.

The proconsul pressed harder: "Revile Christ, and I will release you." Polycarp's answer has echoed across twenty centuries: "Eighty-six years have I served him, and he has done me no wrong. How can I blaspheme my King who saved me?" They burned him. Eyewitnesses wrote that the flames curved around him like a sail filling with wind. They finally killed him with a spear. His friends collected his bones as though they were precious jewels.

The Doctrine

Why People Die for Jesus — and What It Means

Polycarp's death raises an uncomfortable question that the comfortable Western church tends to sidestep: Why does faith sometimes cost everything? And what has made so many ordinary people across two thousand years willing to pay that price?

The answer embedded in Polycarp's words is worth pausing over: He has done me no wrong. Eighty-six years. Not a complaint. A ledger-balancing. Whatever I lose today cannot tip the scales. He is still worth it. This is not stoicism — Polycarp wept when he heard he had been betrayed. It is not bravado — he showed no anger toward his executors. It is what the New Testament calls a "living hope" (1 Peter 1:3): a confidence about the future so settled that the present loses its grip. Polycarp had learned from the Apostle John himself that the risen Jesus is not a memory or an idea — he is a person. And persons can be trusted with your life.

Martyrdom — the Greek word martys means "witness" — is not the only form of costly faith, but it is the most extreme. And every martyr in church history has said, in one way or another, the same thing Polycarp said: the reality of Christ, proved by his resurrection, outweighs every threat the world can make.

The Figure

Polycarp of Smyrna (c. 69–155 AD)

Polycarp occupies a unique and irreplaceable position in the history of the church. He was a personal disciple of the Apostle John — the last surviving apostle, who had leaned against Jesus at the Last Supper and had stood at the foot of the cross. Polycarp became the bishop of Smyrna (a city Jesus himself had addressed in Revelation 2) and spent his long life both shepherding his congregation and vigorously opposing the heresies — Gnosticism, Marcionism — that kept threatening to reshape Christianity into something unrecognizable.

His single surviving letter, to the Philippians, is a pastoral masterpiece: warm, Scripture-saturated, specific, and urgent. The church historian Irenaeus — who appears in Study 6 — had heard Polycarp preach as a child and described him decades later with vivid affection, recording that Polycarp could describe the way in which Jesus had looked and what he had said, as if he had been there himself. Polycarp was the living chain between the apostolic generation and the church that came after. When he died, the chain closed.

Christ in Focus

Jesus called himself the Good Shepherd and said: "The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep." Polycarp, who had learned everything he knew about Jesus from the man who had known Jesus personally, died in the posture of the shepherd — unafraid, protecting his flock with his example, refusing to buy his safety at the cost of their faith.

There is something deeply Johannine about it. John's Gospel is the gospel of love. "Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends." The disciple of John died the way his teacher had described the heart of God — all the way in, holding nothing back, no last-minute recalculation.

The eighty-six years matter too. Polycarp did not arrive at courage in the arena by accident. He arrived there because he had spent nearly a century learning who Jesus is, praying, shepherding, writing, and enduring smaller refusals every day. The great moment was the flower of a very long root system. This is what the New Testament calls "the endurance of the saints" — and it begins today, in ordinary faithfulness.

Then & Now

The Oldest Martyrdom Account — and the Newest

The account of Polycarp's death (The Martyrdom of Polycarp, written by eyewitnesses shortly afterward) is the oldest detailed martyrdom narrative outside the New Testament. It set the template for how the early church understood and honored those who died for Christ: not as tragic victims, but as witnesses — to the reality of the resurrection, which alone explained why death no longer held its final terror.

That tradition is not merely ancient. According to multiple mission organizations tracking persecution, more Christians were killed for their faith in the twentieth century alone than in all previous centuries combined. Right now, in China, North Korea, Nigeria, Sudan, and across the Middle East, men and women are facing the same question Polycarp answered — and answering it the same way.

The communion of saints — the conviction that the church includes not only the living but all who have gone before — means Polycarp is still your brother. His courage is not a relic. It is a deposit made into the family account, drawing on the same Source you draw on.

A Quote to Carry
"Eighty-six years have I served him, and he has done me no wrong. How can I blaspheme my King who saved me?"
— Polycarp of Smyrna, to the Roman proconsul, c. 155 AD
Reflect

Polycarp's final courage was the fruit of eighty-six years of quiet faithfulness. What does that suggest about the relationship between ordinary daily faith and the capacity for extraordinary trust when the stakes are highest?

Prayer

Lord Jesus, you are worth more than anything we possess — and we confess we often live as though you are not. Give us the long obedience that Polycarp had: the kind that is built day by day and tested in the small refusals before it is tested in the great ones. Thank you for every brother and sister in every century and country who has paid the full price. Let their witness strengthen ours. Amen.
