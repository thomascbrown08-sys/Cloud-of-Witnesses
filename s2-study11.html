The Morning Star of the Reformation

John Wycliffe and the First English Bible

WycliffeEnglish BibleReformc. 1328–1384 AD
Opening

The Professor Who Decided the Pope Was Wrong

Oxford, 1370s. John Wycliffe was the most celebrated theologian in England — a philosopher of considerable power, a lecturer at Oxford whose students packed the halls, a man whose opinion on any theological question was treated as the educated position by default. He had the church's confidence and the king's protection. He had, in other words, everything to lose.

Then he started reading the Bible in Latin with fresh eyes. Not for lectures. For the thing itself. And the more he read, the more troubled he became. The Bible he was reading described a church of poverty, service, and Scripture. The church he lived in was wealthy, powerful, and treated Scripture as the exclusive property of the clergy — the laity were not expected to read it, and in many cases were actively discouraged from doing so. The gap between what Wycliffe read and what he saw became impossible to ignore.

He began writing. Then speaking. Then organizing. He argued that the pope had no authority not grounded in Scripture, that the clergy's wealth was a corruption of their calling, and — most explosively — that every Christian person had the right to read Scripture in their own language. He commissioned the first complete translation of the Bible into English. His followers — called Lollards, a word of obscure and probably mocking origin — scattered across England distributing handwritten copies. The church moved against him. He died in his own bed in 1384, condemned but not yet executed. Forty-four years later, the Council of Constance ordered his bones exhumed and burned. They scattered his ashes in the River Swift. Luther would later call him the herald of the Reformation.

The Doctrine

Scripture Above Every Human Authority

Wycliffe's most important theological claim was also his most dangerous one: that Scripture, not the pope, not the councils, not the accumulated traditions of the church, is the supreme authority for Christian belief and life. He did not invent this idea — it runs through the entire apostolic tradition, and Irenaeus had argued for something very like it in the 2nd century (Study 6). But Wycliffe pushed it with a specificity and urgency that his predecessors had not, and at a moment when the institutional church was in a state of visible corruption that made the argument impossible to dismiss as merely academic.

The implication was radical: if Scripture is supreme, and if ordinary Christians have the right and responsibility to know what Scripture says, then Scripture must be available to ordinary Christians in a language they can read. The logic runs directly from one to the other. Wycliffe drew it explicitly: "No man is so rude a scholar but that he might learn the words of the Gospel according to his simplicity." Every human soul had direct access to the word of God, and no institution had the right to stand between them.

This is the doctrine that would later be called Sola Scriptura — Scripture alone as the supreme authority — one of the Five Solas of the Reformation. Wycliffe planted the seed. It would not flower in his lifetime. But Luther, a hundred and thirty years later, would tend the same plant to full bloom.

The Figure

John Wycliffe (c. 1328–1384 AD)

Wycliffe was, by temperament, an academic — more comfortable in the lecture hall than the street, more effective in writing than in organizing. What made him extraordinary was not charisma but courage: the willingness to follow an argument wherever it led, even when the destination was dangerous. He had the protection of John of Gaunt, the king's powerful uncle, for much of his career — which is probably the only reason he died in his own bed rather than at the stake.

The Wycliffe Bible — completed by his associates Nicholas of Hereford and John Purvey after his death — was translated from Jerome's Latin Vulgate, not from the original Greek and Hebrew. It was therefore working at one remove from the source. But it was the first complete English Bible, and it proved something essential: ordinary people in England could read Scripture in their own language and understand it. The Lollard movement that carried it spread widely enough that even after severe persecution — burning at the stake became the penalty for owning a Wycliffe Bible in 1401 — the movement could not be fully extinguished. It went underground. It waited. And when Tyndale arrived with his Greek New Testament a century and a half later, the Lollard networks were still there.

Christ in Focus

Wycliffe's core argument was Christological: Christ, not the pope, is the head of the church. Scripture, not tradition, is the voice of Christ speaking to his people. The institutional authority of the medieval church rested on a chain: pope → bishops → clergy → laity. Wycliffe interrupted that chain by pointing to another one: Christ → Scripture → every believer directly.

This was not anti-institutional for its own sake. Wycliffe was not a cynic or a rebel. He was a man who had spent his life in the church and loved the church enough to grieve deeply what it had become. His argument was that Christ himself had been displaced from the center of the institution that bore his name — and that restoring Scripture to its proper place was the only way to restore Christ to his.

Jesus said: "My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me" (John 10:27). Wycliffe took that literally. The sheep need to be able to hear the voice. They cannot hear it if it is locked in a language they do not speak, mediated exclusively through a clergy they cannot question. Giving ordinary people Scripture in English was, for Wycliffe, a matter of Christology as much as practicality.

Then & Now

They Burned His Bones — and His Ideas Survived

The Council of Constance's decision in 1428 to exhume and burn Wycliffe's remains was not mere vindictiveness. It was an act of theological statement: we declare this man and his ideas anathema, even from beyond the grave. The Swift River, they thought, would scatter both his ashes and his influence. Thomas Fuller, the 17th-century church historian, wrote with dry precision that those ashes eventually reached the Avon, then the Severn, then the sea — and suggested that the sea represented all the nations to which Wycliffe's teaching had spread. They could not burn the idea.

Jan Hus in Bohemia read Wycliffe's writings and was burned at the stake for agreeing with them (1415). Luther read Hus and recognized his own convictions. Calvin built on Luther. Tyndale, meanwhile, worked in the same English tradition Wycliffe had opened. The Reformation was not a sudden event. It was the flowering of a seed that Wycliffe had planted in Oxford soil in the 1370s, watered by the blood of those who carried it forward.

The name Wycliffe also lives on in the organization Wycliffe Bible Translators, founded in 1942 and named in his honor — still doing what he started: making Scripture available in languages that have never had it.

A Quote to Carry
"No man is so rude a scholar but that he might learn the words of the Gospel according to his simplicity."
— John Wycliffe, c. 1378 AD
Reflect

Wycliffe risked everything to put Scripture in the hands of ordinary people — people he believed had the right and the capacity to read it for themselves. What responsibility does having a Bible in your own language place on you?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for men and women who loved your word enough to risk their reputations, their freedom, and their lives to make it accessible. Thank you for Wycliffe's stubborn conviction that every person — the plowboy, the housewife, the merchant, the child — deserves to hear your voice in their own tongue. We hold today what he died hoping others would one day have. Keep us from taking it for granted. Amen.
