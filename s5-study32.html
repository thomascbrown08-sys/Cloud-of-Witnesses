Season V · Study 32 · Germany, 20th century

The Cost of Discipleship

Dietrich Bonhoeffer and the Church That Had to Choose

BonhoefferConfessing ChurchNazi Germany1906–1945 AD
Opening

The Church That Said Yes to Everything

Germany, 1933. Adolf Hitler had been chancellor for three months. The German church — Lutheran, Reformed, and Catholic — faced a decision that the comfortable decades before it had not prepared it for. The Nazi regime was systematically reshaping German institutions, including the church: the German Christians movement, which embraced Nazi ideology enthusiastically, was working to establish a unified "Reich Church" that would subordinate Christian faith to National Socialist ideology, expel clergy of Jewish descent, and replace the cross with the swastika.

Most of the German church went along with varying degrees of enthusiasm or reluctance. A minority did not. The Confessing Church — formed in 1934 around the Barmen Declaration, written primarily by the Reformed theologian Karl Barth — declared that the church's only Lord was Jesus Christ, that no state or political ideology could claim the church's ultimate allegiance, and that a church which accommodated itself to political power at the cost of its confession had ceased to be the church.

Dietrich Bonhoeffer was thirty-two years old when Barmen was signed. He had seen what was coming more clearly and more quickly than almost any of his contemporaries — his resistance to the Reich Church began the day Hitler took power. He was called eccentric, alarmist, politically naive. He helped organize the Confessing Church's illegal seminary at Finkenwalde, training pastors the Reich Church refused to ordain. He worked with the resistance. He was arrested in 1943, imprisoned, and hanged at Flossenbürg concentration camp on April 9, 1945 — three weeks before Germany's surrender, on the direct orders of Heinrich Himmler. He was thirty-nine years old.

The Doctrine

Cheap Grace and Costly Grace

Bonhoeffer's most enduring theological contribution is the distinction he drew in The Cost of Discipleship (1937) between cheap grace and costly grace. It is one of the most important passages in 20th-century Christian theology, and it speaks directly to what he was watching happen around him.

Cheap grace is the grace that costs nothing and changes nothing: "the preaching of forgiveness without requiring repentance, baptism without church discipline, communion without confession, absolution without personal confession. Cheap grace is grace without discipleship, grace without the cross, grace without Jesus Christ, living and incarnate." It is the grace of a Christianity that has become socially comfortable, that blesses whatever the surrounding culture endorses, that offers forgiveness for private sins without ever challenging the public arrangements in which those private sins are embedded.

Bonhoeffer was writing in the context of a German church that had made exactly this trade: it had accepted a culturally comfortable, politically accommodating Christianity in exchange for its prophetic voice, its willingness to name what was wrong, its capacity to say "no" to power on the basis of a higher loyalty. The result was a church that could not recognize genocide as a theological problem because it had long since stopped letting its theology disturb its comfort.

Costly grace is the grace that the gospel actually offers: "costly because it costs a man his life, and it is grace because it gives a man the only true life." It is grace that produces discipleship — genuine, whole-life following of the one who said "take up your cross and follow me." The cross is not an emblem of a comfortable religion. It is the instrument of execution that the Savior chose and that his followers are invited to carry. Bonhoeffer carried his.

The Figure

Dietrich Bonhoeffer (1906–1945 AD)

Bonhoeffer was the son of a distinguished Berlin family — his father was one of Germany's leading psychiatrists, his mother a granddaughter of the church historian Karl von Hase. He was intellectually precocious (completing his doctorate at twenty-one), musically gifted, personally courageous, and possessed of a theological clarity that sharpened under pressure rather than dulling. He had opportunities to stay out of danger — he spent time in London as a pastor and had a position waiting for him in New York when war broke out — and turned them down. He returned to Germany in 1939 because, as he wrote to Reinhold Niebuhr, he had to be with his people in their time of crisis or he would have no right to participate in the rebuilding of the church afterward.

His involvement in the resistance, including the assassination plot against Hitler that led to his arrest, remains theologically complex and is honestly presented as such: he knew that what he was doing required stepping outside the normal categories of Christian ethics, and he was willing to carry the moral weight of that without pretending it was simple. His prison letters — published as Letters and Papers from Prison — are among the most searching and honest theological documents of the 20th century, written by a man who was living what he had written about and finding, in the hardest possible circumstances, that the Christ he had preached was present.

Christ in Focus

Bonhoeffer's most repeated question, in the prison letters and in much of his earlier work, was: "Who is Jesus Christ for us today?" Not: what did Jesus mean to first-century Palestine, or what has Jesus meant to Western Christendom? Who is he for us, in this specific situation, in this specific darkness? It was not an academic question. He was asking it in a Nazi prison cell with a date with the gallows approaching.

His answer was: Jesus is the "man for others" — the one who exists entirely and without remainder for the sake of others, who does not protect himself, who refuses the way of power in favor of the way of the cross. The church that follows this Christ cannot be a church for itself — for its own comfort, its own institutional survival, its own cultural accommodation. It must be a church for others: for the persecuted, the vulnerable, the neighbor the state has decided is expendable. "The church is the church only when it exists for others."

He was executed at dawn. A prison doctor who witnessed it wrote that he had never seen a man die so entirely at peace. The camp doctor recorded that Bonhoeffer knelt and prayed before walking to the gallows without hesitation. The testimony of those present was unanimous: he died the way he had written about living — in the costly grace of a man who had given everything to the Christ who had given everything for him.

Then & Now

The Question That Does Not Age

Bonhoeffer's cheap grace / costly grace distinction is among the most widely quoted and most regularly misapplied passages in recent Christian writing. It is frequently invoked to argue that the church should take a particular political position — and it is invoked equally across the political spectrum, which is itself a signal that the distinction is more demanding than any of its partisan applications suggest.

The question Bonhoeffer was asking is not: which side in the current political debate is correct? It is: has the church so accommodated itself to the comfort and assumptions of its surrounding culture that it has lost the capacity to say "no" to that culture on the basis of a higher loyalty? That question can be asked of churches across the political spectrum, across denominations, in every country. The German church that acquiesced to Nazism is an extreme case. But the pattern of accommodation it represents — the drift toward a Christianity that blesses what power endorses and avoids what power punishes — is a permanent temptation for every church in every comfortable society.

The Barmen Declaration — the document the Confessing Church produced in 1934 — begins: "Jesus Christ, as he is attested for us in Holy Scripture, is the one Word of God which we have to hear and which we have to trust and obey in life and in death." Every word of that sentence is doing load-bearing work. Every word is still needed.

A Quote to Carry
"Cheap grace is grace without discipleship, grace without the cross, grace without Jesus Christ, living and incarnate."
— Dietrich Bonhoeffer, The Cost of Discipleship, 1937
Reflect

Bonhoeffer asked: has the church so accommodated itself to its surrounding culture that it can no longer say "no" to that culture on the basis of a higher loyalty? Where in your own faith life is the answer to that question most uncomfortable?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for Dietrich Bonhoeffer — for the clarity of his seeing, the courage of his choosing, and the peace of his dying. Thank you that the costly grace he wrote about held him when the cost came due. Forgive us for the ways we have accepted cheap grace — forgiveness without change, confession without consequence, a Christianity that costs us nothing and therefore changes nothing. Give us the grace that is actually costly: the grace that produces discipleship, that takes up the cross, that follows Christ even when the road leads somewhere we would not have chosen. Amen.
