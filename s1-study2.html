Two Natures, One Lord

Cyril of Alexandria and the Council of Chalcedon

Hypostatic UnionChalcedonChristologyc. 376–444 AD
Opening

A Sermon That Started a Century of Controversy

Constantinople, 428 AD. A new bishop named Nestorius had arrived from Antioch, and he had a new sermon series. In it, he refused to call Mary Theotokos — the God-bearer, a title the church had used for generations. Call her Christotokos, he said — the Christ-bearer. A perfectly honorable title. Why the controversy?

Because what Nestorius called Mary revealed how he thought Jesus was put together. His sermons suggested that the divine and human in Jesus were two separate persons living in close cooperation — like a man wearing a coat. The coat touches the man, but the man is not the coat. Which meant, if you pressed it: the one who died on the cross was only the human Jesus. God the Son watched from above, uninvolved.

Across the water in Alexandria, Bishop Cyril read a summary of the sermons and put down his pen. He had seen this before. The logic led to the same destination every time: if God did not truly enter human suffering and death — as one person, not as a divine observer — then there was no real atonement. What followed was one of the messiest chapters in church history: rival councils, imperial politics, personal animosity between two very difficult men. But out of the wreckage came the most precise statement the church has ever produced about who Jesus is: the Chalcedonian Definition of 451 AD.

The Doctrine

One Person, Two Natures: The Chalcedonian Definition

The Council of Chalcedon in 451 AD defined what the church had been trying to say since the beginning: Jesus Christ is one person in two natures — fully divine and fully human — without confusion, without change, without division, without separation. Each nature keeps its distinct properties. One person.

Notice the four negatives. The bishops at Chalcedon knew they were drawing a fence around a mystery too large to fully define. They were not explaining how the divine and human natures are united in one person — theologians have debated that ever since. They were protecting the truth from four specific distortions that kept reappearing: don't mix the natures into something third (confusion), don't change one into the other (change), don't split them into two persons (division), don't treat them as only loosely connected (separation).

Why does any of this matter outside a seminary? Because your access to God depends on it. Hebrews says we have a High Priest who "sympathizes with our weaknesses, because he was tempted in every way as we are." That requires the human nature — someone who actually suffered. But this same High Priest "sat down at the right hand of the Majesty in heaven." That requires the divine nature — someone with actual authority. You need both. A purely divine Savior cannot sympathize with you. A purely human savior cannot redeem you.

The Figure

Cyril of Alexandria (c. 376–444 AD)

Cyril was brilliant, combative, politically savvy, and capable of genuine ruthlessness — his treatment of non-Christians in Alexandria was not a glorious chapter, and it should be named honestly. He is a reminder that theological accuracy and personal virtue do not always arrive together, and that God has used imperfect instruments throughout history without thereby endorsing everything they did.

His theology of Christ, however, became the standard the whole church eventually endorsed. His central concern was what theologians call the communication of attributes — because Jesus is one person, what is true of his human nature is truly attributed to the divine person. "God suffered" is not a category error for Cyril. The eternal Son of God genuinely suffered — in his human nature — as one person. That matters enormously for the atonement: it was not a human understudy who died on the cross while God watched from a safe distance.

Christ in Focus

There is a scene in John 11 that the Chalcedonian Definition was built to protect. Jesus stands outside Lazarus's tomb. He knows what he is about to do — he has told his disciples plainly. And yet the text says: "When Jesus therefore saw her weeping, and the Jews also weeping which came with her, he groaned in the spirit and was troubled." Then: "Jesus wept."

The one who is about to command the dead to live is, in this moment, genuinely weeping with those who grieve. Not performing grief. Not calculating an appropriate emotional display. Actually moved, actually troubled, actually in tears — in his human nature, as one person. That is the God Chalcedon was describing.

The same Jesus who wept at the tomb is the same Jesus who stood before it three minutes later and said, "Lazarus, come out." The divine and human are not in competition in him; they are both fully present in one person who is irreducibly both. He is near enough to weep with you. He is powerful enough to call you out of your own grave.

Then & Now

A Fence That Still Holds

The Chalcedonian Definition did not resolve all controversy. The Oriental Orthodox churches — Coptic, Ethiopian, Armenian, Syriac — split from Chalcedon over what was partly a terminological disagreement about how to describe the union of natures. Modern ecumenical dialogue has concluded the underlying beliefs may be closer than the 5th-century language suggested. The split is one of the enduring griefs of Christian history, and the conversation about repair is ongoing.

But Chalcedonian Christology remains the shared ground of Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, and the vast majority of Protestant Christianity — a remarkable convergence across traditions that agree on relatively little else. It is the standard that every orthodox statement about Jesus must fit within.

Whenever someone tells you that Jesus was "just a great moral teacher" or that "Christ" is a spiritual principle detached from the historical man from Nazareth, Chalcedon is the sixteen-century-old answer to the modern question. The church has been here before. It has thought about this more carefully than most of us have — and the answer still holds.

A Quote to Carry
"He became what we are, that we might become what he is."
— Athanasius of Alexandria (the Chalcedonian logic in a single sentence)
Reflect

What difference does it make — not just intellectually but practically, in how you pray — that Jesus knows what it feels like to be exhausted, afraid, or overwhelmed, because he actually experienced those things as one person?

Prayer

Lord Jesus, you are truly God and truly man — and we confess we rarely sit with the wonder of that. Thank you for a High Priest who sympathizes rather than merely observes. Thank you that there is no part of our humanity that you have not genuinely entered. We bring you our full selves today, trusting that you can receive them, because you have already been here. Amen.
