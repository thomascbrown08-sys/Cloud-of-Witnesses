This Is My Body

The Lord's Supper — Three Views, One Table, One Lord

Lord's SupperLutherZwingliCalvinSecond-Order
Opening

The Table That Divided the Reformation

We have already met this debate briefly in Study 21 — Zwingli and Luther at the Marburg Colloquy in 1529, unable to shake hands across the table where Luther had written in chalk: Hoc est corpus meum. This is my body. That encounter fractured the Reformation into Lutheran and Reformed streams that have never fully reunited. It deserves a fuller treatment here, because the question is genuinely important and the positions genuinely different — and because understanding what each tradition actually believes makes it possible to worship alongside people who believe differently without treating them as theologically incompetent.

The question is deceptively simple: What happens at the Lord's Table? What did Jesus mean when he took bread, gave thanks, broke it, and said "This is my body"? What is present — and in what sense — when Christians gather to eat the bread and drink the cup in his name? The answers given by four major traditions are distinct, each with strong exegetical and theological support, and none of them is obviously wrong to those who hold it most carefully.

Four Views, Stated Fairly

What Each Position Says — and What It Is Protecting

Roman Catholic: Transubstantiation. At the words of consecration spoken by the priest, the substance of the bread and wine is wholly transformed into the body and blood of Christ — though the outward appearance (what Aristotelian philosophy called the "accidents") remains unchanged. Christ is therefore bodily, really, physically present in the consecrated elements. The Mass is a re-presentation of the sacrifice of Calvary — not a repetition, but a making-present of the one unrepeatable sacrifice across time.

What it protects: The full reality of Christ's presence at the table. The deep seriousness of the Eucharist as the central act of Christian worship. The connection between the cross and the table. The continuity of priestly mediation in the New Covenant.

Lutheran: Real Presence (Sacramental Union). Christ is truly, bodily present in, with, and under the bread and wine — not by transformation of the elements, but by a mysterious union of Christ's body with the bread. The bread remains bread; the wine remains wine; and Christ's body and blood are genuinely present alongside them. Luther hammered the point at Marburg: "This is my body" means what it says. Christ said it; we believe it; the philosophers' objections about how a body can be in multiple places at once are not our problem — God is not bound by Aristotle.

What it protects: The plain, unqualified meaning of Jesus's words. The genuine givenness of Christ in the sacrament — not something we achieve by faith, but something Christ gives, which faith receives. The objectivity of grace: the sacrament works because of what God does, not because of how hard we are trying.

Zwinglian: Memorial. The Lord's Supper is a commemoration and a pledge — a public act in which the church remembers the death of Christ, proclaims it together, and renews its commitment to him. "This is my body" means "this represents my body" — the word is functioning as "signifies," as in "I am the vine" or "the rock was Christ." Christ's body is at the Father's right hand; a physical body cannot be simultaneously present in ten thousand churches; to insist otherwise is to misunderstand the nature of resurrection bodies and the grammar of figurative language.

What it protects: The integrity of the Ascension — the risen Christ is bodily in heaven, not multiplied across every altar. The distinction between sign and thing signified. The importance of the believer's conscious, personal faith in making the supper meaningful. The simplicity of the gospel: Christ died once, for all, and the table is where we remember that.

Calvinist: Spiritual Real Presence. Calvin sought a middle path between Luther and Zwingli that he believed did more justice to the New Testament than either. Christ is truly and really present at the Lord's Supper — but the mode of his presence is spiritual, not corporeal. The Spirit lifts the believer's heart to the ascended Christ; Christ truly feeds the believer with himself — his body and blood genuinely nourish the soul — but this feeding is a spiritual action of the Holy Spirit, not a physical location of Christ's body in the elements. The sacrament is more than a memorial; it is a genuine means of grace. But it is not the physical presence Luther insisted on.

What it protects: The genuine nourishment of the soul by Christ — more than remembrance, more than symbol. The integrity of the Ascension and the nature of Christ's resurrection body. The role of the Holy Spirit as the one who makes the sacrament effective. The personal faith of the recipient as the instrument by which the benefit is received.

The Conversation Across History

The Grief — and the Hope

The division at Marburg between Lutheran and Reformed traditions over the Lord's Supper is one of the enduring griefs of Protestant history. It separated two movements that agreed on justification by faith, the authority of Scripture, and the necessity of the Reformation — and it has never been fully healed. The Leuenberg Agreement (1973) established table fellowship between European Lutheran and Reformed churches on the basis of a theological statement that each side interpreted somewhat differently. Whether this represents genuine theological convergence or productive ambiguity is still debated.

The deeper grief is the irony: the table that Jesus instituted as the central act of his community's unity has become one of the most potent instruments of its division. Every tradition that practices closed or restricted communion — limiting the table to those who share their specific theological commitments — is making a judgment that the table question is first-order rather than second-order. Every tradition that practices open communion is making the opposite judgment. Neither judgment is obviously wrong; both are serious attempts to honor what the table means.

Christ in Focus

Across all four positions, every tradition agrees on the most important things: the Lord's Supper was instituted by Jesus himself on the night he was betrayed. It is done in remembrance of him — his death, specifically, the body given and the blood poured out. It proclaims his death until he comes. It is communion with Christ and with one another in him. It will find its final fulfillment at the marriage supper of the Lamb, when every barrier between the table and its host is removed.

Paul's warning in 1 Corinthians 11:27–29 — that eating and drinking "without discerning the body" brings judgment — has been interpreted differently across the traditions (does "body" mean Christ's body in the elements, or the body of Christ which is the church?), but the weight of it is agreed: this table is serious. The one who is present at it — in whatever mode your tradition understands that presence — is serious. Coming casually, coming without self-examination, coming while treating the community at the table with contempt — these are failures of reverence toward the one the table is about.

John 6:53–56 — "unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you" — is the text that most directly addresses the necessity of this feeding. Every tradition agrees that this feeding is essential. They disagree about whether and how the Lord's Supper is its primary means. The disagreement is real. The feeding is more real.

What Holds the Family Together

One Bread, One Body

"Because there is one bread, we who are many are one body, for we all partake of the one bread" (1 Corinthians 10:17). Paul's statement is about the unity the table creates and expresses — a unity that exists because there is one Christ, one gospel, one Spirit who joins believers to him. That unity is real whether the bread is transubstantiated, contains Christ alongside it, represents him, or spiritually mediates him. The one to whom all four traditions are trying to be faithful is the same one — and he promised that those who come to him he will never drive away.

The table that Luther and Zwingli could not share in 1529 is a table they will share in the new creation — not because the theological questions will have been declared irrelevant, but because the one who set the table will be visibly present, and every mystery about how he was present in the bread will be resolved in the light of seeing him face to face. We wait for that feast. Until it comes, we eat and drink in remembrance, and proclaim his death until he comes.

A Quote to Carry
"For as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes."
— 1 Corinthians 11:26 (what every tradition agrees the table is doing)
Reflect

When you last came to the Lord's Table, what were you most aware of — the remembrance, the proclamation, the nourishment, the community, the presence? What does that awareness reveal about what the table means to you, and what it might be inviting you toward?

Prayer

Lord Jesus, thank you for the table — for the night you took bread and gave thanks, broke it, and gave it to us with words that have never stopped being debated and never stopped being treasured. Thank you that across every tradition that gathers around it, the same death is proclaimed, the same risen Lord is remembered, the same return is awaited. In our disagreements about how you are present, keep us from forgetting that you are present — and that the table is yours, not ours, set for those who hunger for you. Come, Lord Jesus. Amen.
