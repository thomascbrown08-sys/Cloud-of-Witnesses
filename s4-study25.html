Season IV · Study 25 · Virginia & China, 19th century

The Girl Who Prayed China Open

Lottie Moon and the Reimagining of Mission

Lottie MoonChinaSBC Missions1840–1912 AD
Opening

The Woman Who Was Never Supposed to Be a Missionary

In the late 19th century, the rules were clear: women could not be appointed as missionaries by the Southern Baptist Convention. They could go as the wives of missionaries. They could serve in supporting roles. They could not preach, plant churches, or lead. Charlotte Digges Moon — Lottie, to everyone who knew her — was not the wife of anyone. She was a thirty-two-year-old scholar with two college degrees (unusual enough for a woman in 1872), a gift for languages, and a conviction so clear and so persistent that the organization eventually appointed her as an "assistant missionary" and looked the other way when she did everything a missionary does.

She arrived in Tengchow, China in 1873. She spent the next forty years there. In those four decades she learned Mandarin to a level that impressed educated Chinese, adopted Chinese dress and customs so thoroughly that Chinese women who had never trusted a Western person trusted her, walked on foot into villages that had never received a foreigner, and planted churches in towns that her male colleagues had written off as closed to the gospel. She also wrote letters home — sharp, passionate, occasionally furious letters — arguing that the SBC needed to fund missions properly, that women in China deserved to be treated as full missionaries, and that the organization at home had no idea what it was like on the ground.

She never married. When asked, late in life, why not, she reportedly said: "I have given my life to the Lord Jesus Christ and to China." She starved to death in 1912, having given her food allowance away during a famine until she weighed barely fifty pounds. She was seventy-two years old.

The Doctrine

Identification as Mission Strategy — and as Theology

Lottie Moon's most significant contribution to mission practice was her insistence on genuine cultural identification — not as a strategic calculation, but as a theological conviction. She watched her colleagues hold themselves apart from Chinese life, living in Western-style compounds, eating Western food, conducting services in ways that felt foreign to every Chinese person who attended. She believed this was not just tactically foolish. It was theologically wrong.

If the Incarnation means anything for mission, she argued, it means that the messenger takes on the form of the people they are sent to — not abandoning their identity, but not holding it as a barrier either. She put on Chinese dress because she believed that Chinese women would hear the gospel better from someone who had stopped signaling foreignness with every item of clothing she wore. She ate Chinese food, sat with Chinese women in their homes, learned the idioms and the humor and the grief of the culture — because she understood that the gospel must be received, not just transmitted, and that receiving requires trust, and that trust requires genuine presence.

Her letters also pushed back hard on the paternalism common in her era — the assumption that Western Christianity represented a higher civilization being generously extended to an inferior one. She saw Chinese culture with more respect than most of her contemporaries, and she insisted that the church China needed was a Chinese church, not a Western church planted in Chinese soil.

The Figure

Lottie Moon (1840–1912 AD)

Lottie Moon had a conversion experience as a student at Hollins College, Virginia, that she described as involving genuine intellectual resistance followed by sudden, complete surrender — she had been skeptical and mocking of a revival meeting, then found herself unexpectedly, overwhelmingly converted in the middle of it. She knew herself well enough to distrust easy religiosity. In China, that self-knowledge kept her from the kind of superficial piety that the mission field can produce in people who have never been genuinely tested.

She was also, by multiple accounts, simply delightful — a woman who made friends easily, who laughed readily, who had strong opinions and expressed them clearly and who people, Chinese and Western alike, found themselves drawn to. The quality that made her effective in Tengchow was not merely her sacrifice. It was her genuine love for the people she lived among — a love that Chinese women recognized as real and responded to as such.

The Lottie Moon Christmas Offering — an annual SBC fundraiser named in her honor after her death — has raised over five billion dollars for international missions since its founding in 1888. It is the largest church offering for international missions in the world. The small woman who starved to death in Tengchow has, in that indirect way, sent more missionaries to more places than almost any individual in the history of the SBC.

Christ in Focus

Paul wrote in Philippians 2:5–8: "Have this mind among yourselves, which is yours in Christ Jesus, who, though he was in the form of God, did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped, but emptied himself, by taking the form of a servant, being born in the likeness of men... he humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death, even death on a cross." This is the theological foundation of everything Lottie Moon did in China.

She put down the privileges of her cultural position — her Western identity, her educational standing, the social distance that kept missionaries safe from the full cost of the communities they served — and took up the form of a servant. Not because she was unaware of what she was giving up, but because she believed the pattern of Christ's own mission demanded it. The one who was in the form of God took the form of humanity. The one who had every reason to maintain distance crossed every distance to reach us. How could his missionaries do less?

Her death by starvation — giving food to the hungry until she had none left — is the literal enactment of the same logic. She did not plan to die that way. She simply kept giving until there was nothing left, because the Christ she followed had done the same thing, and she did not see why that should be only a metaphor.

Then & Now

The Church She Helped Plant Is Now Planting Churches

The church in China that missionaries like Lottie Moon and Hudson Taylor (Study 26) helped build has undergone extraordinary growth since the communist revolution and the expulsion of Western missionaries in 1949. Forced to operate without foreign support, without the institutional structures missionaries had established, and under severe government persecution, the Chinese church discovered that it could not only survive but grow — rapidly. Estimates of the number of Christians in China today range from 70 to 100 million, making China home to one of the largest Christian populations on earth. The house church movement that emerged from persecution is now sending its own missionaries to other parts of Asia, to Africa, and to the Middle East.

Lottie Moon's conviction — that the church China needed was a Chinese church, led by Chinese Christians, rooted in Chinese culture — proved more prophetic than most of her contemporaries were willing to believe. The Christianity that survived and flourished in China after the missionaries left was not Western Christianity adapted for Chinese use. It was Chinese Christianity, grown from seeds planted by people like her into soil that turned out to be extraordinarily receptive once it was given the chance.

A Quote to Carry
"I wonder how many of us are willing to make a real sacrifice for the sake of those for whom Christ died."
— Lottie Moon, letter from China, c. 1887
Reflect

Lottie Moon crossed cultural distance because she believed the Incarnation required it. What cultural distance — social, economic, ethnic, generational — might the gospel be calling you to cross in your own context?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for Lottie Moon — for her clarity, her stubbornness, her love. Thank you that she understood the Incarnation well enough to live it out in Chinese dress in a Chinese village, giving her food to hungry children until she had none left. Give us something of her willingness to put down privilege rather than use it as a barrier. And thank you for the church in China that she helped plant — a church now planting its own churches across the world. You are building something we cannot fully see. Help us trust you with the outcomes and be faithful with the means. Amen.
