The Room Where They Asked

The Inquisition — Power, Abuse, and What the Church Must Own

InquisitionDark ChapterHeresyInstitutional Abuse
Opening

Between Myth and Reality

Few chapters in church history have been more systematically distorted — in both directions — than the Inquisition. The popular mythology, shaped partly by Protestant polemics and partly by Enlightenment anti-clericalism, speaks of a centuries-long campaign of mass torture and slaughter in which millions were burned alive for the slightest deviation from Catholic orthodoxy. The revisionist counter-narrative, partly motivated by legitimate correction of that mythology, risks minimizing genuine atrocities as if institutional torture can be defended once you establish that the numbers were smaller than once claimed.

Both distortions serve agendas other than truth. The honest account is more specific, more complex, and ultimately more damning than the mythology — not because the body count rivals modern genocides, but because the institution that used torture and burning as tools of doctrinal enforcement was the church. The one claiming to represent the Christ who submitted to unjust power rather than wielding it was systematically wielding unjust power. That is the wound that the mythology's inflation and the revisionist's minimizing both prevent us from fully seeing.

What Happened

The Inquisition: What It Was, What It Did, What It Cost

The term "Inquisition" covers several distinct institutions across several centuries. The Medieval Inquisition, established in the 1230s in response to the Cathar heresy in southern France, was the first systematic church tribunal for investigating and judging heresy. The Spanish Inquisition, established by the Spanish Crown in 1478 and operated under papal authority, targeted primarily Jewish and Muslim converts to Christianity (conversos and moriscos) suspected of secretly maintaining their former faith. The Roman Inquisition, founded in 1542, focused on the threat of Protestantism in Italy and produced — among other cases — the trial and condemnation of Galileo. The Portuguese Inquisition operated in Portugal and its colonies, including Goa.

What they shared: they were church-sanctioned institutions that used interrogation, including torture, to extract confessions of heresy, and that handed convicted heretics over to secular authorities for execution — typically by burning. The church's justification for handing condemned heretics to the secular arm rather than executing them directly was the canonical principle that the church "does not shed blood" (ecclesia non sitit sanguinem). The tortured logic of this distinction was not lost on contemporaries, and it is not lost now.

The modern historical scholarship on the Spanish Inquisition — particularly the work emerging from the opening of Vatican archives in 1998 — has substantially revised the body-count mythology upward from the revisionists' minimizing and downward from the earlier mythology. A reasonable scholarly consensus suggests that the Spanish Inquisition executed somewhere between 3,000 and 5,000 people over its approximately 350-year existence — far fewer than the millions of popular imagination, but far more than any figure compatible with defending the institution. Beyond the executions, the institution imprisoned thousands, tortured an unknown but substantial number, confiscated property, destroyed reputations, and created a culture of informers and fear across Spanish society for generations.

The Spanish Inquisition's particular targets — Jewish and Muslim converts suspected of secret practice of their former faiths — adds a dimension of ethnic and religious persecution that goes beyond the abstract category of "heresy enforcement." The limpieza de sangre (purity of blood) statutes that developed alongside the Inquisition made Jewish or Moorish ancestry itself a mark of suspicion, regardless of faith practice. This is, structurally, a form of racial persecution — carried out under the authority of an institution claiming to represent Christ.

Figures: The Human Cost and the Human Resistance

Those Who Were Caught and Those Who Refused

Tomás de Torquemada (c. 1420–1498), the first Grand Inquisitor of Spain, has become the byword for inquisitorial cruelty — partly through exaggeration, but with a substantial foundation of historical fact. He oversaw the expulsion of the Jews from Spain in 1492 and the formalization of torture procedures that governed the Spanish Inquisition for generations. He was, by his own account and by the account of his contemporaries, a man of genuine if severe religious conviction. He believed he was protecting souls and the purity of the faith. The sincerity of the conviction does not rehabilitate its expression.

The converso community — Jews who had converted to Christianity, some sincerely and some under the duress of forced baptism or social pressure — represented the human reality at the center of the Spanish Inquisition's work. Many were genuinely Christian by faith. Many maintained some degree of Jewish practice alongside Christian practice, as people whose identity spanned two worlds. The Inquisition made no serious attempt to distinguish these cases with pastoral sensitivity; it treated the entire community as suspects and used torture to extract confessions of "Judaizing" that could not be reliably distinguished from coerced compliance.

Las Casas and other critics within the church spoke against related forms of institutional cruelty in the colonial context — the treatment of indigenous peoples — demonstrating that the capacity for internal dissent existed, even if it was rarely heeded. The institution was not monolithic; the abuse it produced was not inevitable. It was chosen, and it could have been chosen differently.

Christ in Focus

Jesus stood before the high priest's tribunal and was asked: "Are you the Christ, the Son of the Blessed?" He answered honestly and was condemned for it. He stood before Pilate and was tortured and executed as a heretic and revolutionary, under due process of the legal authorities of his day. The one who built the Inquisition claimed to represent the one who was its prototype victim.

The Sermon on the Mount says: "Blessed are the merciful, for they shall receive mercy." The Beatitudes do not include: "Blessed are those who extract confessions under duress." The Great Commission says "go and make disciples" — teach, baptize, instruct. It does not say "compel compliance on pain of burning." The theological tradition that produced the Inquisition did not lack access to the words of Jesus. It lacked the will to let those words govern its institutional behavior when that behavior served the interests of power.

Augustine had written, in the context of the Donatist controversy, a careful and cautious argument for state coercion of heretics — an argument he reached reluctantly and with serious qualifications. By the 13th century, those qualifications had been stripped away and the remaining argument had been handed to institutions with no interest in restraint. This is a pattern the church has produced more than once: a careful theological argument, developed in a specific context with specific limits, stripped of its limits and applied without care. The result, in the Inquisition's case, was centuries of institutional torture in the name of the Lord of the Beatitudes.

Then & Now

What the Inquisition Teaches Every Generation

The most important lesson the Inquisition teaches is not about medieval Catholics. It is about the dynamics of institutional power when it is insulated from accountability. Institutions — including church institutions — reliably protect themselves, expand their authority, and find theological justifications for doing both. The Inquisition was not staffed by people who thought of themselves as villains. It was staffed by people who believed deeply in what they were doing and who were accountable, in practice, to no one capable of stopping them. The combination of sincere conviction, institutional interest, and unaccountable power reliably produces atrocity. It did in the 13th century. It has done so in every century since.

The structural answer to institutional abuse is not the elimination of institutions — the church needs structure, as Season VI's study on church governance examined. The structural answer is accountability: leadership that must answer to the community it serves, financial transparency, genuine due process, the capacity of ordinary members to raise concerns without retaliation, and — undergirding all of it — a theology of institutional humility that takes seriously the church's own susceptibility to the corruptions of power.

Pope John Paul II's 2000 apology for the Inquisition acknowledged real institutional sin. The more demanding question is what institutional change follows acknowledgment. Confession without repentance — change of direction — is not yet the gospel applied to the church's own life. The family that has grieved its worst chapter can be trusted more than the family that pretends the chapter does not exist. But grief is only the beginning.

Reflect

The Inquisition was produced by institutions insulated from accountability. Where in your own church community — at whatever scale — is leadership accountable to those it serves? Where is it not? What would it take to close those gaps?

Prayer

Lord, we grieve the rooms where people were brought in your name and made to suffer. We grieve the confessions extracted under duress, the lives destroyed, the communities terrorized, the conversos who practiced your Son's religion under threat of death for not practicing it purely enough. Forgive your church. Protect us from the self-justifying logic of institutional power dressed in theological language. Give us leaders who serve rather than rule, structures that hold them accountable, and the courage to name what is wrong in our own house before waiting to be judged for it. Amen.
