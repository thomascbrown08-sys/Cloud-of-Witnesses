How We Got the Bible

Athanasius, the Council of Carthage, and the Quiet Work of Canon Formation

CanonScriptureAthanasius367–397 AD
Opening

A Letter That Changed Everything — and Nobody Noticed at First

Every year, the Bishop of Alexandria sent a letter to the churches under his care announcing the date of Easter. In 367 AD, Athanasius — the same Athanasius we met in Study 1, five exiles behind him and still writing — sent his 39th such letter. Most of it was routine. But buried in the middle was a list. Twenty-seven books. The exact twenty-seven books that make up the New Testament you can pick up today. For the first time in recorded history, someone had written down precisely those books and said: these, and no others.

It was not a dramatic moment. There was no thunderclap, no council vote, no imperial decree. Just an old bishop, near the end of his life, writing one more pastoral letter to his scattered churches — and including something he apparently felt they needed: a clear answer to a question that had been generating confusion for three centuries. Which books, exactly, are Scripture?

Thirty years later, in 397 AD, the Council of Carthage formally ratified the same list. The canon was closed — not by power, but by recognition. The church was not deciding which books were authoritative. It was acknowledging which books had already proven themselves to be.

The Doctrine

How the Church Recognized Scripture — and Why That Matters

The word canon comes from a Greek word for a measuring rod or ruler — a standard of measurement. Applied to Scripture, it means the authoritative collection of books that sets the standard for Christian doctrine and life. The question of how that collection was determined matters enormously, because the answer shapes everything about how you relate to your Bible.

The popular version of the story — promoted in books like The Da Vinci Code and echoed in countless college classrooms — is that the canon was imposed by political authority, that the Council of Nicaea (or Constantine, or some other powerful institution) selected certain books and suppressed others for political purposes. This version is historically inaccurate in almost every detail. Nicaea did not vote on the canon. Constantine did not commission a shortlist. The books excluded from the canon were excluded because they were written late, claimed apostolic authorship they did not have, and taught things clearly incompatible with what the churches had received from the apostles from the beginning.

The actual process was organic, slow, and driven by use. Churches everywhere read Matthew, Mark, Luke, John, Paul's letters, and a handful of other texts from the beginning. Disputed books — Hebrews, James, 2 Peter, Revelation — took longer, because different regions had different levels of familiarity with them. The process by which the church recognized the canon was the same process by which you recognize a trustworthy person: over time, under pressure, through repeated testing, the real thing proves itself. What Athanasius did in 367 AD was not impose a new list. He acknowledged what the churches had already been reading and trusting for generations.

This matters because it means your Bible is not a human product — it is a human recognition of a divine product. The church did not create Scripture. The Spirit did. The church, at its best, simply listened.

The Figure

Athanasius — Again (and Why That's Significant)

You have already met Athanasius in Study 1, fighting for the eternal divinity of the Son against the Arians. The fact that he also appears at the hinge point of canon formation is not a coincidence. It is a pattern. The same theological instinct that made Athanasius insist on the full divinity of Christ — the conviction that salvation requires the real God to have really entered history — also drove his insistence on a defined, settled Scripture. You cannot have a trustworthy revelation if the collection of texts that bears that revelation is open-ended and undefined.

Athanasius was a man who understood that the church needs clear walls — not to imprison the Spirit, but to protect the people. Undefined theology and undefined Scripture both left the flock vulnerable to whatever the most persuasive teacher of the moment happened to say. The Festal Letter of 367 was, in its own way, the same act as the Council of Nicaea: a refusal to let clarity be traded for a comfortable vagueness.

Christ in Focus

Jesus himself treated the Old Testament as authoritative Scripture — quoting it in his temptation, citing it in his teaching, claiming to fulfill it in his person. In John 10:35 he says, almost in passing, "Scripture cannot be broken" — a single phrase that carries the entire weight of his view of biblical authority. He did not treat the texts as human documents that happened to contain useful wisdom. He treated them as the word of God, binding and unbreakable.

And before his ascension, he commissioned his apostles to be his witnesses — to speak and write with an authority derived from their firsthand encounter with him and the Spirit's work through them. The New Testament canon is, at its root, the documentary deposit of that apostolic witness. When the church recognized those twenty-seven books as Scripture, it was recognizing the voice of the one who said, "Whoever hears you hears me" (Luke 10:16).

The Bible is not primarily a book about religion. It is a book about Jesus — written in anticipation of him, or in the wake of him, by people whose lives he had reordered entirely. To hold a Bible is to hold two thousand years of testimony to the same person.

Then & Now

The Canon Is Settled — and the Question Keeps Coming Back

Every generation produces a fresh attempt to expand, contract, or relativize the canon. The Gnostic gospels of the 2nd century tried to add texts with secret teachings. The Protestant Reformation debated whether the Apocrypha belonged. Modern scholarship periodically "discovers" that some suppressed gospel represents an authentic alternative tradition. The impulse is always the same: the canon you have is incomplete, or politically motivated, or excluding voices that deserve to be heard.

The honest response is to engage the actual historical record. The books of the New Testament were written within the lifetime of eyewitnesses to Jesus (or those who knew eyewitnesses), were read universally in the churches from the beginning, and were recognized as apostolic in origin and content. The books excluded were written later, claimed false authorship, and taught doctrines incompatible with what Jesus's own disciples had handed on. The canon was not arbitrary — it was discriminating, in the best sense.

The practical weight of all this: the Bible you hold is not a collection of human opinions about God. It is the tested, recognized, cross-generational testimony to the one event in history that changed everything. You can trust it. And the people who died to preserve it, copy it, and translate it — whom you will meet in the studies that follow — trusted it enough to stake their lives on it.

A Quote to Carry
"These are the fountains of salvation, so that he who thirsts may be satisfied with the living words they contain. In these alone is proclaimed the doctrine of godliness."
— Athanasius, 39th Festal Letter, 367 AD (introducing his list of the New Testament books)
Reflect

The canon was recognized, not invented — the church listened for what the Spirit had already authenticated. How does that distinction change the way you think about the authority of your Bible?

Prayer

Father, thank you for not leaving us to guess. Thank you for a word that is tested, trustworthy, and able to be held in human hands. Thank you for Athanasius and for every generation of faithful people who guarded what had been entrusted to them — and for passing it to us intact. Give us a deep love for Scripture — not as a rule book, but as the voice of the One who made us and redeemed us. Let us hear you speaking in it, today. Amen.
