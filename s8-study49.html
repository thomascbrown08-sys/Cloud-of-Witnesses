Season VIII · Study 49 · China & the Soviet Union, 20th century

The Church That Wouldn't Die

The Underground Church in China and the Soviet Union — How Persecution Pruned and Purified

Underground ChurchChinaSoviet UnionPersecution
Opening

What Happens When You Try to Destroy a Church

China, 1949. The People's Liberation Army completes its victory. Mao Zedong declares the founding of the People's Republic. Within two years, the last Western missionaries have been expelled, indigenous church leaders have been imprisoned or killed, church buildings have been seized, and the Chinese church has been officially dissolved as an independent institution. By any measure available to the Communist government, Christianity in China has been eliminated. The experiment is effectively over.

Fifty years later, the Chinese church numbered somewhere between 60 and 100 million people — the single largest rapid growth of Christianity in any nation in history. It had grown not in spite of the persecution but, in a very specific theological sense, partly because of it. Stripped of Western institutional support, Western leadership, Western buildings, Western money, and Western organizational models, the Chinese church had been forced to become Chinese — and in becoming Chinese, it had become something that could not be expelled with the missionaries, confiscated with the buildings, or imprisoned with the leaders. It had become the people themselves.

The Soviet Union's story is parallel in structure. The Bolshevik revolution of 1917 launched what may have been the most sustained and systematic attempt to eliminate Christianity in history — seventy years of organized state atheism, church closures, clergy execution, religious education bans, and the projection of atheism as the ideology of the modern, educated, progressive person. When the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, the Russian Orthodox Church had not been destroyed. It had been driven underground, stripped of everything institutional, and returned — as the early church had been — to its irreducible core: the people who would not stop praying, the babushkas who passed faith to their grandchildren in whispers, the priests who served illegally in apartments and forests.

The Doctrine

Persecution and the Pruning of the Church

Jesus said in John 15:2: "Every branch that does bear fruit he prunes, that it may bear more fruit." The image is horticultural but the logic is theological: pruning removes what is unproductive so that what remains can be more fully productive. The church has experienced, across its history, that certain kinds of institutional excess, cultural comfort, and structural complexity — which accumulate inevitably in periods of freedom — are stripped away in periods of persecution, leaving a church that is simpler, more biblically grounded, and more dependent on God than on its own arrangements.

This does not mean persecution is good, or that God sends it, or that the suffering it produces is anything other than genuine evil inflicted by human beings on other human beings. The families broken by imprisonment, the children raised without parents, the Christians killed for their faith — these are tragedies, not blessings. What the theology of pruning recognizes is that God can bring fruit from what was intended as destruction, and that the church that has been stripped of everything but Christ sometimes discovers, in that stripping, what was always the most essential thing.

The Chinese house church developed a theological depth and a personal intimacy with Scripture — forced to memorize entire books because printed Bibles were unavailable, forced to gather in small groups because large gatherings were dangerous — that the institutional church, comfortable in its buildings and programs, often lacks. The Soviet church produced, in figures like Alexander Solzhenitsyn and Father Aleksandr Men, some of the most profound Christian thinkers of the 20th century — minds shaped by suffering into an engagement with ultimate questions that the comfortable Western academy rarely matched.

Figures: From the Underground

Voices That Survived the Attempt to Silence Them

Wang Mingdao (1900–1991) was the most significant indigenous Chinese church leader of the 20th century — a preacher of extraordinary gifts who refused both Communist registration requirements and the theological compromises that registration would have required. He was imprisoned for twenty-three years, suffered a breakdown during which he recanted his position, recovered his faith, recanted his recantation, and returned to prison voluntarily rather than live under the terms he had accepted in weakness. He was released in 1980 at the age of eighty, nearly blind, and immediately resumed preaching. His example shaped the entire theology of the Chinese house church movement: that faithfulness sometimes requires refusing the terms the state offers, at whatever cost.

Aida Skripnikova was a young Leningrad woman in the 1960s who distributed Christian literature in the streets of the Soviet Union and was repeatedly imprisoned for it. Her testimony — available in Detained for Her Faith — is a portrait of stubborn, cheerful, quietly ferocious faith that consistently baffled and eventually frustrated the authorities who could not find a way to make her stop.

The babushkas — the grandmothers — are the unsung heroes of the Soviet church. In communities where public religious practice had been suppressed for generations, it was often the oldest women who kept the faith alive: teaching their grandchildren prayers and Scripture stories in secret, maintaining the rhythms of the liturgical year in apartments, preserving the embodied memory of Christian practice across the gap of official atheism. When the Soviet Union fell and religious practice became legal again, the first generation to return to the churches found that the grandmothers had been keeping everything alive in the kitchen.

Christ in Focus

Jesus told his disciples in Matthew 16:18: "I will build my church, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it." The Chinese Communist Party tried to build gates against it. The Soviet Union tried to build gates against it. The gates did not prevail. Not because the church was powerful — in both cases, it was stripped of every form of institutional power — but because the one building it was not the church. It was Christ. And Christ, having passed through death and come out the other side, is not threatened by governments.

The underground church's experience also illuminates what the church actually is, at its irreducible core. It is not buildings or programs or denominations or endowments. It is people who have been joined to Christ by faith, filled with his Spirit, and gathered in his name. Two or three in a basement in Beijing or an apartment in Leningrad, reading smuggled Scripture by candlelight — that is the church in its essential form. Everything else is scaffolding. The Chinese and Soviet experience stripped away the scaffolding. What remained was the building.

Then & Now

The Underground Church's Message to the Comfortable Church

The Chinese house church, now numbering in the tens of millions, is not simply a survival story. It is a sending story. The "Back to Jerusalem" vision — the conviction among many Chinese Christians that God is calling them to send missionaries westward along the Silk Road, through the Muslim and Buddhist heartlands of Central Asia, back toward Jerusalem — represents one of the most remarkable mission movements of the 21st century. The church that was supposed to be eliminated is now planning to evangelize the nations. The irony is not lost on those who remember 1949.

The message of the underground church to the comfortable Western church is not primarily one of guilt — "you have it easy and they have it hard." It is primarily one of testimony: this is what the church looks like when it has nothing but Christ, and it turns out that Christ is enough. More than enough. The testimony of Wang Mingdao and the babushkas and the house church members who memorized entire Gospels because they could not afford a Bible is a testimony about the adequacy of the One they clung to, not about the extraordinary heroism of those who clung.

The comfortable church needs that testimony. Not to produce guilt about its comfort, but to recalibrate what it considers essential. When the scaffolding is optional rather than mandatory, the question becomes: are you investing in the scaffolding or in the building? Are you cultivating programs and properties and institutional prestige, or are you cultivating the kind of faith that survives when all of that is taken away?

A Quote to Carry
"The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church."
— Tertullian, c. 197 AD — still true in China, still true in the Soviet Union, still true now
Reflect

The Chinese house church members memorized entire books of Scripture because they had no Bibles. You almost certainly have multiple Bibles within reach right now. What does the gap between their relationship to Scripture and yours tell you about what you might be taking for granted?

Prayer

Lord, thank you that the gates of hell did not prevail — not in China, not in the Soviet Union, not in Romania, not in any of the places where governments spent decades trying to destroy what you were building. Thank you for Wang Mingdao and the babushkas and the millions whose names we do not know who kept the faith alive in basements and kitchens and forests. Teach us, from their testimony, what is actually essential. Strip from us our dependence on the scaffolding that you have not promised to preserve. And grow in us the kind of faith that survives when everything except Christ has been taken away — because it turns out that everything except Christ was never what we needed anyway. Amen.
