Season IV · Study 27 · Scotland & Africa, 19th century

A Doctor in Africa's Heart

David Livingstone, Mission, Exploration, and the Conscience of a Nation

LivingstoneAfricaAbolition1813–1873 AD
Opening

The Mill Boy Who Became Africa's Missionary

David Livingstone began working in a cotton mill in Blantyre, Scotland, at the age of ten — twelve hours a day, six days a week. He propped a book on the spinning machine frame and read during the few moments when his hands were free. He taught himself Latin. By the time he was sixteen he could read the New Testament in Greek. He saved enough money to put himself through medical school, applied to the London Missionary Society, and in 1840 sailed for South Africa. He would spend most of the next thirty-three years in the African interior, dying there on his knees in prayer in a remote village in present-day Zambia in 1873.

Livingstone did not fit neatly into any category. He was a missionary — but he planted relatively few churches and baptized relatively few converts by the standards of his era. He was an explorer — credited with being the first European to see Victoria Falls, with mapping vast stretches of central Africa — but he was contemptuous of exploration for its own sake. He was an abolitionist — his published accounts of the Arab-African slave trade in East Africa shocked British public opinion and directly contributed to political pressure that eventually suppressed that trade — but he sometimes underestimated the degree to which European presence in Africa, including his own, was paving roads the colonial powers would later use.

What drove him was a single conviction, stated most clearly in a speech he gave at Cambridge in 1857: "I beg to direct your attention to Africa. I know that in a few years I shall be cut off in that country, which is now open; do not let it be shut again! I go back to Africa to try to make an open path for commerce and Christianity."

The Doctrine

The Gospel and the Slave Trade — When Faith Demands Action

Livingstone's coupling of "commerce and Christianity" has made later generations uncomfortable, and the discomfort is not entirely misplaced. His theory — that legitimate trade, by replacing the slave trade as the economic engine of central Africa, would create conditions in which Christianity could flourish — was partly right and partly tragically wrong. The legitimate trade he envisioned was real enough; the Christianity he hoped would follow did come, in powerful and lasting forms. What he underestimated was the degree to which the commercial and colonial interests he hoped would displace the slave trade would develop their own forms of exploitation.

What is beyond dispute is his moral clarity about the slave trade itself. He saw it up close — the caravans of chained captives, the burned villages, the skeletal bodies left on the road — and he wrote about it with a precision and a fury that penetrated British drawing rooms in a way that abstract arguments could not. His journals and letters produced political results: the British government moved more aggressively against the East African slave trade in the years following his published accounts. The connection between Christian conviction and antislavery action — which Wilberforce had embodied in the previous generation (Season V) — ran through Livingstone in a distinctly missionary key.

The theological conviction undergirding his abolitionism was the same one undergirding his mission: every human being, regardless of race or origin, bears the image of God. The enslaved African chained on the road to Zanzibar is an image-bearer of the same God who made David Livingstone, and who died for both of them. That conviction, taken seriously, is incompatible with slavery. Livingstone took it seriously.

The Figure

David Livingstone (1813–1873 AD)

Livingstone was not an easy man — his family life was troubled, his relationship with his wife Mary (who died of fever in Africa in 1862) was shaped by his near-total inability to stay home, his relationships with mission partners were frequently strained by his fierce independence, and his later expeditions were plagued by poor planning and difficult companions. Henry Morton Stanley — the journalist sent by the New York Herald to find him when he had been out of contact for years — famously greeted him: "Dr. Livingstone, I presume?" Livingstone was amused. He had not known he was lost.

When he died in 1873, his African companions — Susi and Chuma — performed one of the most extraordinary acts of loyalty in the history of exploration. They removed his heart and buried it under a tree in the village where he died. Then they embalmed his body, wrapped it in bark and sail cloth, and carried it over a thousand miles to the coast — an eleven-month journey — so it could be returned to England for burial in Westminster Abbey. They understood, better than most of his contemporaries, what he had meant.

Christ in Focus

Livingstone is found dead kneeling by his bed in the act of prayer — a death scene so perfectly composed that later interpreters have sometimes questioned it. His companions' testimony, however, is consistent: they found him in that position in the early morning hours of May 1, 1873, cold but kneeling, his head in his hands. Whatever the exact posture, the life it summarized was real: a man who had given himself so completely to the people and the work he loved that there was nothing left to give, still in conversation with the God who had sent him.

Jesus said: "The Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many" (Matthew 20:28). Livingstone's life was a long, slow, non-dramatic version of that pattern — not death on a cross but death in a village in Zambia, kneeling, still praying for the continent he had given everything to. The form is different. The logic is the same: a life poured out for the sake of others, in obedience to the one who poured out his own.

Then & Now

Africa's Christianity — and Africa's Own Missionaries

Sub-Saharan Africa is today the most Christian region on earth by proportion of population. Livingstone helped open the interior to mission. The missionaries who followed him — from dozens of societies and denominations — planted churches that are now among the most vibrant in the world. The church in Nigeria, the church in the Democratic Republic of Congo, the church in Kenya and Uganda and Zimbabwe — these are not outposts of Western Christianity. They are African churches, shaped by African theological voices, reading Scripture through African eyes, and increasingly sending their own missionaries outward.

Nigeria alone sends more missionaries per capita than the United Kingdom. The African church is one of the most significant missionary forces in the world today. Livingstone's prayer — that Africa not be "shut again" — has been answered in ways that would astonish him. The continent he walked across is now walking the gospel outward to the rest of the world.

A Quote to Carry
"I am prepared to go anywhere, provided it be forward."
— David Livingstone
Reflect

Livingstone's African companions carried his body a thousand miles out of loyalty and love. What does that act of faithfulness — from people whose names history nearly forgot — suggest about who really carries the mission of God?

Prayer

Father, thank you for David Livingstone — for his mill-boy stubbornness and his African heart, for his fury at slavery and his love for a continent he gave his life to. Thank you for Susi and Chuma, who carried him home through a thousand miles of bush because they understood what he had meant and what he had given. Thank you for the African church — ancient and new — that he helped plant and that now plants its own. Give us his readiness: to go anywhere, provided it be forward. Amen.
