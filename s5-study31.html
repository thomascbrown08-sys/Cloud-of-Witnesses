The Little Poor Man

Francis of Assisi and the Witness of a Life Poured Out

Francis of AssisiPovertyCreation1181–1226 AD
Opening

A Note Before We Begin

Francis of Assisi is one of the most beloved and most misunderstood figures in the history of the church. He is beloved by Catholics and Protestants alike, by secularists and believers, by environmentalists and traditionalists — which is itself a signal that he means different things to different people, and that some of those meanings are more faithful to the historical man than others.

We include him here for the things that are genuinely, powerfully right about his witness: his radical embodiment of Christian poverty as a protest against materialism, his care for the poor and the sick at personal cost, and his love of the created world as the work of its Maker. We note, without adjudicating, that some elements of medieval Franciscan theology — including certain forms of merit theology and the veneration of Francis himself — belong to the second-order differences between Catholic and Protestant traditions. We sit with what is true and beautiful in this remarkable life, and trust you to do the same with appropriate discernment.

The Story

The Merchant's Son Who Gave Everything Away

Assisi, 1206. Giovanni di Pietro di Bernardone — Francesco, as his father called him, for the France he loved — was the son of a prosperous cloth merchant, a young man who had pursued glory in battle, been captured, spent a year as a prisoner of war, and come home profoundly changed. He had begun giving away his father's cloth to the poor. His father was not amused.

The confrontation came publicly in the town square and then before the Bishop of Assisi: Pietro demanded his son return the money he had given away and renounce the inheritance. Francis stood up in front of the crowd, removed every item of clothing he was wearing — returned it to his father, including the clothes on his back — and said: "Until now I have called you father, but henceforth I desire to say only 'Our Father, who art in heaven.'" He walked out of Assisi naked. He never looked back.

He spent the next twenty years wandering Italy and eventually beyond — preaching, serving lepers whose wounds he dressed with his own hands, begging his daily bread, sleeping in the open, rebuilding ruined churches stone by stone, gathering around him a community of brothers who wanted to live as he lived. He called them the fratres minores — the little brothers. He called himself il poverello — the little poor man. When he died in 1226, at around forty-four, he was one of the most famous people in Europe. He had asked to be laid naked on the bare earth to die, so that nothing would come between him and the soil his Maker had made.

The Doctrine

Poverty as Protest — and as Freedom

Francis's embrace of poverty was not self-punishment. It was a theological statement — a bodily argument against the axiom that wealth is good and more wealth is better. The medieval church had become enormously wealthy, and the gap between the church's institutional comfort and its Lord's instruction to "sell what you possess and give to the poor" (Matthew 19:21) had been growing for centuries. Francis did not write treatises about this gap. He closed it with his body. He became poor, literally and deliberately, because he believed that the poor man from Nazareth was the truest image of God the world had ever seen, and that to follow him required following him into the economic condition he had chosen.

His relationship with creation — expressed most fully in the Canticle of the Sun, his hymn of praise that addresses Brother Sun, Sister Moon, Brother Wind, Sister Water — was equally theological. He did not worship nature. He worshipped nature's Maker, and because he worshipped the Maker, he could not look at any created thing without seeing a word God had spoken. The sun said something about God's glory. The moon said something about God's beauty. The worm in the road was worth stepping around because God had made it and called it good. This is not paganism or pantheism. It is the doctrine of creation pressed further than most of us press it — a reverence for the material world grounded precisely in the conviction that the God who made it entered it in the Incarnation and declared it worth inhabiting.

The Figure

Francis of Assisi (c. 1181–1226 AD)

Francis was not, by temperament, an intellectual. He distrusted learning that produced pride rather than charity, and he was sometimes in tension with the more institutionally ambitious members of his own order — who wanted to build schools and accumulate books and develop the Franciscans into a learned order capable of university teaching. He preferred the barefoot itinerant to the comfortable professor, even a professor of theology. His spirituality was concrete, immediate, embodied: not ideas about the poor but the actual poor person in front of him, with their actual wounds that needed dressing.

His mission to the Sultan of Egypt in 1219 — walking unarmed into the middle of the Crusades and requesting a meeting with the Muslim ruler Malik al-Kamil — is one of the most remarkable acts of courage in medieval history. He was not naive about the risks; he expected to be martyred. The Sultan received him, was apparently moved by him, and sent him home safely. The visit produced no political result. It remains, five centuries before any concept of interfaith dialogue existed, a witness to the conviction that the gospel must be spoken, not merely fought for.

Christ in Focus

Paul writes in 2 Corinthians 8:9: "For you know the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that though he was rich, yet for your sake he became poor, so that you by his poverty might become rich." Francis read this verse and took it at face value. Christ became poor — not metaphorically, not temporarily for rhetorical purposes, but actually, materially, deliberately poor — so that his poverty might accomplish something for those who received it. If that is true of Christ's poverty, Francis reasoned, then voluntary poverty in his followers is not self-punishment but participation: joining the pattern Christ established, sharing the condition he chose, making visible in human flesh the same logic the Incarnation embodied.

The lepers Francis served — touching wounds that made other people recoil, washing feet that no one else would approach — were for him what they were for Christ: the people at the bottom of every human ranking system, who were therefore the people at the top of God's priority list. "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven." Francis took the Beatitudes as a job description. He was, in that narrow and important sense, one of the most consistently literal readers of the Sermon on the Mount in the history of the church.

Then & Now

What He Got Right That We Keep Forgetting

The prosperity gospel — the teaching that God's blessing is primarily expressed through material wealth — is the precise theological inversion of everything Francis stood for. Where he treated voluntary poverty as a form of Christlikeness, prosperity theology treats wealth as the sign of divine favor. Where he saw the poor as the privileged recipients of God's attention, prosperity theology sees poverty as a spiritual problem to be overcome. The contrast is useful not because Francis was simply right about everything, but because his life forces the question that prosperity theology cannot comfortably answer: what do you do with the carpenter from Nazareth who had nowhere to lay his head?

His creation care also speaks to a contemporary conversation that is worth engaging carefully. Francis's love of the natural world was Christological — grounded in the doctrine of creation and the Incarnation, not in a spirituality that equates God with nature. The Christian tradition has robust resources for environmental ethics that do not require any fusion of Creator and creation: the world is God's work, entrusted to human stewardship, and its degradation is an offense against the Maker as well as the making. Francis embodied that tradition before most of the tradition had articulated it.

A Quote to Carry
"Praised be You, my Lord, with all your creatures, especially Sir Brother Sun, who is the day and through whom You give us light. And he is beautiful and radiant with great splendour; and bears a likeness of You, Most High."
— Francis of Assisi, Canticle of the Sun, c. 1224
Reflect

Francis closed the gap between his theology and his lifestyle with his body — not his words. What gap between what you believe and how you actually live is the Holy Spirit most persistently drawing your attention to?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for Francis — the merchant's son who stripped off everything and walked away free. Thank you for the witness of a life that took the Beatitudes literally and the poor seriously and the created world as your word spoken in matter. Forgive us for the gaps between our theology and our practice — for believing in simplicity while accumulating, for affirming the dignity of the poor while remaining comfortable at their distance. Give us something of his holy recklessness: to let what is true about you reshape what is true about how we live. Amen.
