Season VIII · Study 51 · The Whole World, Now

The Unfinished Letter

The Great Commission — What It Means That the Story Is Still Running

Great CommissionMatthew 28MissionUnfinished
Opening

A Letter Still Being Written

The New Testament ends with the church in motion. The book of Acts — which scholars note has no formal conclusion — simply stops mid-story, with Paul under house arrest in Rome, still preaching and still receiving visitors, "proclaiming the kingdom of God and teaching about the Lord Jesus Christ with all boldness and without hindrance." The last word of Acts in Greek is akōlutōs — unhindered. The letter stops. The story doesn't.

This is not an accident of editorial carelessness. It is the shape of the story itself. Luke, who wrote Acts, knows how the story ends: it ends when every tribe and tongue and people and nation has heard, when the last of the elect has been gathered, when the Lord who commanded the mission returns to complete it. That end has not come. The letter is still being written. The commission is still in force.

Matthew 28:18–20 — the Great Commission — is among the most familiar passages in the New Testament and among the most regularly misunderstood. Its force comes not from the command ("go") but from the authorization that grounds it: "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me." Jesus is not sending his disciples out on their own authority into a world that does not belong to them. He is sending them as his authorized representatives into a world that is already his — every nation, every culture, every unreached people group already under his sovereignty, already his by right. The mission is not a conquest. It is a disclosure — the announcement that the one who already owns everything is also willing to forgive and receive everyone who comes to him.

The Doctrine

The Commission: What It Actually Says

"Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age" (Matthew 28:19–20).

The main verb is not "go." In the Greek, "go" is a participle — "going," or "as you go." The main verb is "make disciples." This matters because it means the Commission is not primarily addressed to a special class of professional missionaries. It is addressed to the disciples wherever they go — in their ordinary lives, in their neighborhoods, in their workplaces, in the relationships they already inhabit. The going is assumed. The making of disciples is the command.

Discipleship — the word means a learner, a student, someone who is formed by the teaching and example of a master — is a comprehensive vision of what the mission produces. Not converts who have said a prayer and been enrolled on a membership list. Disciples: people being taught to observe everything Jesus commanded, formed in their whole life around his teaching and his presence. The Commission is not complete when someone makes an initial commitment. It is ongoing — the continuous work of teaching, forming, and accompanying people in the life of following Jesus.

The promise attached to the Commission — "I am with you always, to the end of the age" — is the most important clause in the passage. The mission is impossible by human means. Two thousand years of church history demonstrate this repeatedly: the most effective missionaries have been people who knew they could not do what they were attempting and did it anyway, trusting the presence of the one who promised to accompany them. Patrick went back to Ireland. Carey sailed for India. Tyndale translated in hiding. Wurmbrand preached in solitary confinement. The commission has always exceeded the capacity of those who carried it; the promise has always been that the one commanding it would also be carrying it.

The Measure of What Remains

How Far the Commission Has Come — and How Far It Has Left to Go

In 100 AD, Christianity existed primarily in a thin arc along the northern Mediterranean and in scattered communities in North Africa and Mesopotamia. By 500 AD it had spread across the Roman Empire, into Ethiopia and Armenia, and along the Silk Road toward Central Asia. By 1000 AD it had reached the British Isles, Scandinavia, and Eastern Europe. By 1500 AD it had survived the fall of Constantinople and the rise of Islam, and was about to be carried to the Americas by the Spanish and Portuguese. By 1800 AD Carey and the modern missionary movement were beginning to reach the vast populations of Asia and Africa that centuries of European Christianity had not. By 2000 AD, every nation on earth had some Christian presence, and the church was the single most geographically widespread institution in human history.

And yet the commission is not finished. The concept of "unreached people groups" — ethno-linguistic communities with no indigenous church and insufficient gospel witness to evangelize themselves without outside help — identifies approximately 7,000 such groups, representing roughly 3 billion people. Most are in the "10/40 Window" (the band of latitude between 10 and 40 degrees north, covering much of the Muslim, Hindu, and Buddhist heartlands of the world). The commission that has produced so much in two thousand years still has its largest unfinished chapters ahead of it.

Christ in Focus

The Great Commission is grounded in the resurrection. "All authority has been given to me" — this is the language of a king who has been vindicated, whose claim has been proved, whose victory is established. The risen Christ commands the mission from the position of demonstrated lordship. The cross that looked like defeat has been shown to be the instrument of precisely the victory it claimed to accomplish. The one sending the disciples out is not a visionary who died for his ideals. He is the Lord of creation who rose from the dead and is coming back.

This grounds the mission's hope in something more durable than human enthusiasm or institutional momentum. Movements die. Organizations fade. Enthusiasm ebbs. The church has produced all of these in its two-thousand-year history, and it has continued despite all of them because the commission runs on the authority and the presence of the one who gave it, not on the quality of those who received it. Peter, who denied him, preached at Pentecost. Thomas, who doubted, reached India. Saul, who murdered Christians, became their greatest missionary. The history of the commission is the history of God working through the least likely people in the least likely circumstances to accomplish purposes that exceed any human planner's imagination.

It is still happening. The story is still running. The letter is still unfinished. And the one who said "I am with you always, to the end of the age" has not changed his mind.

Then & Now

The Commission in Our Generation

The global church has more resources, more people, more communication technology, and more geographical access than at any point in its history. The Bible is available in more languages, to more people, more cheaply than in any previous generation. Wycliffe and SIL are working on translation projects in the remaining languages that lack Scripture. The Internet reaches places that missionaries and Bibles cannot. Satellite radio and television carry Christian teaching into countries closed to Christian workers.

At the same time, the Western church — which has historically provided the largest share of missionary sending and funding — is facing significant internal challenges: declining membership, theological drift, institutional exhaustion, and a cultural environment increasingly hostile to Christian public witness. The baton is being passed, in significant measure, to the Korean church, the Nigerian church, the Brazilian church, and the Chinese church — the very communities that were once the recipients of Western mission, now becoming its providers.

The Great Commission does not belong to any national or cultural expression of Christianity. It was given to disciples, and disciples are found now, as they were in 30 AD, in the most surprising places. The commission is not in danger. The question for each generation is not whether it will be completed — the one who gave it has promised it will be — but whether this generation will participate in the completing, or leave it entirely to others.

A Quote to Carry
"And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age."
— Matthew 28:20 — the promise that makes the command possible
Reflect

The Commission is addressed to disciples wherever they go — in ordinary life, not just on the mission field. In what specific relationships or contexts is Jesus sending you, right now, to make disciples? What would it look like to take that seriously this week?

Prayer

Lord Jesus, all authority in heaven and on earth is yours. The mission belongs to you. The outcomes belong to you. What you have asked of us is faithfulness — to go, to make disciples, to baptize, to teach, to trust your presence in the going. Give us that faithfulness. Give us the courage to say your name where it is unwelcome, the patience to stay where the work is slow, the humility to pass the baton when others can carry it further, and the confidence to begin again when we have failed. The letter is not finished. Let us write our chapter well. Amen.
