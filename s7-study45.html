God Wants You Rich

The Prosperity Gospel — Where It Came From, What It Teaches, and What It Costs

Prosperity GospelWord of FaithDark ChapterFalse Teaching
Opening

Why This Belongs in the Dark Chapters

The Crusades and the Inquisition belong to the distant past — painful to own, but safely historical. The prosperity gospel does not have that comfort. It is alive, growing, and currently reaching more people in more countries than almost any other form of Christianity on earth. This is the dark chapter happening in real time.

We include it here — alongside the Crusades and the Inquisition — because it represents a comparable structural failure: the substitution of human desire for God's revealed character, the use of Scripture to sanctify arrangements that benefit those who teach it at the expense of those who receive it, and the production of a "gospel" that is, at its core, not the gospel of the New Testament. The medieval church sold indulgences — certificates trading on spiritual currency to relieve earthly anxiety. The prosperity gospel sells a version of the same transaction in contemporary dress. The product has changed. The mechanism has not.

We approach it with grief rather than contempt. Many people who sit in prosperity gospel churches are genuinely hungry for God and genuinely want to trust him. Many who preach it are not cynics — they believe it, at least partly. The tragedy is real on both sides. The error must be named precisely because the people it is harming deserve better than to be left in it.

What Happened — and What It Teaches

The Origins, the Core Claims, and the Biblical Response

Where it came from. The prosperity gospel in its recognizable modern form grew from several 20th-century American currents. New Thought — a late 19th-century metaphysical movement holding that the mind can control physical reality, including health and material conditions, through positive thinking — provided the basic mental architecture. Pentecostalism provided the supernatural framework and the language of faith as operative power. The postwar American economic boom provided the cultural soil: a Christianity suited to a consumer economy that had come to treat material blessing as evidence of right relationship with God.

Key figures in its development include E.W. Kenyon (whose ideas influenced later prosperity teachers, though he predated the movement's full form), Oral Roberts (who made healing evangelism into a media phenomenon), Kenneth Hagin (whose "word of faith" theology became the theological backbone of the movement), Kenneth Copeland (who extended and systematized Hagin's teaching), and Joel Osteen (whose Your Best Life Now brought a softened version of the theology to an audience of tens of millions).

The core claims. The prosperity gospel in its full form teaches that God's will is for every believer to be physically healthy and financially wealthy; that poverty and sickness are always the result of insufficient faith or sin; that the believer can "claim" health and wealth by speaking them in faith; that giving to the ministry (specifically, the ministry of the prosperity preacher) activates a "hundredfold return" of God's blessing; and that Jesus's atoning death purchased not only forgiveness of sin but also physical health and material prosperity for believers in this life. Jesus, in this framework, was not poor — he was wealthy, and his poverty at the crucifixion was a temporary anomaly.

What the Bible actually says. The poverty of Jesus is not ambiguous: "The Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head" (Matthew 8:20). Paul's own testimony about his ministry is an explicit catalogue of suffering — shipwreck, imprisonment, beatings, hunger, exposure (2 Corinthians 11:23–28) — and he describes this as the normal pattern of apostolic ministry, not a failure of faith. The prosperity gospel's proof texts — particularly "by his wounds you are healed" (Isaiah 53:5, quoted in 1 Peter 2:24) and "I wish above all things that you may prosper and be in health" (3 John 2, the greeting formula of a personal letter) — require systematic misreading that ignores context, genre, and the unanimous testimony of the surrounding New Testament material.

The theological problem runs deeper than proof-texting. The prosperity gospel redefines the gospel itself. The New Testament's "good news" is the announcement that through Christ's death and resurrection, sinners are forgiven, reconciled to God, and given the Spirit as a deposit of the life to come. It is explicitly not the promise of earthly prosperity — Paul himself writes that "all who desire to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted" (2 Timothy 3:12), and Hebrews 11 explicitly lists faithful believers who "died without receiving the things promised" (Hebrews 11:13) as examples of faith, not failures of it. The prosperity gospel substitutes the currency of this age — money, health, success — for the currency of the age to come. That is not a minor adjustment. It is a different gospel.

Paul writes in Galatians 1:8–9: "Even if we or an angel from heaven should preach to you a gospel contrary to the one we preached to you, let him be accursed." This is among the strongest language in the New Testament. The prosperity gospel is a gospel contrary to the one Paul preached — not in minor details but in its fundamental account of what God has promised, what faith produces, and what the cross accomplished.

The Human Cost

What Happens to Real People

The prosperity gospel's impact on real people in real circumstances is not theoretical. In the United States, it has extracted enormous sums from poor communities — communities already financially vulnerable — on the promise of divine financial return that does not arrive. The "seed faith" principle — give to this ministry and God will return it tenfold — functions economically as a transfer of wealth from poor congregants to wealthy ministry organizations. The preachers grow rich. The congregants, when the promised return does not come, are told they lacked faith, which compounds material poverty with spiritual shame.

In sub-Saharan Africa — where the prosperity gospel has spread rapidly — the impact has been documented by African theologians and sociologists with particular urgency. The Nigerian prosperity gospel context, the Ghanaian "faith" churches, the Zimbabwean healing ministries — these operate in contexts of genuine material deprivation where the promise of divine prosperity is almost irresistibly attractive. The result, documented repeatedly, is financial exploitation of the poor by wealthy pastors operating without accountability, theological displacement of traditional African Christianity's more orthodox strands, and the specific cruelty of telling sick people that their illness is the result of insufficient faith or unconfessed sin.

This last point deserves its own emphasis. Telling a dying person that they are dying because they lack faith is not a minor pastoral error. It is a specific cruelty that compounds physical suffering with spiritual condemnation. It is the inverse of what Jesus did — he healed without demanding faith first, he restored without first requiring an explanation of the sick person's spiritual failures, and he explicitly corrected the assumption that suffering is always the result of sin ("Rabbi, who sinned, this man or his parents?" — "Neither," John 9:3). The prosperity gospel's response to suffering is the theology Jesus explicitly rejected.

Christ in Focus

Paul writes in Philippians 4:11–13: "I have learned, in whatever situation I am, to be content. I know how to be brought low, and I know how to abound. In any and every circumstance, I have learned the secret of facing plenty and hunger, abundance and need. I can do all things through him who strengthens me." This passage — specifically "I can do all things through him who strengthens me" — is one of the most frequently misused texts in the New Testament. It is quoted as a promise of supernatural achievement; it is in fact a statement about contentment in any circumstance, including poverty and hunger. The Christ who strengthens Paul in Philippians 4 strengthens him to be content in need, not to transcend need.

Jesus was, in the terms the prosperity gospel uses to evaluate things, a failure. He died poor, convicted as a criminal, abandoned by most of his followers, without property, title, or institutional position. The prosperity gospel can only make sense of this by claiming he was secretly wealthy — a claim with no textual support and all the textual evidence against it. The real Jesus — who had nowhere to lay his head, who was buried in a borrowed tomb, whose ministry was funded by a group of women (Luke 8:3), who said "blessed are the poor in spirit" and "woe to you who are rich" — is the Jesus the prosperity gospel cannot account for.

The cross is, precisely, the point where every prosperity theology collapses. God's will for his own Son, in the moment of greatest obedience, was suffering, abandonment, death. Not as a temporary anomaly to be quickly reversed, but as the appointed instrument of the world's redemption. "For the joy set before him, he endured the cross, scorning its shame" (Hebrews 12:2). The joy was on the other side of the cross, not a substitute for it. A gospel that promises to remove the cross from the life of the follower has removed the center of the faith.

Then & Now

What the Church Owes Those the Prosperity Gospel Has Harmed

The prosperity gospel is not primarily a theological problem. It is a pastoral problem with theological roots. Its primary victims are people who wanted to trust God, whose circumstances were hard, and who were told by teachers with apparent authority that if they gave enough and believed enough and spoke the right words with enough conviction, God would improve their circumstances. When the circumstances did not improve, or when sickness progressed, or when financial giving produced financial crisis rather than financial return, they were told the failure was their own. The damage — to faith, to finances, to the capacity to trust either God or the church — is real and substantial.

The orthodox church's responsibility to those people is not primarily to win an argument about theology. It is to offer the actual gospel — the announcement that Christ suffered with us and for us, that his love does not fluctuate with our financial giving or the quality of our positive confession, that the suffering he does not remove he enters, and that the resurrection he promises is real even when the current circumstances are not improved. That gospel — the costly grace Bonhoeffer described — is genuinely good news for people in hard circumstances in a way that the prosperity gospel, for all its promises, is not.

A Quote to Carry
"I have learned, in whatever situation I am, to be content... I can do all things through him who strengthens me."
— Philippians 4:11–13 — a verse about contentment in poverty, not transcendence of it
Reflect

The prosperity gospel is attractive because it promises what every human heart genuinely wants: security, health, provision. The question it cannot answer is: what does your faith look like when those things are not given? What is your honest answer to that question right now?

Prayer

Lord Jesus, you had nowhere to lay your head. You were buried in a borrowed tomb. You said blessed are the poor and woe to the rich and take up your cross. Forgive us — and forgive those who have taught in your name — for the times we have traded your actual gospel for one more palatable to human desire. Comfort those who have been told their suffering is their own fault. Give them the real you: not the Jesus who promises to remove the cross, but the Jesus who carries it with us, who was acquainted with grief, and who is making all things new. That is enough. That is more than enough. Amen.
