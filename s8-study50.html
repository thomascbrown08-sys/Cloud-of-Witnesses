Season VIII · Study 50 · Los Angeles & the World, 1906–present

A New Wind Blowing

Azusa Street, Global Pentecostalism, and the Movement That Surprised Everyone

Azusa StreetPentecostalismSeymour20th century
Opening

An Abandoned Stable on Azusa Street

Los Angeles, April 1906. William Joseph Seymour was a one-eyed Black preacher from Louisiana, the son of formerly enslaved parents, who had traveled to Los Angeles to preach at a small Holiness church — and been locked out of it when the pastor disagreed with his theology about the baptism of the Holy Spirit. He had no pulpit, no building, no money, and no institutional support of any kind. He began holding prayer meetings in the living room of a house on Bonnie Brae Street. The meetings grew, and on April 9, 1906, several people began speaking in tongues — a phenomenon that had been reported in scattered places through the 19th-century Holiness movement but had never produced a sustained public manifestation on this scale.

Within weeks the meetings had outgrown the house and moved to an abandoned stable at 312 Azusa Street. The building seated about 800 people; the meetings ran three times a day, seven days a week, for three years. News spread through word of mouth and through a newsletter Seymour produced, which was mailed free to whoever requested it. People came from across the country and eventually from around the world — returning home to reproduce what they had experienced, spreading the tongues-speaking, emotionally intense, racially integrated worship to every continent.

The Azusa Street Revival is the most commonly cited origin point of modern Pentecostalism. A century later, the movement it launched had grown to an estimated 600–700 million people — making it the fastest-growing religious movement in the history of the world. It had grown in places the early participants could not have imagined: rural Latin America, urban sub-Saharan Africa, South Korea, the Philippines, Guatemala, Brazil. The abandoned stable had become the seed of the largest new Christian movement since the Reformation.

The Doctrine

The Pentecostal Claim — and the Questions It Raises

The theological heart of Pentecostalism is a claim about the work of the Holy Spirit: specifically, that the spiritual gifts described in Acts 2 and 1 Corinthians 12–14 — particularly tongues, prophecy, healing, and related phenomena — are available and intended for the church in every generation, not only in the apostolic age. We examined the cessationist/continuationist debate in Season VI (Study 42). Here we focus not on the abstract debate but on the specific movement and what it has produced.

Classical Pentecostalism taught, following Charles Parham (who influenced Seymour), that speaking in tongues is the "initial evidence" of Spirit baptism — a distinct second blessing subsequent to conversion. This specific teaching has been the most disputed element of the Pentecostal theological package: most charismatic Christians who affirm continuing gifts do not hold that tongues is the required evidence of Spirit baptism. The broader Pentecostal/charismatic family today includes enormous theological diversity, from doctrinally rigorous Reformed charismatics to prosperity gospel Pentecostals — a range so wide that "Pentecostal/charismatic" functions more as a family resemblance than a precise doctrinal category.

What the movement has consistently emphasized, at its best, is the immediate presence and active power of the Holy Spirit in the life of the believer and the worshipping community — an emphasis the Western church had in many places lost, particularly as it absorbed Enlightenment assumptions about the orderliness of the natural world and the irrelevance of the miraculous to modern people. The Pentecostal movement's explosive growth in the Majority World — in communities where spiritual warfare, miraculous healing, and direct divine intervention are not philosophically inconvenient but existentially urgent — suggests that its emphasis on the Spirit's active power addresses something the more rationalistic Western Christianity was failing to address.

The Figure

William Joseph Seymour (1870–1922 AD)

Seymour's significance is not only religious but also sociological and racial. The Azusa Street meetings were remarkable in 1906 for being genuinely racially integrated — in Jim Crow Los Angeles, Black and white worshippers gathered together, sat together, and on occasion embraced each other. The Los Angeles Times, which covered the revival with a mixture of mockery and alarm, noted with evident bewilderment the racial mixing it witnessed. Frank Bartleman, an observer and participant, wrote: "The color line was washed away in the blood." This was not a minor social detail. In 1906 America, it was extraordinary. The revival that had racial integration at its center was being led by the son of formerly enslaved people, which is a reminder that the Spirit's work does not ask permission from the social hierarchies it disrupts.

The racial integration did not last. Within a few years Seymour's White collaborators had largely moved on to found separate organizations, and the institutional Pentecostal movement that developed from Azusa Street divided along racial lines like the rest of American Christianity. Seymour died in relative obscurity in 1922, having been largely eclipsed in the movement he had helped launch. The man who started the fire was left standing outside it — a grief that deserves to be named honestly, not minimized.

Christ in Focus

Acts 2:16–18, quoting Joel 2:28–29: "In the last days, God says, I will pour out my Spirit on all people. Your sons and daughters will prophesy, your young men will see visions, your old men will dream dreams. Even on my servants, both men and women, I will pour out my Spirit in those days, and they will prophesy." Peter's interpretation of what happened at Pentecost is that it is the fulfillment of Joel's vision of the "last days" — the age inaugurated by Christ's resurrection, the age in which the Spirit is poured out on "all people," without the ethnic, gender, or social distinctions that had previously governed prophetic endowment.

The Pentecostal movement, at its origin, was an attempt to take Acts 2 at face value — to live as if the "last days" were genuinely ongoing, the Spirit genuinely active, the gifts genuinely available. Whatever one's view of the cessation question, the instinct behind the movement points toward something real: the church can become so institutionally settled, so theologically tidy, so culturally comfortable that it loses its sense of living in the Spirit's present activity. The Azusa Street revival was, among other things, a protest against that domestication — an insistence that the wind blows where it will (John 3:8) and that the church's job is not to manage the wind but to open the windows.

Then & Now

The Movement and Its Open Questions

Global Pentecostalism is too large and too diverse to be assessed as a single thing. At its best, it represents the gospel of the living Christ proclaimed with urgency and received with joy in communities desperate for precisely the kind of hope it announces. The explosive growth of Pentecostal Christianity in sub-Saharan Africa, in Latin America, and in East Asia represents — whatever one's theology of gifts — a genuine work of the Spirit in gathering people to Christ who had not previously been reached by the more institutionally conventional forms of Christianity.

At its worst, the Pentecostal/charismatic world has produced some of the most egregious examples of the prosperity gospel (Study 45), the most manipulative uses of spiritual authority, and the most doctrinally thin Christianity currently being offered to large numbers of people. The same movement that produced the martyred Chinese house church pastor and the rigorous Korean prayer movement has also produced televangelists whose theology is demonstrably false and whose financial practices are demonstrably exploitative. The range is not incidental — it is structural, reflecting what happens when a movement grows faster than its theological roots can support.

The honest engagement with Pentecostalism requires holding both the genuine fruit and the genuine failure, applying the test Paul provides in 1 Corinthians: does it build up the church? Does it lead to the confession that Jesus is Lord? Is it marked by love — the gift that never ends? That test cuts through the denominational and theological debates to the question that matters: is the Spirit of Christ present in this, producing what the Spirit of Christ produces?

A Quote to Carry
"The color line was washed away in the blood."
— Frank Bartleman, eyewitness to the Azusa Street Revival, 1906
Reflect

The Azusa Street revival began in the humblest possible circumstances — an abandoned stable, led by the son of enslaved people, with no institutional backing — and produced the fastest-growing religious movement in history. What does that pattern suggest about where and how the Spirit tends to move?

Prayer

Holy Spirit, come. Come as you came at Pentecost — not on our terms, not in our preferred forms, not confined to our theological systems about when and how you may appear. Come to the abandoned stables and the living rooms and the basement meetings. Come to the communities that have stopped expecting you. Come to us. Pour out your gifts for the common good, produce your fruit in our lives, and lead us — all of us, across every tradition — to the confession that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father. Amen.
