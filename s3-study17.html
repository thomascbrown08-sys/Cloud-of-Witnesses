The Monk Who Couldn't Stop Shaking

Martin Luther and the Discovery That Changed the World

LutherJustificationReformation1483–1546 AD
Opening

The First Mass, and the Terror That Would Not Leave

May 2, 1507. Martin Luther — barely twenty-three years old, a newly ordained priest — stood at the altar in Erfurt to celebrate his first Mass. His father had ridden in from the mines with twenty horses and a party of friends to celebrate. Luther lifted the bread. And nearly dropped it. He later wrote that he had been so overwhelmed by the holiness of God — the terrible weight of standing in the presence of the eternal, immortal, all-powerful Creator, holding his body in trembling hands — that he could barely continue. He wanted to flee the altar.

This was not an isolated moment. Luther had entered the monastery two years earlier not out of gentle piety but out of mortal terror — caught in a thunderstorm, lightning striking the ground near him, he had screamed a promise to St. Anne to become a monk if he survived. In the monastery he threw himself into the ascetic life with a thoroughness that alarmed his superiors. He confessed for hours. He deprived himself of sleep and food. He tried, by the sheer force of religious effort, to become holy enough to stand before a holy God. And it never worked. No amount of confession was enough. No amount of penance covered what he felt. "I was a good monk," he wrote later, "and I kept the rule of my order so strictly that I may say that if ever a monk got to heaven by his monkery it was I." But he did not feel he was getting to heaven. He felt he was getting worse.

The breakthrough came not in a monastery cell but in a study. Reading Paul's letter to the Romans. The phrase that had always terrified him — "the righteousness of God" — suddenly opened like a door.

The Doctrine

Justified by Faith: What Luther Discovered in Romans

The phrase "the righteousness of God" had, for Luther, always carried the sense of God's own terrifying standard — the standard by which he judges sinners and finds them wanting. Romans 1:17, which says "the righteousness of God is revealed in the gospel, from faith to faith," had seemed to him a threat: God's righteous wrath revealed, demanding what sinners cannot provide.

Then he read it differently. The righteousness of God "revealed in the gospel" — could that mean something given rather than something demanded? Not the standard God holds us to, but the verdict God pronounces over us? He went back to the context. "The just shall live by faith." Not: the just will earn life by their performance. The just — those declared righteous — shall live, and the ground of that declaration is faith. Not their own righteousness, which they can never produce in sufficient quantity, but a righteousness that comes from outside them — the righteousness of Christ, credited to their account, received by faith, not earned by works.

He later wrote: "I felt that I was altogether born again and had entered paradise itself through open gates. There a totally other face of the entire Scripture showed itself to me." The doctrine is called justification by faith alone — not faith plus works, not faith plus penance, not faith plus whatever religious effort we can muster. Faith alone, in Christ alone, whose righteousness covers sinners entirely. The ground of standing before God is not what we have done or become, but what Christ has done and is.

This is not a license to live carelessly — Luther was emphatic about that, and Study 19 takes it up directly. But it is the end of the exhausting, always-failing project of trying to earn God's favor. The favor is already given. In Christ. For free.

The Figure

Martin Luther (1483–1546 AD)

Luther was not a serene figure. He was earthy, volatile, funny, sometimes brutal in his polemics, occasionally wrong in ways that had serious consequences — his vicious writings against the Jewish people are a stain on his legacy that must be named and cannot be excused. He was also brave with a courage that seems almost impossible to replicate: standing before the assembled power of the Holy Roman Empire at the Diet of Worms in 1521, asked to recant, and refusing. "Here I stand. I can do no other. God help me. Amen."

He translated the entire Bible into German — a translation of such linguistic quality that it effectively standardized the German language. He wrote catechisms that are still in use. He wrote hymns — "A Mighty Fortress Is Our God" remains one of the most sung pieces of music in Christian history. He married a former nun (Katharina von Bora) and demonstrated that a pastor's home could be a center of theological conversation, hospitality, and genuine domestic happiness. He was, in every dimension, enormous — the scope of his output, the scale of his impact, the depth of his flaws, and the height of his courage all larger than ordinary life.

Christ in Focus

Luther's great gift to the church was recovering something Jesus had said plainly and the system had buried: "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest" (Matthew 11:28). The medieval penitential system — confession, absolution, penance, indulgences, the whole elaborate machinery — was built on the premise that the labor and the heaviness had to be reduced before you could come. Luther discovered, in Romans, that you come with the labor and the heaviness, and Christ takes it.

He developed what he called a "theology of the cross" as opposed to a "theology of glory." A theology of glory looks for God in power, success, achievement, and religious performance. A theology of the cross finds God in weakness, failure, suffering, and the scandal of a crucified Savior. The place where God is most fully revealed is not the incense-filled cathedral at high Mass but the bloody hill outside Jerusalem, where the Son of God died as a criminal so that criminals could be declared righteous.

This is not pessimism. It is the most radical possible good news: the God who reveals himself in the cross is the God who has already absorbed the worst of what you are, so that you can receive the best of what he is. That exchange — your sin for his righteousness — Luther called the "great exchange," and it is the heartbeat of everything he ever wrote.

Then & Now

The Reformation That Would Not Stop

Luther nailed his 95 Theses to the door of the Castle Church in Wittenberg on October 31, 1517 — intending an academic debate about indulgences. Within two weeks, printed copies had spread across Germany. Within two years he was the most famous man in Europe. Within five years the Western church had split in a way it has never fully repaired. He did not plan any of this. He was, by his own account, astonished at the force of what he had released.

The Reformation's most important and lasting gift was not any specific doctrinal settlement — the Reformers disagreed vigorously among themselves about many things. Its gift was the insistence that the gospel is good news for ordinary sinners who cannot fix themselves: that justification is a declarative verdict, not a gradual improvement; that the righteousness required is Christ's righteousness, received by faith, not our own righteousness produced by effort. This is the claim that Luther's terrified, shaking hands at that first Mass could not grasp — and that Romans finally gave him.

The question Luther's discovery puts to every person in every generation is the same one it put to him: are you trying to earn your standing before God, or have you received it as a gift? The exhaustion of the first approach and the freedom of the second are still available to be discovered — in the same letter to the Romans where Luther found them.

A Quote to Carry
"I felt that I was altogether born again and had entered paradise itself through open gates."
— Martin Luther, describing his discovery in Romans, c. 1518 AD
Reflect

Luther spent years trying to earn what could only be received. Is there any place in your own faith where you are still trying to earn what God has already freely given?

Prayer

Father, thank you for the gift that Luther found in Romans — that righteousness is not achieved but received, not earned but credited, not the product of our effort but the fruit of Christ's. Forgive us for the ways we drift back toward a religion of performance — as though the cross were not enough, as though your verdict needed our supplement. Let us rest in what Christ has done: entirely, securely, today. And raise up in our generation voices willing to say, at whatever cost: here I stand on the gospel alone. Amen.
