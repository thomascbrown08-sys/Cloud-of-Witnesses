When the Church Went to War

The Crusades — Causes, Conduct, and What the Family Must Reckon With

CrusadesDark ChapterHoly WarMedieval Church
Opening

How to Enter a Dark Room

This study — and the three that follow it — is difficult in a specific way. We are not talking about what other people did wrong. We are talking about what the family did wrong. The Crusades were not carried out by pagans in opposition to Christianity. They were carried out under the sign of the cross, blessed by popes, preached by bishops, and joined by men who genuinely believed they were serving God. The failure belongs to the family.

We approach it the way a family approaches honest memory of its worst chapters: not to destroy the institution, not to use the past as a weapon in present arguments, but because a church that cannot name its own sins is a church that has not taken the gospel seriously enough to apply it to itself. "Judgment begins with the household of God" (1 Peter 4:17). That cuts both ways: God judges what is wrong in his own house, and his house is therefore capable of naming what is wrong without collapsing.

We will also resist the temptation of easy dismissal — pretending that the Crusades were simply the product of medieval barbarism with no connection to genuine Christian conviction, or that they were so obviously wrong that no serious person could have believed otherwise at the time. Both of those moves are a form of dishonesty. The people who went on Crusade were not uniquely evil. They were human beings in a specific historical moment making choices that we, at our comfortable distance, can see more clearly than they could. The distance does not absolve the choices. It should, however, produce more grief than contempt.

What Happened

The Crusades: Causes, Course, and Conduct

The Crusades were a series of military campaigns, sponsored by the papacy and the European nobility, primarily aimed at recovering Jerusalem and the Holy Land from Muslim control. The First Crusade was launched in 1095 by Pope Urban II, following a plea from the Byzantine Emperor Alexios I Komnenos, who needed military assistance against the Seljuk Turks. Urban preached the expedition at Clermont in November 1095, promising spiritual rewards — including what amounted to plenary indulgence — to those who took the cross.

The First Crusade (1096–1099) succeeded in capturing Jerusalem in July 1099. The accounts of what happened when the city fell are among the most disturbing passages in medieval history: the crusading army massacred a substantial portion of Jerusalem's population, including Muslims, Jews, and some Eastern Christians. The sources — both Christian and Muslim — agree on the basic facts, though they differ in the precise numbers. The massacre was celebrated in many Western sources as a sign of divine favor. It was not a regrettable excess by undisciplined soldiers. It was, in significant measure, an intentional act by leaders who had been told they were fighting a holy war.

Seven major Crusades followed over the next two centuries, with diminishing success and increasing internal contradiction. The Fourth Crusade (1202–1204) never reached the Holy Land — it was diverted, partly through Venetian commercial interests and partly through political opportunism, to the sack of Constantinople, the greatest Christian city in the East. The crusaders ransacked Orthodox churches, stole relics, installed a Latin patriarch, and inflicted damage on Eastern Christianity that has never been fully repaired. This was a Christian army attacking a Christian city. The shock and grief of the Eastern church, for whom the event remains a defining wound, is entirely legitimate.

The Northern Crusades brought military force against the pagan Baltic peoples and the Russian Orthodox church. The Albigensian Crusade (1209–1229) was directed against the Cathar heretics of southern France — a war against Christians, however heterodox, on Christian soil, resulting in a staggering loss of life and the effective destruction of Occitan culture. The Crusades were not a unified movement with a single character; they varied enormously. What they shared was the use of sanctified violence as a tool of ecclesiastical and political power, blessed by the church's highest authorities.

Figures: The Best and the Worst

Not Everyone Went Along

Pope Urban II launched the movement with a speech that mixed genuine religious conviction — he believed Jerusalem was the inheritance of Christendom — with shrewd political calculation about the papacy's role in European affairs. His responsibility for what followed is real, even if he could not have fully anticipated it.

Bernard of Clairvaux, the greatest preacher of the 12th century, threw himself into recruiting for the Second Crusade with a zeal that he later had cause to regret when the campaign ended in catastrophic failure. His willingness to use his spiritual authority to mobilize military violence is a sobering example of how even genuine holiness can be bent toward destructive ends by the assumptions of the surrounding culture.

Francis of Assisi — whom we met in Season V — walked unarmed into the Fifth Crusade's military lines in 1219 and met personally with Sultan al-Kamil, attempting to convert him through conversation rather than combat. He was not taken seriously by the crusading leadership. His witness stands as a sharp counter-testimony to the dominant mode of his era: the same gospel that was being preached at the point of a sword could also be preached at the cost of one's own life, in the spirit of the one who went to the cross without raising a sword in his defense.

Saladin, the Muslim ruler whose recapture of Jerusalem in 1187 sparked the Third Crusade, is remembered in both Muslim and Christian sources for his relative mercy toward the city's population — a striking contrast to the First Crusade's massacre, not lost on contemporaries on either side.

Christ in Focus

At Gethsemane, when the soldiers came to arrest Jesus, Peter drew a sword and cut off the ear of the high priest's servant. Jesus told him to put it away: "All who take the sword will perish by the sword" (Matthew 26:52). Then he healed the ear. The one who could have called twelve legions of angels to his defense chose a different path — the path to the cross, through submission, not resistance.

The crusading theology that developed in the 11th century had to do significant work to get around this. It drew on Augustine's just war theory (which Augustine had developed cautiously and for defensive purposes) and on the Old Testament's holy war traditions, and it produced a vision of sanctified violence in defense of sacred space. The theology was not purely cynical — it was worked out by serious people with genuine convictions. But it required setting aside the specific and very clear word of the one in whose name the Crusades were fought.

Jesus said: "My kingdom is not of this world. If my kingdom were of this world, my servants would have been fighting" (John 18:36). The city of Jerusalem — whatever its symbolic weight — is not the kingdom of God. The church has never been given a mandate to conquer territory. Its mission is to proclaim, to love, to suffer if necessary, and to trust the God who governs history to accomplish his purposes through those means. The Crusades stand as the clearest possible example of what happens when the church forgets this and reaches for the sword instead.

Then & Now

The Long Shadow and the Necessary Reckoning

The Crusades' shadow falls unevenly across the present. In the Muslim world, the memory of the Crusades — particularly the First Crusade's massacre at Jerusalem — has been kept alive as a defining encounter between Christianity and Islam, and it shapes relationships between those traditions in ways that Western Christians often underestimate. When jihadist movements in the 20th and 21st centuries describe Western military interventions as "new Crusades," they are drawing on a memory that is, from within that tradition, entirely rational to maintain.

In Eastern Christianity — Orthodox churches — the sack of Constantinople in 1204 remains an open wound. The theological and cultural damage of the Fourth Crusade contributed directly to the conditions that made the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople in 1453 possible, and it produced a deep suspicion of Western Christianity that the Great Schism of 1054 had begun and the Crusades deepened. Genuine ecumenical dialogue between Catholic/Protestant and Orthodox traditions must reckon honestly with this history.

For the Western church, the honest lesson is structural rather than merely historical: the Crusades show what happens when the church becomes so entangled with political and military power that it loses the capacity to say no to that power. Every subsequent generation of the church has faced a version of the same temptation — to use the available instruments of coercive force to advance or protect Christianity — and the Crusades are the clearest possible warning about where that road leads.

Pope John Paul II issued an apology for the Crusades in 2000, as part of a broader "purification of memory" for the Great Jubilee year. It was received variously: some found it insufficient, some found it historically imprecise, some found it genuinely courageous. What it acknowledged — that the church had done things in the name of Christ that were incompatible with Christ's own teaching and spirit — is the honest starting point for any Christian engagement with this history.

A Quote to Carry
"God wills it."
— The crowd's response at Clermont, 1095, to Urban II's call for the First Crusade — words that echo with a terrible irony across nine centuries of consequences
Reflect

The people who joined the Crusades were not uniquely evil — they were ordinary people shaped by their cultural moment who genuinely believed they were serving God. What in your own cultural moment might your descendants grieve in the same way? What would it take to see it now rather than only in retrospect?

Prayer

Lord Jesus, we grieve what was done in your name by those who forgot your name's meaning. We grieve the blood shed at Jerusalem in 1099, the sack of Constantinople, the wars carried out under your cross against those you died to reach. Forgive your church — and forgive us for the ways we too are tempted to reach for power rather than the cross, for coercion rather than proclamation, for the sword rather than the word. Teach us again that your kingdom is not of this world, and that it advances by the means you chose: proclamation, love, and if necessary, suffering. Amen.
