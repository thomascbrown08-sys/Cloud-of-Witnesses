Season III · Study 21 · Zurich, Switzerland, 16th century

The Fire and the Hammer

Ulrich Zwingli and the Price of Taking Convictions to Their Conclusions

ZwingliSwiss ReformationMarburg1484–1531 AD
Opening

The Table That Divided the Reformation

Marburg, October 1529. The two greatest living leaders of the Protestant Reformation sat across a table from each other, and the fate of a unified Protestant movement hung in the air. Philip of Hesse, the Protestant prince who had arranged the colloquy, desperately wanted the Lutheran and Swiss Reformed wings of the Reformation to forge a political and theological alliance. Luther and Zwingli had been lobbing pamphlets at each other for years. This was the chance to reconcile in person.

They agreed on fourteen of the fifteen articles put before them. Fourteen. Then they came to the Lord's Supper. Luther took a piece of chalk and wrote on the table in German: Hoc est corpus meum. This is my body. He believed Christ was truly, corporally present in the bread and wine of communion — not in the Catholic sense of transubstantiation, but really, genuinely, physically present. Zwingli believed the words were figurative — "this represents my body" — and that Christ's presence at the table was spiritual and symbolic, not physical. His body, ascended to the Father's right hand, was in heaven, not in bread.

They argued for three days. They could not agree. When Zwingli, moved — some witnesses said he was weeping — reached across to offer Luther his hand as a brother in Christ, Luther refused it. He would not recognize as a brother someone who held what he considered a fundamental error about the sacrament. Philip of Hesse's dream of Protestant unity died in that room. The split between Lutheran and Reformed Protestantism that began at Marburg has never been fully healed. Two years later, Zwingli was dead — killed on a battlefield, bearing arms as a military chaplain, in a religious war partly of his own making.

The Doctrine

What the Reformation Agreed On — and Where It Fractured

Zwingli's great contribution to the Reformation was the Swiss reform of Zurich, which he accomplished with extraordinary speed and thoroughness between 1519 and his death in 1531. He arrived at his Reformation convictions independently of Luther — reading the Greek New Testament directly, preaching through Matthew chapter by chapter beginning on January 1, 1519, and letting Scripture's plain meaning challenge everything he found incompatible with it. Like Luther, he rejected justification by works, affirmed Scripture's supreme authority, and dismantled the Roman sacramental system. Like Luther, he was personally courageous to a degree that left bystanders astonished.

Where he differed from Luther, and where his influence on the Reformed tradition that Calvin would later develop was decisive, was in his approach to the relationship between Scripture and church practice. Luther's principle was that what Scripture does not forbid, the church may retain. Zwingli's was stricter: what Scripture does not explicitly authorize, the church should remove. This produced a leaner, more austere worship and church life in Zurich than in Wittenberg — no images, no organs, no choral music in worship, a simple commemorative Lord's Supper. Whether Zwingli's principle or Luther's is correct remains a live question within Protestantism, and the two traditions it generated are still recognizable today.

The Lord's Supper dispute deserves honest treatment. The question of what Jesus meant at the Last Supper — "this is my body" — is one on which Lutherans, Reformed, Catholics, and Orthodox all disagree, and the disagreement is not trivial. It touches the nature of Christ's presence, the relationship between physical and spiritual realities, and the meaning of the Incarnation. Season VI will explore the Lord's Supper question further. Here it is enough to note that both Luther and Zwingli were reading the same text with genuine commitment to its authority and came to opposite conclusions — a reminder that sola Scriptura does not automatically produce uniform interpretation, and that the church's ongoing task of reading together, in community, across tradition, is not optional.

The Figure

Ulrich Zwingli (1484–1531 AD)

Zwingli was the contemporary of Luther and Calvin but preceded Calvin in Zurich — making the Swiss city a center of Reformed thought even before Geneva assumed that role. He was, like Luther, a product of humanist education — he knew Erasmus and shared his passion for the Greek New Testament. Unlike Luther, his breakthrough was less dramatically personal: he came to his convictions through patient biblical study rather than a single transforming insight. He was also a man of his political world in ways that eventually killed him.

His death on the battlefield at Kappel on October 11, 1531, requires honest acknowledgment. Zwingli had pushed for a military confrontation between the Protestant cantons of Switzerland and the Catholic cantons that proved catastrophically ill-advised. He died as a military chaplain bearing arms — a posture most of his Reformation contemporaries, including Calvin, later explicitly rejected as a confusion of the church's calling with the state's. His death was the consequence of taking a certain kind of political theology further than it should have gone. He had helped provoke a war his side lost. This is part of his story, and it matters as a caution about the ways that genuine convictions, pressed without sufficient wisdom, can lead reformers to places they should not have gone.

He died at forty-seven, his career unfinished, his influence enormous. Heinrich Bullinger, his successor in Zurich, carried the Swiss Reformed tradition forward and eventually helped broker the agreement (the Consensus Tigurinus) between Zurich and Geneva that gave the Reformed world a unified theological voice. What Zwingli began, Bullinger and Calvin completed.

Christ in Focus

For all their differences about the Lord's Supper, Luther and Zwingli were agreed on the thing that mattered most: the one whose body and blood the table commemorates is the crucified and risen Christ, who justifies sinners by grace through faith. Their disagreement was about the mode of his presence at the table, not about his person, his work, or the gospel his death accomplished.

That matters. The Reformation was not primarily a fight about sacramental theology or church governance — those were real debates, and they had real consequences. The Reformation's heart was the recovery of the gospel: that Jesus Christ, the eternal Son made flesh, died for sinners and rose victorious, and that all who trust in him are declared righteous before God. On that, Luther and Zwingli, Calvin and Arminius, the Lutheran and Reformed and Anabaptist traditions all stood together. The center held, even when the edges were disputed.

The Marburg table, where Luther and Zwingli could not agree, also points forward to another table — the one where every tribe and tongue and tradition will gather in the presence of the one whose body was broken for all of them. The eucharistic disagreements of the 16th century will not divide the eternal feast. The Lamb who was slain is the host, and his invitation does not require that his guests first resolve every question about how he is present. He is present. That is the beginning and the end of it.

Then & Now

The Reformation's Unfinished Business

The fracture at Marburg between the Lutheran and Reformed traditions was never formally repaired, though the 20th century produced significant ecumenical dialogue and the Leuenberg Agreement (1973) declared a fellowship in pulpit and table between European Lutheran and Reformed churches. The Lord's Supper question also remains one of the most significant points of division between Protestants and Catholics and between Protestants and the Eastern Orthodox. Christians who share a common faith in the crucified and risen Christ cannot always share the table that commemorates him — a grief that should make us more, not less, eager for the unity Jesus prayed for in John 17.

Zwingli's story also carries a warning about the danger of too closely identifying any human program — political, ecclesiastical, or cultural — with the cause of Christ. The Reformation's greatest temptations have always been versions of what Zwingli succumbed to: the conviction that the kingdom of God required a particular political arrangement enforced by the power available to those who believed in it. That path does not end at Kappel alone. It has ended badly in every century it has been tried.

The Reformation's greatest gift, in the end, was not a political program but a theological recovery: the announcement that sinners are justified by grace through faith in Christ alone, the Scripture that declares it placed in ordinary hands, and the Spirit who uses that Scripture to generate a living faith that no army and no institution can finally suppress. That gift outlived Zwingli's death, outlived the fractures at Marburg, and outlives every generation's partial mishandling of it. It will outlive ours too — and be handed on.

A Quote to Carry
"The word of God will take its course as surely as the Rhine, which can be stemmed for a moment but not for long."
— Ulrich Zwingli, c. 1520 AD
Reflect

Luther and Zwingli agreed on fourteen of fifteen articles and still could not call each other brothers. How do you hold the tension between taking doctrinal disagreements seriously and maintaining genuine fellowship with those who share the gospel but differ on secondary matters?

Prayer

Lord, we thank you for the Reformers — their courage, their scholarship, their willingness to lose everything rather than soften what they believed Scripture plainly said. We grieve the fractures they left behind and confess our own share in the church's ongoing divisions. Give us their clarity on what is first and non-negotiable — the gospel of grace alone, through faith alone, in Christ alone — and their passion for your word. And give us what they sometimes lacked: the humility to hold our secondary convictions with open hands, and the love to call brothers those who share our Savior even when they disagree with our systems. Amen.
