Chosen or Choosing?

Election and Human Freedom — Going Deeper into the Oldest Argument

ElectionPredestinationFree WillTULIPSecond-Order
Opening

Back to the Oldest Question in Soteriology

We touched this question in Study 20 — the overview of Arminius and Wesley alongside the Reformed tradition. Here we go deeper: into the specific contours of what each position claims, the biblical texts that most press each side, and the pastoral and theological stakes that make this debate feel so important to those most invested in it. We approach it the same way we did before: not to adjudicate the mechanism the Bible never fully systematizes, but to understand both sides from their own best arguments — and to hold them charitably, as the family disagreement this is, rather than the first-order division it is sometimes treated as.

A preliminary note: the acronym TULIP — Total depravity, Unconditional election, Limited atonement, Irresistible grace, Perseverance of the saints — summarizes the five points affirmed at the Synod of Dort (1618–19) in response to the Arminian Remonstrance. It is a useful shorthand that sometimes misleads. The "five points" were Dort's response to five specific Arminian objections; they were never intended as a summary of the whole of Calvinist theology. A Calvinist who knows only the five points may know less of Calvinist theology than one who has read Calvin's Institutes, and the caricature of Calvinism as a cold, mechanistic system of divine puppetry owes more to the acronym than to the tradition.

The Debate, Going Deeper

Key Texts and What Each Side Does With Them

Romans 9:10–23 — Jacob and Esau chosen before birth, before either had done good or evil. "So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy." The potter and the clay. This is the central Reformed text, and it is genuinely difficult to read without hearing something like unconditional election. Arminians respond: Paul is speaking of national election (Israel as a people) rather than individual election to salvation; or he is describing God's foreknowledge of individual faith. The Reformed response: the context is about individual destinies, not merely national roles; and "foreknowledge" in Paul typically means a foreloved relationship, not mere prescience.

2 Peter 3:9 — "The Lord is not slow to fulfill his promise as some count slowness, but is patient toward you, not wishing that any should perish, but that all should come to repentance." This is the central Arminian text. If God genuinely does not wish any to perish, how can he unconditionally elect only some? Calvinists respond: "any" and "all" refer to all types of people, or to all the elect; or God has different levels of "willing" — his revealed will wishes all to repent; his secret will determines who will. Arminians respond: this distinction makes the revealed gospel offer disingenuous — if God secretly wills the damnation of those he outwardly invites to repent, what kind of invitation is that?

John 6:37–44 — Both sides in one passage. "All that the Father gives me will come to me" (v.37a — Calvinist) "and whoever comes to me I will never drive away" (v.37b — Arminian). "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him" (v.44 — Calvinist). The same Jesus who says "no one can come unless drawn" also says "whoever comes I will not drive away." Both sentences are emphatic. Neither qualifies the other. Jesus appears to be affirming both divine sovereignty in drawing and genuine human coming, without explaining how they relate.

The "Limited Atonement" question is the most contested of the five points, and the one where Calvinist disagreement within the Reformed camp is most significant. Did Christ die to actually secure the salvation of the elect (particular redemption), or to make salvation possible for all while securing it for none unless they believe (general/hypothetical universalism)? The texts press in both directions: John 10:11 ("the good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep" — his specific sheep), and 1 John 2:2 ("he is the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world"). "Four-point Calvinists" (who affirm TULIP without the L, or reframe it significantly) are not rare within the Reformed tradition.

What both sides agree on: Human beings are sinners who cannot save themselves. Salvation is entirely by grace. Faith is the means of receiving salvation. The Spirit's work is essential for any genuine response to the gospel. God is sovereign over all things. Human beings are genuinely responsible for their choices. The gospel must be proclaimed to all people without qualification. Hell is real and avoidable. Heaven is real and entered through Christ alone. The vast majority of what matters is shared.

The Shared Pastoral Concern

What Both Sides Are Trying to Protect

Reformed theology's deepest pastoral concern is assurance: if my salvation ultimately depends on the stability of my faith, and my faith fluctuates (as all faith does), how can I ever be certain I am truly saved? The Calvinist answer — grounded in unconditional election and the perseverance of the saints — is: your security rests not on the quality of your faith but on the character of the God who chose you before you existed. "I am sure that neither death nor life... will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord" (Romans 8:38–39). That is not a conditional promise.

Arminian theology's deepest pastoral concern is the genuineness of the gospel offer: when the evangelist says "whoever believes in him will not perish," is that offer genuinely open to everyone in the room, or only to the secretly elect? If God has already determined that certain people in the room cannot come, is the invitation honest? The Arminian answer — grounded in prevenient grace and the universal love of God — is: the offer is genuinely open to everyone, which means it can be genuinely proclaimed to everyone without mental reservation. "Whosoever will may come" is not a coded message with hidden exceptions.

Notice that both concerns are pastoral before they are theoretical. Both are trying to preach the gospel faithfully — one to people who need certainty, one to people who need openness. The God of the New Testament appears to want both preached, which may be why the New Testament contains both emphases without fully resolving the tension between them.

Christ in Focus

Whatever position we hold on election, every tradition agrees that Jesus Christ is the one in whom election is grounded, secured, and received. Paul writes in Ephesians 1:4 that God "chose us in him before the foundation of the world." The election is in Christ — which means that the question "am I elect?" is not answered by introspection (do I feel elect enough?) but by the same question that settles every other question in the Christian life: am I in Christ? Have I believed in him? Am I trusting him?

Calvin's practical instruction on the question of assurance was consistent and pastoral: do not try to determine whether you are elect by examining the hidden decrees of God — you cannot see them. Look to Christ. He said "whoever comes to me I will never drive away." Go to him. Come to him. Trust him. If you have come, you are among those given to him by the Father. The assurance is found at the cross and in the risen Christ, not in the attempt to peer behind the curtain of divine election.

That instruction is one that both Calvinist and Arminian can give without remainder. Come to Christ. Trust Christ. Abide in Christ. Whatever the mechanism behind the invitation, the invitation itself is genuine, the promise is unconditional, and the Christ who makes it is faithful.

What Holds the Family Together

The Mystery the Bible Preserves Intact

The New Testament holds divine sovereignty and human responsibility together in a way that resists the complete systematization either tradition attempts. Jesus says "no one can come to me unless the Father draws him" in the same breath as "whoever comes to me I will not drive away." Paul says "work out your own salvation with fear and trembling" in the same breath as "it is God who works in you." The tension is not a contradiction to be resolved — it may be a mystery to be inhabited.

The Christian tradition has generally held that when Scripture teaches both divine sovereignty and human responsibility as real without fully explaining how they coexist, the appropriate response is to preach both — not to reduce one to the other for the sake of a tidy system. The Calvinist preaches "God chose you in Christ before the foundation of the world" and calls people to repentance and faith. The Arminian preaches "God loves you and Christ died for you" and calls people to repentance and faith. Both are preaching the gospel. Both are right to do so. The preaching is the means God has appointed; the outcomes are his.

A Quote to Carry
"Work out your own salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you, both to will and to work for his good pleasure."
— Philippians 2:12–13 (both sides of the tension in two verses)
Reflect

Both sides of this debate are trying to protect something pastorally important — Calvinists want assurance grounded in God's character, Arminians want a genuinely open invitation to all. Which of those pastoral concerns do you most need to hear right now, and what does that tell you?

Prayer

Lord, thank you that you are bigger than our systems about you — that the New Testament holds divine sovereignty and human freedom together without fully resolving the tension, and that this is probably a mercy rather than an oversight. Thank you for Calvin and Arminius, for Luther and Wesley, who were all trying to preach the same gospel faithfully and arrived at different accounts of its mechanics. Guard us from the arrogance that substitutes our preferred systematic theology for the mystery of your governance. And keep us coming — always coming — to the Christ who said "whoever comes to me I will never drive away." Amen.
