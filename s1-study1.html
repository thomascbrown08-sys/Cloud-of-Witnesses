The Man They Couldn't Define Away

Athanasius of Alexandria and the Battle for the Eternal Son

TrinityNicaeaAlexandriac. 296–373 AD
Opening

A Deacon in the Back Row Who Wouldn't Stay Quiet

Alexandria, 325 AD. Hundreds of bishops filed into the great hall at Nicaea — some still bearing the scars of the last Roman persecution — to settle a question threatening to tear the church apart. The Emperor Constantine had called them. A popular Alexandrian priest named Arius had been teaching something that sounded almost reasonable: Jesus was the greatest being God ever created. Glorious, divine-like, exalted above all others — but created. Not eternal. Not quite God.

It had a tidy logic to it. It appealed to the Greek philosophical mind. Arius had powerful friends. The political winds were behind him. And yet in the back of the room sat a young deacon named Athanasius — too junior even to vote — who kept pushing back against bishops twice his age with a calm, unyielding insistence: the Son of God has no beginning.

Nicaea sided with Athanasius. The Creed was written. And it should have been over. It wasn't. Over the next five decades, emperors came and went — most of them Arian sympathizers — and Athanasius was exiled five separate times, once by force when imperial soldiers surrounded the cathedral in Alexandria and ejected him. His enemies coined a phrase they meant as mockery: Athanasius contra mundum. Athanasius against the world. He wore it like a badge. He died in his own bed in 373 AD, still Bishop of Alexandria, having outlasted four emperors and every one of his opponents.

The Doctrine

Why "Fully God" Is Not Negotiable

Arius's position seems almost reasonable — until you follow it to its conclusion. If the Son of God is a created being, however magnificent, then when Jesus died on the cross, a creature died for other creatures. The debt goes unpaid. A created being cannot bear the infinite weight of sin against an infinite God, and cannot give what only God possesses: eternal, resurrection life.

The theological term is the ontological necessity of the Incarnation — which simply means that for salvation to work, the Savior must actually be God. Not God-adjacent. Not God-lite. The eternal, second Person of the Trinity stepping into full humanity: both natures together, so that the bridge between God and us can hold the weight of what needs to cross.

The Nicene Creed captures the crucial distinction with a single Greek word: homoousios — the Son is "of the same substance" as the Father. Arius's followers proposed homoiousios — of "similar" substance. One letter. In Greek, an iota. The difference between a Savior and a very impressive man. The phrase "not one iota of difference" entered the English language from this controversy — because it turned out that iota made all the difference in the world.

The Figure

Athanasius of Alexandria (c. 296–373 AD)

Athanasius was born and educated in Alexandria, Egypt — one of the ancient world's great intellectual capitals — and became Bishop of Alexandria in 328 AD at around thirty years old. What made him remarkable was not merely that he was right, but that he held his position across fifty years of sustained institutional pressure without becoming bitter or hardened.

During his exiles he wrote prolifically — treatises, pastoral letters, the biography of Anthony of Egypt that introduced monasticism to the Western church. He built relationships with desert monks who lived beyond the reach of imperial politics. He understood that the fight over a Greek word was a fight for the identity of Jesus — and therefore a fight for whether salvation was genuinely possible at all. Theology, for Athanasius, was never merely academic. It was always pastoral.

Christ in Focus

The Gospels hold two kinds of scenes in stunning tension. Jesus gets tired and asks for water at a well. Jesus commands the storm to stop — and it does. Jesus weeps at Lazarus's tomb. Jesus commands Lazarus back from the dead. Jesus cries out from the cross, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" Jesus, three days later, walks out of that same tomb.

Athanasius understood that you cannot strip away either set of scenes without destroying the person entirely — and with the person, the mission. If Jesus is not fully God, the resurrection is not a real victory over death. If he is not fully human, his suffering does not reach us where we actually live. Athanasius spent his life insisting on both, because he understood that the whole point of the Incarnation was to close the gap between a holy God and broken humanity — and only a Jesus who is genuinely both can do that.

As he wrote: "The Son of God became the Son of Man so that the sons of men might become sons of God." That is not a theological formula. It is the deepest possible good news.

Then & Now

Arianism Did Not Die at Nicaea

It mutated. It resurfaced throughout the medieval period, in Enlightenment-era "low Christology," and in explicitly modern forms: Jehovah's Witnesses teach a version of Arian theology today, holding that Jesus is the greatest created being but not co-eternal with the Father. The question Arius raised has never stopped being asked.

The Nicene Creed recited every Sunday in Catholic, Orthodox, Anglican, Lutheran, and many other Protestant churches is, quite literally, Athanasius's answer still being given — sixteen centuries later — to that same question. When you say "God from God, Light from Light, true God from true God, begotten not made, of one substance with the Father," you are standing with the young deacon in the back row of the hall at Nicaea.

The Incarnation — God himself taking on human flesh — also became the most powerful theological argument in history for human dignity. If God entered human physicality and declared it worth inhabiting, then human beings carry an inherent worth that no emperor, no ideology, no system of oppression can legitimately override. The Athanasian settlement was not just a church matter. It was a civilization matter.

A Quote to Carry
"The Son of God became the Son of Man so that the sons of men might become sons of God."
— Athanasius of Alexandria
Reflect

If Jesus were not fully God but only a very exalted created being, what would that do to your confidence when you pray? What would it change about the cross?

Prayer

Father, thank you that you did not send a messenger — you came yourself. Thank you for a Jesus who is not merely like you, but is you, clothed in human flesh. Give us something of Athanasius's courage: to hold to what is true not from stubbornness, but from the deep security of knowing who Jesus really is. And in a world full of versions of Jesus that are smaller than the real one, keep us anchored to the one the Scriptures reveal — the eternal Son, the true Savior, the Lord of all. Amen.
