Follow Me Into the Dark

Hudson Taylor and the Mission That Went Inland

Hudson TaylorChina Inland MissionIdentification1832–1905 AD
Opening

The Man Who Put On Chinese Clothes

London, 1853. James Hudson Taylor was twenty-one years old, already studying medicine in preparation for China, already learning Mandarin in his spare moments, when he began a spiritual experiment that would later define his mission strategy. He stopped cutting his hair in the Western style and let it grow, intending to eventually dye it black and adopt a Chinese queue — the long braid worn by Chinese men under the Qing dynasty. He knew that Chinese people associated Western dress with foreign imperialism and military aggression. He wanted to arrive in China looking, as far as possible, like someone who had come to join the Chinese rather than to improve them.

His colleagues thought he was eccentric. His supporters were sometimes embarrassed. The mission societies of the day operated on the principle that the missionary's Western identity was part of their authority — that the prestige of British civilization reinforced rather than hindered the gospel's reception. Taylor thought they had it exactly backward. He arrived in China in 1854, dressed in Chinese clothes, and spent the next fifty years moving deeper and deeper inland — past the coastal treaty ports where Western missionaries clustered, past the cities that had some Western presence, into the provinces of the interior where no missionary had ever been and where the sight of a Western face in any form would have ended every conversation before it began.

In 1865, on a beach at Brighton, England, after a period of spiritual crisis, he formed the China Inland Mission on a radically different model from every existing mission society: interdenominational, entirely faith-funded (no guaranteed salaries, no appeals for money — trusting God to provide), and committed to reaching the inland provinces that coastal missions were not reaching. He recruited missionaries from across denominational lines. He sent women as well as men. He insisted that every worker adopt Chinese dress and customs. He called the mission's workers to go where no one else would go.

The Doctrine

Faith Mission — and What It Actually Means

The China Inland Mission's founding principle — no guaranteed salaries, no appeals for money, trust God to provide — was not a gimmick. It was a theological statement about the nature of mission and the character of God. Taylor had been shaped by the ministry of George Müller, the Bristol orphanage director who had built and run institutions serving thousands of children for decades without ever making a public appeal for funds, relying entirely on prayer. Taylor extended the same principle to missions.

His argument: if God has called workers to a field, he will provide for them. Making that provision dependent on the donors' awareness of the need — through public appeals and fundraising campaigns — was, in Taylor's view, a subtle transfer of trust from God to human enthusiasm. It also meant that the least-known fields, with the least-compelling stories, received the least support — which meant exactly the places most in need were least likely to be reached. Trusting God to provide, without manipulation, meant that the provision followed the calling rather than the publicity.

This model — the "faith mission" approach — shaped the entire landscape of 20th-century Protestant missions. Wycliffe Bible Translators, OM (Operation Mobilisation), YWAM, and dozens of other major mission organizations operate on principles Taylor established. The CIM itself, now OMF International, continues to work across East Asia.

The Figure

Hudson Taylor (1832–1905 AD)

Taylor's life in China was marked by tremendous fruitfulness and tremendous loss. His first wife, Maria Dyer, died in 1870 at thirty-three, leaving him with four children. Two of those children had already died in China; two more would die there. He eventually remarried, and his second wife died in China too. He lost colleagues to disease and violence. The Boxer Rebellion of 1900 killed fifty-eight CIM missionaries and twenty-one of their children. Taylor, by then elderly and in failing health, received the news at his home in Switzerland where he had gone to recover. He died in Changsha, China, in 1905 — exactly where he wanted to die, in the country he had given his life to.

He is sometimes quoted: "All God's giants have been weak men who did great things for God because they reckoned on God being with them." He believed this about himself. He described his spiritual life as an ongoing experience of inadequacy regularly rescued by divine sufficiency — a pattern he learned not from theory but from the kind of circumstances that make theory irrelevant. His interior spiritual life, reflected in his letters and journals, is among the most honest and searching in the missionary literature of any era.

Christ in Focus

Taylor's famous phrase — "Christ is either Lord of all, or he is not Lord at all" — is more than a missions slogan. It is a statement about the scope of the Incarnation and the nature of Christ's claim. If the risen Christ has been given "all authority in heaven and on earth," then the inland provinces of China are as much under his authority as the drawing rooms of London, and the obligation to bring his name to those provinces is as strong as the obligation to maintain the missions already established on the coast.

Taylor's identification with Chinese culture also expressed a conviction about the nature of Christ's own mission. Jesus did not come to humanity in general — he came to a specific people in a specific time and place, learning their stories, using their idioms, working within their cultural forms to make the Father known. Every effective missionary has done the same: not bringing a generic Christ to a generic culture, but the Christ who is already Lord of that specific culture, disclosed in the specific forms that culture can receive and recognize.

His last known words, recorded by those present at his deathbed in Changsha, were in Chinese. He had become, in some deep sense, what he had set out to be: not an Englishman working in China, but a servant of the Chinese church, embedded so deeply that the boundary between the guest and the host had blurred almost entirely.

Then & Now

The Inland — Then and Now

Taylor's insistence on reaching the unreached interior rather than consolidating in comfortable coastal cities gave mission strategy a vocabulary it still uses: the "unreached people groups" framework, the focus on places with "no gospel witness," the willingness to go where existing mission work has not gone rather than reinforcing what already exists. The U.S. Center for World Mission, Joshua Project, and similar organizations that map and track unreached peoples are operating on strategic logic that Taylor helped establish.

The CIM that Taylor founded is now OMF International, working across East and Southeast Asia. The Chinese church it helped plant is, as we noted in Study 25, now one of the most missionary-minded in the world — sending its own workers into the hardest and most closed fields, including the "Back to Jerusalem" movement, which envisions Chinese missionaries carrying the gospel along the Silk Road back toward Jerusalem through the Muslim, Buddhist, and Hindu heartlands of Central Asia. The mission Taylor began has turned outward in ways he could not have imagined and would certainly have rejoiced to see.

A Quote to Carry
"Christ is either Lord of all, or he is not Lord at all."
— Hudson Taylor
Reflect

Taylor went to the interior because no one else would go there. Where in your own world — social, geographic, relational — is there an "inland" that the gospel has not yet reached, and what would it cost you to go there?

Prayer

Lord, thank you for Hudson Taylor — for the young man who dyed his hair and put on Chinese clothes and walked into the interior, and who kept walking for fifty years. Thank you for his insistence that Christ is Lord of all, not just of the places that are easy to reach. Give us his courage to go where the gospel hasn't yet been heard, his faith to trust you with provision and outcome, and his love for the people he served so deeply that the boundaries between foreigner and neighbor eventually dissolved. Raise up in our generation workers willing to go to the hard places, the overlooked places, the places where no one is watching. Amen.
