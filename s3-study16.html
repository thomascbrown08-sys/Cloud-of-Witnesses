The Cracks in the Cathedral

A Dark Chapter: Pre-Reformation Corruption and the Voices That Would Not Be Silenced

Dark ChapterJan HusSavonarolaReform
Opening

A Note Before We Begin

This study deals with a chapter of church history that is genuinely painful — a period of institutional corruption so severe that it provoked the most consequential rupture in Western Christianity since the Great Schism. We enter it as a family enters the room where a hard conversation must be had: not to condemn wholesale, not to pretend there was nothing good in the medieval church, but because honest history requires naming what happened.

The two figures at the center of this study — Jan Hus and Girolamo Savonarola — were not critics from outside the church. They loved the church and were destroyed by it. Understanding them, and the world they inhabited, is essential for understanding why Luther's hammer struck the door at Wittenberg like a match dropped in a room full of kindling. The kindling had been accumulating for a very long time.

What Happened

The State of the Late Medieval Church

The medieval church at its best was genuinely beautiful — the great cathedrals, the monastic traditions of scholarship and hospitality, the preservation of learning through the dark centuries, the care for the poor that the parish system at its best provided. The same institution that built Chartres also built hospitals across Europe. This matters: the corruption we are about to describe was not the whole story, and the grief the reformers felt was the grief of people who knew how good the church could be and saw how badly it had failed its own ideals.

At its worst, however, the late medieval church presented a picture that its own members found indefensible. The papacy had spent most of the 14th century in Avignon under effective French political control — a period known as the "Babylonian Captivity of the Church." Then came the Great Schism (1378–1417), during which two and sometimes three rival popes simultaneously excommunicated each other, each claiming to be the true Vicar of Christ. The spectacle of competing popes hurling anathemas at each other shattered the idea that papal authority was spiritually self-authenticating.

Simony — the buying and selling of church offices — was widespread. Bishops purchased their sees and expected a return on their investment. The sale of indulgences — certificates purporting to reduce time in purgatory — had become a significant revenue stream, sold by traveling agents who combined theological claims with carnival showmanship. Johann Tetzel, the indulgence-seller whose pitch directly provoked Luther, reportedly used the rhyme: "As soon as a coin in the coffer rings, a soul from purgatory springs." The phrase was memorable because it was, at the level of popular religion, essentially accurate about how the system worked.

Clerical celibacy was widely unenforced. Absentee bishops — who collected the revenues of sees they never visited — were common. Pope Alexander VI (Rodrigo Borgia, r. 1492–1503) used the papacy to enrich his children by multiple mistresses, including his son Cesare and his daughter Lucrezia, whose name became a byword for the corruption of the era. He was not unusual in kind, only in degree.

The Figures

Jan Hus and Girolamo Savonarola

Jan Hus (c. 1369–1415) was a Czech theologian and preacher at Charles University in Prague who read Wycliffe's writings and found in them a mirror for what he already believed: that Scripture was the supreme authority, that the church's wealth was a corruption of its calling, and that ordinary people had the right to receive the communion cup — a privilege the medieval church had reserved for the clergy alone. He was summoned to the Council of Constance in 1414 under a guarantee of safe conduct from the Holy Roman Emperor. The council revoked the safe conduct on the grounds that promises to heretics need not be kept. After a perfunctory trial, he was burned at the stake on July 6, 1415.

His last words were the 51st Psalm. He reportedly said: "In 100 years, God will raise up a man whose calls for reform cannot be suppressed." Martin Luther was born 33 years later. When Luther read about Hus, he wrote: "We are all Hussites without knowing it."

Girolamo Savonarola (1452–1498) was a Dominican friar in Florence whose prophetic preaching against Medici excess and papal corruption drew massive crowds and, for a time, genuine political power. His "Bonfires of the Vanities" — in which his followers burned mirrors, cosmetics, fine clothing, and secular books — represented a genuine moral seriousness, however theocratic and coercive the methods. Pope Alexander VI, the target of much of his preaching, eventually had him excommunicated, tortured, hanged, and burned in the same Florentine square where his bonfires had burned. Savonarola is a more complicated figure than Hus — his reform program had authoritarian elements, and his willingness to use political power to impose moral standards was a path the Reformation would later explicitly reject. But his cry against the corruption of the church was heard and remembered.

Christ in Focus

Jesus warned his disciples that those who occupy the seat of Moses can teach what is right while failing to live by it — and that his followers should therefore listen to what they teach but not imitate what they do (Matthew 23:2–3). He reserved his sharpest words not for the openly sinful but for those who used religious authority to burden people while exempting themselves, who "shut the kingdom of heaven in men's faces" (Matthew 23:13), who had turned the Father's house into a marketplace.

The pre-Reformation church was not populated exclusively by cynics. Many of the bishops who sold indulgences genuinely believed in the theological system that underwrote them. Many who opposed Bible translation genuinely believed they were protecting the flock. The corruption was real, but so was the sincere religious practice woven around it. What the Reformers — and Hus and Savonarola before them — were reacting to was not hypocrisy alone. It was the replacement of Christ himself at the center: the substitution of a system of religious transactions for the free grace of a crucified and risen Savior.

The Reformation was, in the deepest sense, a recovery of the announcement that Jesus made in Luke 4: "The Spirit of the Lord is upon me, because he has anointed me to proclaim good news to the poor." Not good news to those who could afford indulgences. Not salvation for those with access to the right clerical intermediaries. Good news. For the poor. Free.

Then & Now

The Pattern and the Warning

Every human institution — including the church — tends over time toward self-preservation, accumulation of power, and the protection of those who lead it at the expense of those it serves. This is not unique to Christianity; it is the consistent testimony of history about every institution of any kind. The specific danger for the church is that its theological vocabulary can be used to sanctify arrangements that are, at bottom, arrangements of human power. Indulgences were not invented by cynics. They were invented by theologians who had convinced themselves that the metaphysics worked.

The lesson Hus and Savonarola teach — both by what they said and by what destroyed them — is that reform within an institution is the most dangerous form of courage. They loved the church too much to leave it, and the church destroyed them for it. Luther, when his turn came, began by trying to reform from within and ended by founding something new. The Reformation was not, in its origins, a plan to split the church. It was a plea for the church to be what it claimed to be.

The institutional church today, in all its forms, remains capable of the same drift. The antidote is the same in every generation: Scripture in the hands of ordinary people, Christ at the center, and leaders accountable to the communities they serve rather than the other way around.

A Quote to Carry
"In 100 years, God will raise up a man whose calls for reform cannot be suppressed."
— Jan Hus, at the Council of Constance, 1415 AD (attributed)
Reflect

Hus and Savonarola loved the church enough to confront it at mortal cost. What does their example suggest about the difference between loyal critique and disloyalty — and what it might look like to hold that distinction faithfully today?

Prayer

Father, we grieve the ways your church has traded the gospel for power, the poor for the privileged, the free grace of Christ for a system of religious transactions. Thank you for the Hussites and the Lollards and every soul who held on to what was true when the institution that bore your name was working against it. Purify your church in our generation — beginning with us. Give us leaders who serve rather than accumulate, and communities courageous enough to hold them accountable to what Scripture actually says. Amen.
