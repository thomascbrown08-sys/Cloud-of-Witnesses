But What About Free Will?

Arminius, Wesley, and the Question That Has Divided Friends Without Dividing the Church

ArminiusWesleyElectionA Second-Order Question
Opening

The Question That Won't Stay Academic

Somewhere in every church, in every generation, someone asks the question. Sometimes it comes from honest curiosity: if God chooses who is saved, is the offer of the gospel genuinely open to everyone? Sometimes it comes from pastoral anguish: if my friend rejects Christ, is that God's sovereign decision or their own real choice? Sometimes it comes from the logic of prayer: why pray if God has already determined everything? And sometimes it comes from the gut: doesn't a God who predetermines some to damnation sound more like a tyrant than a Father?

The debate has a long history — Augustine vs. Pelagius was one version of it; Luther vs. Erasmus was another. In the late 16th century it crystallized around a Dutch theologian named Jacob Arminius, who raised careful objections to the way Reformed theology had come to describe predestination, and whose followers (the Remonstrants) laid out his positions in a document called the Remonstrance of 1610. The Reformed response came at the Synod of Dort (1618–19), which produced the doctrinal statement popularly summarized by the acronym TULIP. The debate between those two positions has continued without ceasing ever since.

This study does not settle that debate. It cannot, because the debate involves genuine tensions within Scripture that careful, prayerful, intelligent Christians have read differently for four centuries. What it can do is make sure you understand what each side is actually saying, what each side is protecting, and what both sides agree on — which turns out to be a great deal.

The Question, Fairly Stated

What Each Side Believes — and What Each Side Is Protecting

The Reformed (Calvinist) position holds that God, before creation, freely and unconditionally chose specific individuals for salvation — not on the basis of anything he foresaw in them, including their faith, but entirely according to his sovereign will and grace. Human beings in their fallen state are genuinely unable — not just unwilling — to come to God on their own. God therefore regenerates (gives new spiritual life to) those he has chosen before they respond in faith; the faith that follows is the result of regeneration, not its cause. The Spirit's call to the elect is effectual: it accomplishes what it intends. Those who are truly saved will persevere to the end, because God holds them, not because they hold God.

What this protects: The absolute sovereignty of God in salvation. The impossibility of any human merit or capacity contributing to being saved. The deepest possible assurance — if my salvation depends entirely on God's choice rather than my response, it is as secure as God is unchanging. The removal of all boasting: no one can stand before God and say they did their part. The free grace of the gospel: salvation is given, not earned.

The Arminian (Wesleyan) position holds that God genuinely desires all people to be saved (1 Timothy 2:4; 2 Peter 3:9), and that the gospel offer is therefore genuinely open and genuinely meant for everyone who hears it. God, through what Arminius and Wesley called prevenient grace — grace that comes before and enables — restores to all people enough capacity to respond to the gospel. Election, in this view, is God's foreknowledge of who will freely respond in faith; his choosing is conditioned on that foreseen faith. Human beings genuinely choose, and their choice is real. The Spirit's work can be resisted. Those who respond can, in principle, fall away, though this is treated differently in different Arminian traditions.

What this protects: The genuine universality of the gospel invitation — God really does mean it when he says "whoever will may come." The reality of human responsibility — our choices are not an illusion orchestrated by divine decree. The coherence of the call to repentance and faith — God would not genuinely command what he has, in certain people's cases, made impossible. The character of God as a Father who loves all his children and grieves over those who turn away.

What both sides agree on: Human beings are fallen and in need of grace. Salvation is in Christ alone. No one can save themselves. Faith is the means of receiving salvation. The Holy Spirit's work is essential. The gospel must be proclaimed to all. The Christian life involves both genuine divine sovereignty and genuine human responsibility, held together in a tension that neither side claims to fully resolve. Both sides worship the same Christ, receive the same Bible as authoritative, and expect to share the same heaven.

The debate is ultimately about how divine sovereignty and human freedom interact in the moment of conversion — something the New Testament clearly affirms both sides of without fully explaining the mechanism. Both John 6:37a ("All that the Father gives me will come to me") and John 6:37b ("and whoever comes to me I will never drive away") are in the same sentence, and both are spoken by Jesus. Christians with exactly the same commitment to Scripture's authority have read that tension differently for four centuries. They are unlikely to resolve it before Jesus returns.

The Figures

Arminius and Wesley

Jacob Arminius (1560–1609) was a Dutch Reformed theologian — not a radical outsider but a product of Calvinist training in Geneva — who came to believe that the standard Calvinist account of predestination made God the author of sin and rendered the gospel call disingenuous. He was a careful, churchly man whose published writings were moderate in tone. The more pointed Arminianism of the Remonstrance was developed by his followers after his death. He himself died before Dort, still within the Reformed church, still committed to Scripture and to grace.

John Wesley (1703–1791) brought the Arminian tradition into English-speaking evangelicalism with an energy and pastoral warmth that made it accessible to millions. His open-air preaching to coal miners and factory workers offered a gospel that was urgently, personally addressed to everyone present — "you can be saved, today, now" — which fit naturally with his conviction that the offer was genuinely universal. His lifelong friendship, and vigorous theological disagreement, with the Calvinist George Whitefield is one of the more poignant stories in 18th-century Christianity: two men who loved each other, disagreed sharply, and both built movements that changed the English-speaking world. Wesley reportedly said he hoped to be near Whitefield in heaven — just not close enough to hear him say "I told you so."

Christ in Focus

Whatever position you hold on the mechanics of election and free will, both sides converge on the same Christ and the same invitation. Jesus said: "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest" (Matthew 11:28). The "all" is not qualified. The invitation is not addressed only to the elect. It is addressed to everyone who is tired. Come.

He also said: "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him" (John 6:44). The drawing is the Father's work. The coming is ours. Both are true. The mechanism by which a sovereign God and a genuinely responsible human being both act in the same moment is something the New Testament affirms without fully systematizing — and perhaps that is intentional. We are not called to figure out the metaphysics before we respond to the invitation. We are called to come.

The pastoral word for both traditions is the same one Jesus gave to Nicodemus — the most careful, respectable, theologically educated man who came to him in the dark: "You must be born again" (John 3:7). Not: you must understand all the doctrines correctly. Not: you must first resolve the question of election. Born again — a new life, given by the Spirit, received by faith, in Christ. That is the center. The questions around it are real and worth engaging honestly. But they are not the center.

Then & Now

A Disagreement Worth Having Charitably

The Reformed and Arminian traditions have coexisted within evangelical Protestantism for four centuries. Baptist churches hold both positions. Anglican churches contain both. Many individual Christians hold a position somewhere between the poles, or hold the tensions without fully resolving them. The debate has been, at its best, a productive one — sharpening both sides' understanding of what Scripture actually says and what is actually at stake.

At its worst, it has generated a tribal bitterness that treats the other side as barely Christian. This is almost certainly not what either Arminius or Calvin intended, and it is certainly not what the New Testament models. Paul's instruction to the Romans — who had their own fierce internal disagreements — was to "welcome one another as Christ has welcomed you, for the glory of God" (Romans 15:7). The welcome comes before the resolution of every theological disagreement.

Season VI of this curriculum will return to the question of election and explore it more fully. For now, the most important things are these: both sides believe that salvation is by grace, that human beings need it desperately, that it is found in Christ alone, and that faith is the means of receiving it. On those things, Wesley and Calvin would agree. And on those things, the whole gospel stands.

A Quote to Carry
"All that the Father gives me will come to me, and whoever comes to me I will never drive away."
— Jesus, John 6:37 (both sides of the debate in a single sentence)
Reflect

Which aspect of this question do you find most pastorally important — the security that comes from God's sovereign choice, or the genuine openness of the gospel to everyone? What does your instinct here reveal about what you most need to hear from the gospel right now?

Prayer

Lord, thank you that you are bigger than our theologies about you. Thank you that both the sovereignty of your grace and the genuine reality of our response are true, even when we cannot fully reconcile them. Guard us from the arrogance of assuming we have resolved what four centuries of careful Christians have held in tension. Give us the humility to hold our convictions firmly and our fellow believers warmly. And in the middle of all our questions, keep us coming to the one place both sides agree: the Christ who says "whoever comes to me I will never drive away." Amen.
